17  Fiscal distress: the retainer burden

The paper’s central use of Hansei Ichiran is not to predict who got which 1884 peerage rank. It is to document the structural fiscal crisis that made the 1871 abolition of the feudal domains (廃藩置県はいはんちけん, 廃藩置県はいはんちけん (haihan chiken)) a mutually advantageous bargain in the first place.

The Political Coase Theorem argument requires that both parties to a bargain perceive the gains from cooperation to exceed the costs of conflict. For the new Meiji government, the gain was monopoly over taxation and the military. For the 大名だいみょう (daimyō) — the roughly 280 feudal lords — the gain had to be large enough to outweigh their reluctance to surrender three centuries of inherited sovereignty. Hansei Ichiran lets us see why that gain was, in fact, large: by 1870 most domains were insolvent.

ImportantWhat the paper claims, in one sentence

The retainer burden documented by Hansei Ichiran shows that the 大名だいみょう’s political property rights were no longer a valuable asset by 1870 but had become a massive financial liability — making them receptive to a bargain that would secure their personal wealth and status while shedding the fiscal albatross.

17.1 The metric: retainers per 1,000 koku

The headline statistic the paper uses is retainers per 1,000 koku — the number of hereditary 侍 (samurai) (侍) a domain owed stipends to, normalised by the domain’s revenue base (its 石高こくだか, 石高こくだか (kokudaka)). It functions as a standardised measure of structural insolvency: how heavily a domain’s fixed, hereditary payroll obligations weighed on its productive capacity.

A higher ratio means more 侍 mouths to feed for each unit of economic base — and because 侍 stipends were a hereditary right (掌禄, 秩禄 (chitsuroku)), not a discretionary expense the lord could trim, the ratio is closer to a fixed liability than a flexible cost. A domain at 100 retainers per 1,000 koku has roughly twice the per-koku stipend burden of one at 50.

The numerator is the count of 侍-class persons living off domain stipends (combining 士族しぞく — 士族しぞく (shizoku), the regular 侍 class — and 卒族そつぞく — 卒族そつぞく (sotsu), the foot-rank retainers). The denominator is the composite 石高こくだか from Hansei Ichiran, in thousands of koku.

The paper’s published values come from domain-specific historical sources; the dataset values are computed uniformly from Hansei Ichiran’s status-class returns. They agree exactly for 長州ちょうしゅう (where definitions clearly match), agree approximately for 熊本 and 加賀かが, and diverge for 薩摩さつま because the paper’s footnote 5 explicitly excludes 足軽 while the dataset’s 士族しぞく+卒族そつぞく sum includes them.

TipWhat the divergence shows

Even under the paper’s narrower “侍-only” definition, 薩摩さつま carried over 220 stipend-recipients per 1,000 koku. Under the broader status-class definition, the ratio is closer to 400. Either way, 薩摩さつま was structurally insolvent. The paper notes that this is precisely why 薩摩さつま had been forced into “unorthodox cash-based monopolies” (sugar, after the 1827 reforms) just to service its retainer payroll.

17.3 What the broader distribution shows

The four focal cases above are not extremes plucked from a long tail of much-leaner domains. They are representative. Across the 266 domains with computable values:

  • Median ratio: 54 retainers per 1,000 koku
  • Mean: 71
  • 80th percentile: 89
  • Standard deviation: 60

A median 大名だいみょう paying stipends to 54 people for every 1,000 koku of assessed revenue is paying stipends to ~5.4% of their formal koku base — and because the 侍 class also collected sub-stipends for their dependents (wives, children, retainers’ retainers), the practical per-domain cash flow leakage was higher still.

The pattern is striking. The top ten includes several of the southwestern domains that drove the Restoration (薩摩さつま, 佐土原 as 薩摩さつま’s cadet), the Northern Alliance core (米沢), and the geographically peripheral special-status domains (対馬府中つしまふちゅう with its Korea-diplomacy obligations, 館たて in Hokkaido). What unites them is that each had to support a very large 侍 class on a small or moderate revenue base.

挙母 at the top is an outlier of the most-distressed kind: a small 譜代ふだい domain (Naitō 内藤 family, Mikawa) with only 2,125 koku of 石高こくだか but 947 retainers — that is roughly one stipend obligation for every 2.2 koku of formal revenue.

長州ちょうしゅう’s position at this end of the distribution, despite its leadership role in the Restoration, is the revealing case. The paper makes the point explicitly via Craig (1961): 長州ちょうしゅう had already cut 侍 stipends by roughly 50 percent in the 1840s “just to stave off imminent financial collapse.” Its leanness in 1870 is post-reform, not natural; before the 1840s reforms its ratio would have been comparable to its 薩摩さつま counterpart in the Sat-Chō alliance. Every other major domain still had the reforms ahead of it — and the 1871 buyout offered them a way to do it without paying the political cost themselves.

The 外様とざま mean (~91 retainers per 1,000 koku) runs distinctly higher than the 譜代ふだい mean (~55). This is the structural pattern the paper relies on: the major 外様とざま domains, especially the southwestern coalition that drove the Restoration, were uniformly carrying heavier retainer burdens than the 幕府ばくふ (bakufu)-affiliated 譜代ふだい families. They were not the lean military operations of romantic Restoration narratives. They were heavily indebted feudal employers whose payroll obligations had become unsustainable.

17.5 What this implies for the bargain

By 1870, the typical 大名だいみょう was paying out hereditary stipends to ~5 percent of their formal koku base — a fixed obligation with no realistic prospect of relief through ordinary fiscal management. The 1871 廃藩置県はいはんちけん offered each lord:

  1. Debt relief. The state assumed the domain’s accumulated debts to merchants and rice-brokers.
  2. A personal stipend of one-tenth of the domain’s revenue, paid by the central government (later converted to interest-bearing 金禄公債 bonds in the 1876 秩禄処分, 秩禄処分 (秩禄 shobun)).
  3. kazoku peerage status, eventually formalised as the 1884 five-tier system.
  4. Access to government office. The largest domains received 参議 (sangi, Imperial Councilor) appointments; the rest received 元老院 (genrōin, Senate of Elders) membership and other formal positions.

Set against the alternative — continuing to service a hereditary stipend payroll worth a third of national revenue, with no monopoly cash crops, no tax-collection apparatus, and the new Imperial Army next door — the bargain was lopsided in favour of acceptance. Only one domain head (島津久光しまづひさみつ, the Satsuma 国父) seriously objected, and as the paper notes (Ravina 2003), his retainer 大久保利通おおくぼとしみち had already taken the matter out of his hands.

That receptiveness is what Hansei Ichiran documents. The 1870 survey was not commissioned to predict 1884 peerage assignments. It was commissioned because the new government needed to know — in precise, per-domain detail — exactly how unsustainable the existing system was, so that the buyout could be priced and the political bargain could be made.
