17  Fiscal distress: the retainer burden

The paper’s central use of Hansei Ichiran is not to predict who got which 1884 peerage rank. It is to document the structural fiscal crisis that made the 1871 abolition of the feudal domains (haihan chiken (廃藩置県)) a mutually advantageous bargain in the first place.

The Political Coase Theorem argument requires that both parties to a bargain perceive the gains from cooperation to exceed the costs of conflict. For the new Meiji government, the gain was monopoly over taxation and the military. For the daimyō (大名) — the roughly 280 feudal lords — the gain had to be large enough to outweigh their reluctance to surrender three centuries of inherited sovereignty. Hansei Ichiran lets us see why that gain was, in fact, large: by 1870 most domains were insolvent.

ImportantWhat the paper claims, in one sentence

The retainer burden documented by Hansei Ichiran shows that the daimyō’s political property rights were no longer a valuable asset by 1870 but had become a massive financial liability — making them receptive to a bargain that would secure their personal wealth and status while shedding the fiscal albatross.

17.1 The metric: retainers per 1,000 koku

The headline statistic the paper uses is retainers per 1,000 koku — the number of hereditary samurai (侍) a domain owed stipends to, normalised by the domain’s revenue base (its kokudaka (石高)). It functions as a standardised measure of structural insolvency: how heavily a domain’s fixed, hereditary payroll obligations weighed on its productive capacity.

A higher ratio means more samurai mouths to feed for each unit of economic base — and because samurai stipends were a hereditary right chitsuroku (秩禄), not a discretionary expense the lord could trim, the ratio is closer to a fixed liability than a flexible cost. A domain at 100 retainers per 1,000 koku has roughly twice the per-koku stipend burden of one at 50.

The numerator is the count of samurai-class persons living off domain stipends (combining shizoku (士族), the regular samurai class — and sotsu (卒族), the foot-rank retainers). The denominator is the composite kokudaka from Hansei Ichiran, in thousands of koku.

The paper’s published values come from domain-specific historical sources; the dataset values are computed uniformly from Hansei Ichiran’s status-class returns. They agree exactly for 長州 (where definitions clearly match), agree approximately for 熊本 and 加賀, and diverge for 薩摩 because the paper’s footnote 5 explicitly excludes ashigaru while the dataset’s shizoku+sotsu sum includes them.

TipWhat the divergence shows

Even under the paper’s narrower “samurai-only” definition, 薩摩 carried over 220 stipend-recipients per 1,000 koku. Under the broader status-class definition, the ratio is closer to 400. Either way, 薩摩 was structurally insolvent. The paper notes that this is precisely why 薩摩 had been forced into “unorthodox cash-based monopolies” (sugar, after the 1827 reforms) just to service its retainer payroll.

17.3 What the broader distribution shows

The four focal cases above are not extremes plucked from a long tail of much-leaner domains. They are representative. Across the 266 domains with computable values:

  • Median ratio: 54 retainers per 1,000 koku
  • Mean: 69
  • 80th percentile: 87
  • Standard deviation: 58

A median daimyō paying stipends to 54 people for every 1,000 koku of assessed revenue is paying stipends to ~5.4% of their formal koku base — and because the samurai class also collected sub-stipends for their dependents (wives, children, retainers’ retainers), the practical per-domain cash flow leakage was higher still.

The pattern is striking. The top ten includes several of the southwestern domains that drove the Restoration (薩摩, 佐土原 as 薩摩’s cadet, 飫肥 as a southwestern tozama (外様)), the Northern Alliance core (米沢), and the geographically peripheral special-status domains (館 in Hokkaido). What unites them is that each had to support a very large samurai class on a small or moderate revenue base.

挙母 at the top is an outlier of the most-distressed kind: a small fudai domain (Naitō 内藤 family, Mikawa) with only 2,125 koku of kokudaka but 947 retainers — that is roughly one stipend obligation for every 2.2 koku of formal revenue.

NoteA note on the 対馬府中藩 (Tsushima) figure

Tsushima’s retainer burden here — 199.3 per 1,000 koku at kokudaka 75,592 (rank 12) — is derived from the 1872 Kyūdaka kyūryō torishirabechō (旧高旧領取調帳), the Meiji government’s per-village cadastral survey, hosted as a free open-access database by the National Museum of Japanese History (国立歴史民俗博物館 / Rekihaku). The Rekihaku value is kusadaka (草高) tier — actual rated land yield — which is the conceptually right metric for the paper’s revenue-base IV (see Chapter 4).

Either way, Tsushima reads as structurally distressed: its retainer burden is one of the dozen highest in the dataset.

長州’s position at this end of the distribution, despite its leadership role in the Restoration, is the revealing case. The paper makes the point explicitly via Craig (1961): 長州 had already cut samurai stipends by roughly 50 percent in the 1840s “just to stave off imminent financial collapse.” Its leanness in 1870 is post-reform, not natural; before the 1840s reforms its ratio would have been comparable to its 薩摩 counterpart in the Sat-Chō alliance. Every other major domain still had the reforms ahead of it — and the 1871 buyout offered them a way to do it without paying the political cost themselves.

The tozama mean (~89 retainers per 1,000 koku) runs distinctly higher than the fudai mean (~53). This is the structural pattern the paper relies on: the major tozama domains, especially the southwestern coalition that drove the Restoration, were uniformly carrying heavier retainer burdens than the bakufu (幕府)-affiliated fudai families. They were not the lean military operations of romantic Restoration narratives. They were heavily indebted feudal employers whose payroll obligations had become unsustainable.

17.5 What this implies for the bargain

By 1870, the typical daimyō was paying out hereditary stipends to ~5 percent of their formal koku base — a fixed obligation with no realistic prospect of relief through ordinary fiscal management. The 1871 haihan chiken offered each lord:

  1. Debt relief. The state assumed the domain’s accumulated debts to merchants and rice-brokers.
  2. A personal stipend of one-tenth of the domain’s revenue, paid by the central government (later converted to interest-bearing kinroku kōsai (金禄公債) bonds in the 1876 chitsuroku shobun (秩禄処分) — terms detailed in the project’s transcription of 太政官布告 第108号: regressive multipliers × progressive interest, 5-year grace + 30-year lottery amortization; Sat-Chō-Saga-Tosa former daimyō all sit in the least-generous conversion tier).
  3. kazoku peerage status, eventually formalised as the 1884 five-tier system (see the project transcription of Kanpō #307).
  4. Access to government office. The largest domains received sangi (参議, Imperial Councilor) appointments; the rest received genrōin (元老院, Senate of Elders) membership and other formal positions.

Set against the alternative — continuing to service a hereditary stipend payroll worth a third of national revenue, with no monopoly cash crops, no tax-collection apparatus, and the new Imperial Army next door — the bargain was lopsided in favour of acceptance. Only one domain head (島津久光, the Satsuma 国父) seriously objected, and as the paper notes (Ravina 2003), his retainer 大久保利通 had already taken the matter out of his hands.

That receptiveness is what Hansei Ichiran documents. The 1870 survey was not commissioned to predict 1884 peerage assignments. It was commissioned because the new government needed to know — in precise, per-domain detail — exactly how unsustainable the existing system was, so that the buyout could be priced and the political bargain could be made.
