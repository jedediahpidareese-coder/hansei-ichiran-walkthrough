15  shinpan / fudai / tozama — the Tokugawa political classification

The previous chapters of this walkthrough have used the Tokugawa-era category (shinpan / fudai / tozama / sankyō / tenryō (天領)) as a control variable without formally introducing it. This chapter fills that gap: where the classification comes from, what each category means in practice, how the 286 domains in the dataset distribute across the categories, and how the categories interact with the 1884 kazoku (華族) rank outcomes.

ImportantWhat the categories are, in one sentence

The Tokugawa bakufu (幕府, the shōgunate) classified daimyō (大名) houses by their relationship to Tokugawa Ieyasu at the moment of Sekigahara (1600) — Tokugawa cadets (shinpan), long-standing vassals (fudai), post-Sekigahara submitters (tozama) — and that classification largely fixed each family’s political role for the next 250 years. The classification was not just historical record; it determined which houses could supply senior bakufu officials (only fudai), which could be reduced or relocated more freely (mostly tozama), and which were treated as quasi-family (shinpan).

15.1 The four categories — definitions and origins

15.1.1 shinpan (親藩) — Tokugawa cadets

Houses founded by sons or close male relatives of Tokugawa Ieyasu and continued as separate domains. The category was deliberately constructed by Ieyasu and his successors to ensure that, if the main Tokugawa line failed to produce an heir, an unambiguous chain of succession existed within the Tokugawa family itself.

  • The three flagship shinpan houses are the gosanke (御三家) (Owari-Tokugawa, Kii-Tokugawa, Mito-Tokugawa), established by three of Ieyasu’s sons. Each got a major domain (Nagoya, Wakayama, Mito).
  • The next tier is the 越前松平 lineage (Echizen-Matsudaira), descended from Ieyasu’s second son 結城秀康 — Fukui is the senior line. The Echizen-Matsudaira branches multiplied through the Tokugawa period and supplied many smaller shinpan.
  • A separate branch is the 久松松平 lineage (Hisamatsu-Matsudaira), descended from Ieyasu’s half-brother through his mother’s remarriage. Major Hisamatsu-Matsudaira domains include Iyo-Matsuyama, Imabari, and Kuwana (the latter reclassified to shinpan; see the methodology callout below).
  • The sankyō (Hitotsubashi, Tayasu, Shimizu) — three later 18th-century lines from sons of Yoshimune and Ieshige — are a separate sub-category (see chapter 02h).

15.1.2 fudai (譜代) — long-standing vassals

Houses whose ancestors served the Tokugawa family before Sekigahara (1600) as vassals — many of them from the Mikawa heartland where the Matsudaira/Tokugawa originated. The fudai were the political backbone of the bakufu administration: only fudai could hold the senior offices of 老中 (rōjū) and 若年寄 (wakadoshiyori), making them the operating elite.

In compensation for their political reliability, fudai were typically given smaller domains — Tokugawa policy explicitly limited them to moderate territories on the assumption that their administrative power, not their land base, was their real reward. Hence the strikingly skewed kokudaka (石高) distribution: most fudai are concentrated at 10,000–50,000 koku, with only a few crossing 100,000.

Within fudai, key sub-tiers include the 大老 (tairō) houses (Ii of Hikone, Sakai of Shōnai, etc., who supplied the highest bakufu post on rotation), standard fudai (Sakai, Honda, Mizuno, Itakura, Toda, Mōri-of-Saiki, etc.), and late-elevation fudai (旗本 promoted to daimyō status in the 18th-19th centuries).

15.1.3 tozama (外様) — outside vassals

Houses who only submitted to the Tokugawa at or after Sekigahara (1600). The category includes most of the major regional powers of Sengoku Japan — Shimazu in Satsuma, Mōri in Chōshū, Date in Sendai, Maeda in Kaga, Yamauchi in Tosa, Kuroda in Fukuoka, Hosokawa in Kumamoto, Nabeshima in Saga.

External-vassal status carried a double-edged meaning: tozama were typically allowed to keep large territories (because their submission was the price of peace, and reducing them was politically expensive), but they were structurally excluded from the inner bakufu administration. The standard sankin kōtai treatment was harsher for tozama, the bakufu maintained closer surveillance of them, and any sign of disloyalty would be met more aggressively (the most spectacular Edo-era reductions were of tozama houses).

The bakufu’s strategic geography placed tozama mostly in outer Japan (Kyushu, Shikoku, the Japan Sea coast, the north) — far from the political centre. The closer tozama (e.g., the eastern Date branches, Ueda) were the exception.

15.1.4 sankyō (御三卿) — hatamoto-class Tokugawa relatives

A small mid-18th-century innovation: three branch houses (Hitotsubashi, Tayasu, Shimizu) of the shogunal family, with no domain but with stipends and ceremonial status. Their political function was to serve as an heir pool for the shōgun (将軍). By 1870 only Hitotsubashi and Tayasu were still extant (Shimizu had become extinct and was only revived in 1895), and these two are the sankyō rows in the dataset. See the Sankyō walkthrough for the full discussion.

15.1.5 tenryō — bakufu direct holdings

Lands held directly by the bakufu, administered by appointed daikan (代官) rather than by an inheritable daimyō family. By 1870 most former tenryō had been reorganised into 府 / 県 administrative units; the dataset contains one residual tenryō row for completeness.

15.2 How the 286 distribute across categories

Category Count Share
fudai 137 47.9%
tozama 117 40.9%
shinpan 29 10.1%
sankyō 2 0.7%
tenryō 1 0.3%
Total 286 100%

The fudai-heavy distribution is structural: the bakufu created many small fudai houses (most of them at 10,000–30,000 koku) to staff its administration, while the tozama are fewer but on-average wealthier. The shinpan cluster at the top — large Tokugawa-cadet domains in the major centres — with only 29 houses total.

Three patterns visible here:

  1. fudai are by far the smallest — mean ~43,000 koku, max only 250,000. This is the structural consequence of the “small territories for the politically-trusted” Tokugawa policy.
  2. shinpan have the highest mean (~151,000) because the gosanke + Echizen branches anchor the top of the distribution.
  3. tozama are skewed — mean is similar to shinpan (~115,000), but with the largest tail (加賀 at 1.35M, 長州 at 988k, 薩摩 at 870k). Their median (~30,000) is actually similar to fudai.

15.4 Genmai (genmai) distribution — the kazoku-rule numerator

The 1884 kazoku-rei threshold rule operates on genmai (post-1869 tax-reported rice flow), not kokudaka. The genmai (現米) distribution shows the same shape as kokudaka but compressed:

Category n Mean Median Min Max
shinpan 25 66,688 28,195 1,658 327,468
tozama 107 48,809 13,140 1,618 637,279
fudai 130 16,919 9,862 1,907 94,030

The median fudai genmai is ~10,000 koku — well below the 50,000 marquess threshold and the 50,000 count threshold. This is why fudai cluster overwhelmingly at viscount in 1884 (see the cross-tab below). The genmai compression also explains many of the “Boshin punishments” that turn out to be mechanical (Sendai’s pre-Boshin kokudaka of 620k put it in marquess territory; its post-Boshin genmai of 66k put it squarely in count — without any discretionary penalty).

15.5 Category × 1884 kazoku rank

How did the families ruling each domain category fare in the 1884 ennoblement? The cross-tab tells most of the story:

Category Prince Marquess Count Viscount Baron n
shinpan 1 4 4 20 0 29
fudai 0 0 13 118 5 136 (+1 NaN)
tozama 2 11 16 84 4 117
sankyō 0 0 2 0 0 2
All 3 15 35 222 9 284

The patterns:

  • No shinpan became baron — the lowest-ranked shinpan are all viscount. The 1884 rule for cadet-Tokugawa lines effectively floored them at viscount, even for very small territories.
  • No fudai received marquess or prince — the highest fudai rank is count, even for 250,000-koku Himeji. The combination of structurally-small fudai territories + the kokudaka/genmai threshold made marquess very hard for a fudai house to reach.
  • All three princes are anti-bakufu winners — Shimazu (Satsuma), Mōri (Chōshū), and Tokugawa-main (Shizuoka). The first two are tozama (Restoration leaders); Shizuoka is shinpan (Tokugawa main-line exception).
  • The 11 tozama marquesses include the major Restoration powers — Kaga, Kumamoto, Saga, Fukuoka, Tosa, Hiroshima, Okayama, Tokushima, Date-Uwajima, and the gosanke houses’ tozama equivalent (none in this list, but Mito’s promotion is a separately-clause case).
  • The 2 sankyō are both count by the explicit sankyō clause.

15.6 Category × Boshin alignment

A perhaps-surprising result: fudai houses were more often anti-bakufu than pro-bakufu in the data. The cross-tab:

Category Anti-bakufu Neutral Pro-bakufu n
shinpan 21 2 6 29
fudai 112 3 22 137
tozama 102 4 11 117
sankyō 0 0 2 2
tenryō 0 1 0 1

The 112 anti-bakufu fudai look counterintuitive — fudai were the bakufu’s political backbone, so we might expect them to defend the bakufu in the Boshin War (Boshin sensō). The puzzle resolves on close inspection:

  • Most “anti-bakufu” fudai are coded as such by the “post-Toba-Fushimi imperial consolidation” rule (Beasley 1972, ch. XI). After the bakufu’s military defeat at Toba-Fushimi (January 1868), the imperial side issued a general directive to all daimyō to switch sides; most small fudai complied without resistance. This is end-state alignment, not active anti-bakufu combat.
  • The actively-anti-bakufu fudai are a much smaller subset — Hikone (彦根 Ii, defected pre-Toba-Fushimi), Yodo (淀, castle-gate refusal at Toba-Fushimi 1868-01-28), and a few others.
  • The 22 pro-bakufu fudai are the actually-loyal core — Sendai-affiliated, Aizu-affiliated, members of the Northern Alliance who fought on the pro-bakufu side, etc.

The shinpan split (21 anti / 6 pro) is similarly explained — most shinpan either defected or simply complied with the imperial directive; the 6 pro-bakufu shinpan are the actively-loyal Tokugawa-cadet branches (Aizu/斗南, Kuwana, Iyo-Matsuyama, Shimousa-Tsuruta, Mito itself by family-affiliation, Shizuoka).

The tozama split (102 anti / 11 pro) is the cleanest: most tozama were on the Restoration side, and the 11 pro-bakufu tozama are the surprise cases — typically smaller eastern tozama or tozama whose specific lord supported the bakufu against the regional pattern.

15.7 Why fudai cluster at viscount

The rank cluster pattern — 118 of 137 fudai (~86%) at viscount, 0 above count — is mostly mechanical:

  1. fudai median genmai is ~10,000 koku (well below the 50,000-koku count threshold).
  2. The 1884 rule placed everything below 50,000 at viscount by default.
  3. The 13 count-rank fudai are exactly the fudai whose genmai cleared 50,000 — Hikone, Himeji, Kōriyama, Toyotsu, Mito’s surrounding loyalists, etc.

The 5 baron-rank fudai are the post-1868 fudai elevations: 今尾 (Owari 付家老), 新宮/田辺 (Kii 付家老), 松岡 (Mito 付家老), and 田原本 (旗本 elevation). See chapter 07 §D for full per-family narratives.

15.8 Four mis-classifications corrected during sourcing review

TipMethodology note

The dataset’s domain_category column was initially seeded from a web-scraped Bakumatsu-era domain index. Every row was subsequently re-cited against published academic sources (児玉幸多 ed.『藩史総覧 (Hanshi sōran, “Comprehensive Survey of Han History”)』新人物往来社 1977; Beasley 1972; Japanese Wikipedia shinpan / 譜代大名 / 外様大名 / sankyō articles used as a finder only). The re-sourcing surfaced four mis-classifications, now corrected:

Domain Before After Reason
仙台藩 shinpan tozama The Date family is the canonical 国主-class tozama — the initial web-scraped coding as shinpan was wrong
伊予吉田藩 shinpan tozama Date-Uwajima cadet branch — same tozama provenance as Sendai/Uwajima
対馬府中藩 shinpan tozama Sō family has kokushukaku (country-lord status) but is tozama, not shinpan
桑名藩 fudai shinpan Hisamatsu-Matsudaira convention — consistent with 伊予松山 + 今治, which were already shinpan

The first three corrections came from cross-checking against Beasley (1972) p. xii table of principal domains in 1860 (which explicitly classifies Date and Sō as tozama). The Kuwana correction came from the Hisamatsu-Matsudaira lineage tracing — since 伊予松山 and 今治 (both Hisamatsu-Matsudaira) were already coded shinpan, consistency requires Kuwana (also Hisamatsu-Matsudaira) be shinpan as well.

These mis-classifications matter for the paper because they appear in cross-tabs where category is a regressor. The final distribution in this chapter — shinpan 29 / fudai 137 / tozama 117 / sankyō 2 / tenryō 1 — reflects the corrected values.

15.9 What this teaches about the paper’s empirical claim

Three lessons from the cross-tabs:

  1. Category and rank are mechanically linked through the genmai threshold — fudai cluster at viscount because their genmai is small. This is a structural link, not a discretionary one.
  2. Category and Boshin alignment are weakly linked in the data because most domains followed the post-Toba-Fushimi imperial directive. The active-combatant subset is small in every category.
  3. The interaction of category × Boshin × rank is most informative in the deviation sample (chapter 07) — the 19 families whose 1884 rank diverged from genmai-mechanical prediction split by category as: 3 shinpan deviations (Shizuoka, Mito, Fukui), 5 tozama upgrades + 2 tozama downgrades, and 5 fudai downgrades. The category effect is real but operates through specific rule provisions (the sanke clause, the Tokugawa-main clause, the post-1868-elevation provision), not through general “shinpan are favoured” or “tozama are punished” patterns.

Citation chain for the Tokugawa-era category column:

Primary scholarly reference: 児玉幸多 ed.『藩史総覧』新人物往来社 (1977) — canonical han-classification compendium covering all ~300 domains with formal category designations.

Definitive secondary: Kokushi daijiten (Yoshikawa Kōbunkan, 1979–1997) — the standard reference work for individual house histories.

English source for major domains: Beasley, W.G. The Meiji Restoration. Stanford University Press (1972), p. xii — “Principal Domains in 1860” table with explicit shinpan/fudai/tozama designations.

Cross-validation finder: JP Wikipedia shinpan / 譜代大名 / 外様大名 / sankyō articles, used as a quick lookup for the rare disputed cases.

Hisamatsu-Matsudaira convention for the Kuwana reclassification: 中川 学『松平氏由緒書 (Matsudaira-shi yuishogaki, “Matsudaira Lineage Records”)』(Mikawa Edo bunko 1989); JP Wikipedia 久松松平家 article.

The full per-row citation lives in the domain_category_source column of the dataset, and the audit of the four corrections accompanies the dataset release.
