15  親藩しんぱん / 譜代ふだい / 外様とざま — the Tokugawa political classification

The previous chapters of this walkthrough have used the Tokugawa-era category (親藩しんぱん / 譜代ふだい / 外様とざま / 御三卿ごさんきょう / 天領てんりょう) as a control variable without formally introducing it. This chapter fills that gap: where the classification comes from, what each category means in practice, how the 287 domains in the dataset distribute across the categories, and how the categories interact with the 1884 華族かぞく (kazoku) rank outcomes.

ImportantWhat the categories are, in one sentence

The Tokugawa 幕府ばくふ (bakufu / shōgunate) classified 大名だいみょう (daimyō) houses by their relationship to Tokugawa Ieyasu at the moment of Sekigahara (1600) — Tokugawa cadets (親藩しんぱん), long-standing vassals (譜代ふだい), post-Sekigahara submitters (外様とざま) — and that classification largely fixed each family’s political role for the next 250 years. The classification was not just historical record; it determined which houses could supply senior 幕府ばくふ (bakufu) officials (only 譜代ふだい), which could be reduced or relocated more freely (mostly 外様とざま), and which were treated as quasi-family (親藩しんぱん).

15.1 The four categories — definitions and origins

15.1.1 親藩しんぱん (shinpan) — Tokugawa cadets

Houses founded by sons or close male relatives of Tokugawa Ieyasu and continued as separate domains. The category was deliberately constructed by Ieyasu and his successors to ensure that, if the main Tokugawa line failed to produce an heir, an unambiguous chain of succession existed within the Tokugawa family itself.

  • The three flagship 親藩しんぱん houses are the 御三家ごさんけ (Owari-Tokugawa, Kii-Tokugawa, Mito-Tokugawa), established by three of Ieyasu’s sons. Each got a major domain (Nagoya, Wakayama, Mito).
  • The next tier is the 越前えちぜん松平まつだいら lineage (Echizen-Matsudaira), descended from Ieyasu’s second son 結城秀康ゆうきひでやす — Fukui is the senior line. The Echizen-Matsudaira branches multiplied through the Tokugawa period and supplied many smaller 親藩しんぱん.
  • A separate branch is the 久松ひさまつ松平まつだいら lineage (Hisamatsu-Matsudaira), descended from Ieyasu’s half-brother through his mother’s remarriage. Major Hisamatsu-Matsudaira domains include Iyo-Matsuyama, Imabari, and Kuwana (the latter reclassified to 親藩しんぱん in 2026-05-20; see methodology callout below).
  • The 御三卿ごさんきょう (Hitotsubashi, Tayasu, Shimizu) — three later 18th-century lines from sons of Yoshimune and Ieshige — are a separate sub-category (see chapter 02h).

15.1.2 譜代ふだい (fudai) — long-standing vassals

Houses whose ancestors served the Tokugawa family before Sekigahara (1600) as vassals — many of them from the Mikawa heartland where the Matsudaira/Tokugawa originated. The 譜代ふだい were the political backbone of the 幕府ばくふ administration: only 譜代ふだい could hold the senior offices of 老中ろうじゅう (rōjū) and 若年寄わかどしより (wakadoshiyori), making them the operating elite.

In compensation for their political reliability, 譜代ふだい were typically given smaller domains — Tokugawa policy explicitly limited them to moderate territories on the assumption that their administrative power, not their land base, was their real reward. Hence the strikingly skewed 石高こくだか (kokudaka) distribution: most 譜代ふだい are concentrated at 10,000–50,000 koku, with only a few crossing 100,000.

Within 譜代ふだい, key sub-tiers include the 大老たいろう (tairō) houses (Ii of Hikone, Sakai of Shōnai, etc., who supplied the highest 幕府ばくふ post on rotation), standard 譜代ふだい (Sakai, Honda, Mizuno, Itakura, Toda, Mōri-of-Saiki, etc.), and late-elevation 譜代ふだい (旗本はたもと promoted to 大名だいみょう status in the 18th-19th centuries).

15.1.3 外様とざま (tozama) — outside vassals

Houses who only submitted to the Tokugawa at or after Sekigahara (1600). The category includes most of the major regional powers of Sengoku Japan — Shimazu in Satsuma, Mōri in Chōshū, Date in Sendai, Maeda in Kaga, Yamauchi in Tosa, Kuroda in Fukuoka, Hosokawa in Kumamoto, Nabeshima in Saga.

External-vassal status carried a double-edged meaning: 外様とざま were typically allowed to keep large territories (because their submission was the price of peace, and reducing them was politically expensive), but they were structurally excluded from the inner 幕府ばくふ administration. The standard sankin kōtai treatment was harsher for 外様とざま, the 幕府ばくふ maintained closer surveillance of them, and any sign of disloyalty would be met more aggressively (the most spectacular Edo-era reductions were of 外様とざま houses).

The 幕府ばくふ’s strategic geography placed 外様とざま mostly in outer Japan (Kyushu, Shikoku, the Japan Sea coast, the north) — far from the political centre. The closer 外様とざま (e.g., the eastern Date branches, Ueda) were the exception.

15.1.4 御三卿ごさんきょう (sankyō) — hatamoto-class Tokugawa relatives

A small mid-18th-century innovation: three branch houses (Hitotsubashi, Tayasu, Shimizu) of the shogunal family, with no domain but with stipends and ceremonial status. Their political function was to serve as an heir pool for the 将軍しょうぐん (shōgun). By 1870 only Hitotsubashi and Tayasu were still extant (Shimizu had become extinct and was only revived in 1895), and these two are the 御三卿ごさんきょう rows in the dataset. See the Sankyō walkthrough for the full discussion.

15.1.5 天領てんりょう (tenryō) — 幕府ばくふ direct holdings

Lands held directly by the 幕府ばくふ, administered by appointed 代官だいかん rather than by an inheritable 大名だいみょう family. By 1870 most former 天領てんりょう had been reorganised into 府 / 県 administrative units; the dataset contains one residual 天領てんりょう row for completeness.

The 譜代ふだい-heavy distribution is structural: the 幕府ばくふ created many small 譜代ふだい houses (most of them at 10,000–30,000 koku) to staff its administration, while the 外様とざま are fewer but on-average wealthier. The 親藩しんぱん cluster at the top — large Tokugawa-cadet domains in the major centres — with only 29 houses total.

Three patterns visible here:

  1. 譜代ふだい are by far the smallest — mean ~43,000 koku, max only 250,000. This is the structural consequence of the “small territories for the politically-trusted” Tokugawa policy.
  2. 親藩しんぱん have the highest mean (~151,000) because the sanke + Echizen branches anchor the top of the distribution.
  3. 外様とざま are skewed — mean is similar to 親藩しんぱん (~115,000), but with the largest tail (加賀かが at 1.35M, 長州ちょうしゅう at 988k, 薩摩さつま at 870k). Their median (~30,000) is actually similar to 譜代ふだい.

15.4 Genmai (現米げんまい) distribution — the 華族かぞく-rule numerator

The 1884 華族かぞく-rei threshold rule operates on 現米げんまい (post-1869 tax-reported rice flow), not 石高こくだか. The 現米げんまい (genmai) distribution shows the same shape as 石高こくだか but compressed:

Category n Mean Median Min Max
親藩しんぱん 25 66,688 28,195 1,658 327,468
外様とざま 107 48,809 13,140 1,618 637,279
譜代ふだい 130 16,919 9,862 1,907 94,030

The median 譜代ふだい 現米げんまい is ~10,000 koku — well below the 50,000 marquess threshold and the 50,000 count threshold. This is why 譜代ふだい cluster overwhelmingly at viscount in 1884 (see the cross-tab below). The 現米げんまい compression also explains many of the “Boshin punishments” that turn out to be mechanical (Sendai’s pre-Boshin 石高こくだか of 620k put it in marquess territory; its post-Boshin 現米げんまい of 66k put it squarely in count — without any discretionary penalty).

15.5 Category × 1884 華族かぞく rank

How did the families ruling each domain category fare in the 1884 ennoblement? The cross-tab tells most of the story:

譜代ふだい 0 0 13 118 5 136 (+2 NaN)
外様とざま 2 11 16 84 4 117
御三卿ごさんきょう 0 0 2 0 0 2

The patterns:

  • No 親藩しんぱん became baron — the lowest-ranked 親藩しんぱん are all viscount. The 1884 rule for cadet-Tokugawa lines effectively floored them at viscount, even for very small territories.
  • No 譜代ふだい received marquess or prince — the highest 譜代ふだい rank is count, even for 250,000-koku Himeji. The combination of structurally-small 譜代ふだい territories + the 石高こくだか/現米げんまい threshold made marquess very hard for a 譜代ふだい house to reach.
  • All three princes are anti-幕府ばくふ winners — Shimazu (Satsuma), Mōri (Chōshū), and Tokugawa-main (Shizuoka). The first two are 外様とざま (Restoration leaders); Shizuoka is 親藩しんぱん (Tokugawa main-line exception).
  • The 11 外様とざま marquesses include the major Restoration powers — Kaga, Kumamoto, Saga, Fukuoka, Tosa, Hiroshima, Okayama, Tokushima, Date-Uwajima, and the sanke houses’ 外様とざま equivalent (none in this list, but Mito’s promotion is a separately-clause case).
  • The 2 御三卿ごさんきょう are both count by the explicit 御三卿ごさんきょう clause.

15.6 Category × Boshin alignment

A perhaps-surprising result: 譜代ふだい houses were more often anti-幕府ばくふ than pro-幕府ばくふ in the data. The cross-tab:

Category Anti-幕府ばくふ Neutral Pro-幕府ばくふ n
親藩しんぱん 21 2 6 29
譜代ふだい 113 3 22 138
外様とざま 102 4 11 117
御三卿ごさんきょう 0 0 2 2
天領てんりょう 0 1 0 1

The 113 anti-幕府ばくふ 譜代ふだい look counterintuitive — 譜代ふだい were the 幕府ばくふ’s political backbone, so we might expect them to defend the 幕府ばくふ in the 戊辰戦争ぼしんせんそう (Boshin sensō). The puzzle resolves on close inspection:

  • Most “anti-幕府ばくふ” 譜代ふだい are coded as such by the “post-Toba-Fushimi imperial consolidation” rule (Beasley 1972, ch. XI). After the 幕府ばくふ’s military defeat at Toba-Fushimi (January 1868), the imperial side issued a general directive to all 大名だいみょう to switch sides; most small 譜代ふだい complied without resistance. This is end-state alignment, not active anti-幕府ばくふ combat.
  • The actively-anti-幕府ばくふ 譜代ふだい are a much smaller subset — Hikone (彦根ひこね Ii, defected pre-Toba-Fushimi), Yodo (淀, castle-gate refusal at Toba-Fushimi 1868-01-28), and a few others.
  • The 22 pro-幕府ばくふ 譜代ふだい are the actually-loyal core — Sendai-affiliated, Aizu-affiliated, members of the Northern Alliance who fought on the pro-幕府ばくふ side, etc.

The 親藩しんぱん split (21 anti / 6 pro) is similarly explained — most 親藩しんぱん either defected or simply complied with the imperial directive; the 6 pro-幕府ばくふ 親藩しんぱん are the actively-loyal Tokugawa-cadet branches (Aizu/斗南となみ, Kuwana, Iyo-Matsuyama, Shimousa-Tsuruta, Mito itself by family-affiliation, Shizuoka).

The 外様とざま split (102 anti / 11 pro) is the cleanest: most 外様とざま were on the Restoration side, and the 11 pro-幕府ばくふ 外様とざま are the surprise cases — typically smaller eastern 外様とざま or 外様とざま whose specific lord supported the 幕府ばくふ against the regional pattern.

15.7 Why 譜代ふだい cluster at viscount

The rank cluster pattern — 118 of 138 譜代ふだい (~85%) at viscount, 0 above count — is mostly mechanical:

  1. 譜代ふだい median 現米げんまい is ~10,000 koku (well below the 50,000-koku count threshold).
  2. The 1884 rule placed everything below 50,000 at viscount by default.
  3. The 13 count-rank 譜代ふだい are exactly the 譜代ふだい whose 現米げんまい cleared 50,000 — Hikone, Himeji, Kōriyama, Toyotsu, Mito’s surrounding loyalists, etc.

The 5 baron-rank 譜代ふだい are the post-1868 譜代ふだい elevations: 今尾いまお (Owari 付家老つけがろう), 新宮しんぐう/田辺たなべ (Kii 付家老つけがろう), 松岡まつおか (Mito 付家老つけがろう), and 田原本たわらもと (旗本はたもと elevation). See chapter 07 §D for full per-family narratives.

15.8 Four mis-classifications corrected during sourcing review

TipMethodology note

The dataset’s domain_category column was initially seeded from a web-scraped Bakumatsu-era domain index. Every row was subsequently re-cited against published academic sources (児玉幸多こだまこうた ed.『藩史総覧はんしそうらん』新人物往来社しんじんぶつおうらいしゃ 1977; Beasley 1972; Japanese Wikipedia 親藩しんぱん / 譜代大名ふだいだいみょう / 外様大名とざまだいみょう / 御三卿ごさんきょう articles used as a finder only). The re-sourcing surfaced four mis-classifications, now corrected:

Domain Before After Reason
仙台藩せんだいはん 親藩しんぱん 外様とざま The Date family is the canonical 国主こくしゅ-class 外様とざま — the bakumatsu.org coding as 親藩しんぱん was wrong
伊予吉田藩いよよしだはん 親藩しんぱん 外様とざま Date-Uwajima cadet branch — same 外様とざま provenance as Sendai/Uwajima
対馬府中藩つしまふちゅうはん 親藩しんぱん 外様とざま Sō family has 国主格こくしゅかく (country-lord status) but is 外様とざま, not 親藩しんぱん
桑名藩くわなはん 譜代ふだい 親藩しんぱん Hisamatsu-Matsudaira convention — consistent with 伊予松山いよまつやま + 今治いまばり, which were already 親藩しんぱん

The first three corrections came from cross-checking against Beasley (1972) p. xii table of principal domains in 1860 (which explicitly classifies Date and Sō as 外様とざま). The Kuwana correction came from the Hisamatsu-Matsudaira lineage tracing — since 伊予松山いよまつやま and 今治いまばり (both Hisamatsu-Matsudaira) were already coded 親藩しんぱん, consistency requires Kuwana (also Hisamatsu-Matsudaira) be 親藩しんぱん as well.

These mis-classifications matter for the paper because they appear in cross-tabs where category is a regressor. The final distribution in this chapter — 親藩しんぱん 29 / 譜代ふだい 138 / 外様とざま 117 / 御三卿ごさんきょう 2 / 天領てんりょう 1 — reflects the corrected values.

15.9 What this teaches about the paper’s empirical claim

Three lessons from the cross-tabs:

  1. Category and rank are mechanically linked through the 現米げんまい threshold — 譜代ふだい cluster at viscount because their 現米げんまい is small. This is a structural link, not a discretionary one.
  2. Category and Boshin alignment are weakly linked in the data because most domains followed the post-Toba-Fushimi imperial directive. The active-combatant subset is small in every category.
  3. The interaction of category × Boshin × rank is most informative in the deviation sample (chapter 07) — the 19 families whose 1884 rank diverged from 現米げんまい-mechanical prediction split by category as: 3 親藩しんぱん deviations (Shizuoka, Mito, Fukui), 5 外様とざま upgrades + 2 外様とざま downgrades, and 5 譜代ふだい downgrades. The category effect is real but operates through specific rule provisions (the sanke clause, the Tokugawa-main clause, the post-1868-elevation provision), not through general “親藩しんぱん are favoured” or “外様とざま are punished” patterns.
