2  What is Hansei Ichiran?

Hansei Ichiran literally means “han-system overview” — a survey of every daimyō (大名だいみょう) domain (藩はん, han) compiled by the early Meiji government in 1870.

2.1 Why 1870?

By 1870, the Meiji government had been in power for two years. The Tokugawa bakufu (幕府ばくふ, the shōgunate) had been overthrown in the 1868–69 Boshin War (Boshin sensō), but the country was still administratively organized into roughly 280 semi-autonomous han, each ruled by a hereditary daimyō (feudal lord).

The new government needed to know, with precision, what each domain actually was: how big, how rich, how many soldiers, how many households, what taxes it collected. That knowledge was the prerequisite for the next step — abolishing the domains entirely, which happened the following year (1871, haihan chiken (廃藩置県はいはんちけん)).

Hansei Ichiran is the document that captured the han system at the very moment before it was dismantled.

TipWhy this matters for the paper

The paper applies the Political Coase Theorem to explain why Japan’s 1871 abolition of the feudal domains (haihan chiken) occurred peacefully rather than triggering a second civil war. The argument: facing mutual fiscal crises, the Meiji government and the daimyō reached a Coasean bargain — the state assumed domain debts and granted kazoku (華族かぞく, “peerage”) status, stipends, and government offices as institutionalised side payments, in exchange for sole ownership over taxation.

Hansei Ichiran is the quantitative basis for the paper’s claim that the daimyō themselves were facing structural insolvency by 1870. The headline statistic the paper draws from the survey is retainers per 1,000 koku — a measure of how heavily each domain’s hereditary stipend obligations weighed on its revenue base. The 1870 survey shows the burden had become unsustainable for most domains. That is what made the buyout attractive enough to accept without resistance. See the fiscal-distress chapter for the per-domain detail.

2.2 What’s in the book

Hansei Ichiran is two volumes, roughly 1,000 pages each, totaling 2,000-plus printed pages of dense vertical-script Japanese. Within each volume, every domain gets an entry of one to four pages (depending on its size) in a standardised column format that makes the same data fields directly comparable across domains.

The two volumes cover complementary subjects:

  • Volume 1 — fiscal and demographic data. For each domain: the revenue assessment in its several variants (sōdaka (草高くさだか) / omotedaka (表高おもてだか) / naidaka (内高うちだか) / genmai (現米げんまい), all explained later), tax receipts, total population and household counts, and the breakdown of households into samurai (侍さむらい) (shizoku (士族しぞく)), foot-rank retainers (sotsu (卒族そつぞく)), commoners (heimin (平民へいみん)), and religious establishments.

  • Volume 2 — military and administrative data. For each domain: troop strength (infantry, cavalry, riflemen, artillery), the number of warships if any, and a roster of administrative officials (the appointed governor, senior councilors, junior councilors).

The two volumes are each sub-divided internally into several numbered sub-tables that group entries by domain size for printing convenience; that internal numbering has no analytic significance and the data fields are consistent within each volume. So when the walkthrough cites “Hansei Ichiran vol. 1” or “vol. 2”, that’s all the structural detail the reader needs.

2.3 What the printed page looks like

Each domain’s entry is laid out in vertical columns reading right-to-left, in the brushed-style typography of the Meiji era. The first column is the domain’s name preceded by ○ (a circle marker). Subsequent columns list field labels and their values in kanji numerals.

Saga (佐嘉藩さがはん, modern 佐賀藩さがはん) has a Volume 1 fiscal entry but is completely absent from Volume 2’s military rosters — the standard tables of troop strength, officer rosters, and warships that every other major domain has. This is a gap in the source itself, not an extraction failure, and the dataset substitutes 藤野保ふじのたもつ (2010) as a published-source supplement for Saga’s military data.

Glossary of essential terms (used throughout the walkthrough)

  • 藩はん (han) — a feudal domain. By 1870, the political unit ruled by a daimyō.
  • 大名だいみょう (daimyō) — the hereditary lord of a han. There were about 280 in 1870.
  • 石高こくだか (kokudaka) — the assessed rice-yield of a domain. 1 koku ≈ 150 kg of rice, the amount one adult would eat in a year. Used as a proxy for revenue, military obligation, and rank.
  • 公侯伯子男こうこうはくしだん (kō-kō-haku-shi-dan) — the five tiers of the 1884 kazoku (華族かぞく) peerage: prince / marquess / count / viscount / baron.
  • 草高くさだか (sōdaka) / 表高おもてだか (omotedaka) / 現米げんまい (genmai) — three different ways to measure a domain’s revenue. The Hansei Ichiran reports several of them. They are NOT interchangeable; see The Variables for why.
  • 戊辰戦争ぼしんせんそう (Boshin sensō, “Boshin War”) — the 1868–69 civil war between Tokugawa loyalists and the Imperial coalition.
  • 奥羽越列藩同盟おううえつれっぱんどうめい (Ōuetsu Reppan Dōmei) — the Northern Alliance, the pro-Tokugawa coalition of ~31 northern domains during the Boshin War.

2.4 How the original was accessed

The 1870 Hansei Ichiran is held in scan form at the National Diet Library (NDL), Japan’s national library. The project worked from:

  1. The NDL scans, page-by-page
  2. Carefully cropped sub-sections for each variable
  3. Manual transcription cross-checked against OCR

The data extraction was an iterative process; the result is summarized in The Dataset.

Source: National Diet Library Digital Collections (https://dl.ndl.go.jp/). A documented extraction pipeline accompanies the dataset release.
