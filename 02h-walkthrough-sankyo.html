10  Walkthrough: Tokugawa sankyō (御三卿) (一橋 + 田安 + 清水)

The Tokugawa sankyō (徳川三卿) — Hitotsubashi (一橋), Tayasu (田安), and Shimizu (清水) — are an unusual case: three high-status shogunal-family lines that functioned more like household establishments than territorial daimyō (大名) houses, yet still received kazoku (華族) rank in 1884. The project includes them for completeness, and they each appear as a row in analytic_wide.csv. Despite their unusual status, Hansei Ichiran registers them with substantial figures: 一橋藩 (Hitotsubashi) has a full register — kokudaka (石高) 119,429, population 115,228, household count 25,725 — with only its army left blank, while 田安藩 (Tayasu) records no kokudaka of its own but does carry a population of 84,743 and 721 troops.

ImportantNot domains — but in the dataset

The sankyō were:

  • 一橋家 (Hitotsubashi) — founded 1740 by Tokugawa Munetada (son of shōgun (将軍) Yoshimune)
  • 田安家 (Tayasu) — founded 1731 by Tokugawa Munetake (son of Yoshimune)
  • 清水家 (Shimizu) — founded 1759 by Tokugawa Shigeyoshi (son of shōgun Ieshige)

Unlike the gosanke (御三家 — Owari, Kii, Mito — actual daimyō houses with substantial domains), the sankyō were shogunal-family establishments supported largely by stipends drawn on scattered Tokugawa holdings rather than a consolidated castle domain. Their political function was to serve as a pool of potential heirs for the shōgun when the main Tokugawa line failed to produce one. The most famous sankyō-to-shōgun was Tokugawa Yoshinobu (徳川慶喜), who was the head of Hitotsubashi before becoming the 15th and last shōgun in 1866.

Hansei Ichiran does list 一橋藩 and 田安藩 as separate entries (even though strictly speaking they were not han in the territorial sense) — this walkthrough follows Hansei Ichiran’s enumeration. Shimizu is not in the 286-domain dataset because by 1870 the line had become extinct; it was only restored in 1895.

10.2 Why count, not viscount?

The sankyō received count rank in 1884 irrespective of their landholding — the 叙爵内規 (assignment rules) had an explicit exception clause for the sankyō houses, ranking them at count level as a courtesy reflecting their shogunal-family status. (This is why 一橋’s substantial registered kokudaka of 119,429 did not lift it above count, and 田安’s lack of recorded kokudaka did not drop it below.) From the 1884 kazoku Law internal rules:

「徳川氏ノ親族中、三卿（一橋・田安・清水）ノ各家ハ伯爵ニ叙ス」 Among the Tokugawa family, each of the sankyō houses (一橋・田安・清水) shall be ranked at count.

This is one of the several explicit exception clauses written into the 1884 rules:

Exception Houses affected Outcome
Tokugawa main → prince Iesato (Shizuoka) Tokugawa-main exception
Gosanke → marquess (regardless of genmai (現米)) Owari, Kii, Mito Gosanke clause
Sankyō → count Hitotsubashi, Tayasu, Shimizu Sankyō clause
Shimazu (Satsuma) + Mōri (Chōshū) → prince Restoration leaders Restoration reward

So if you look at the genmai-deviation table in chapter 07, the 3 pro-bakufu (幕府) rank-upgrades (Mito marquess, Shizuoka prince, and Ryūoka count) reflect dynastic Tokugawa status — not Restoration merit — and are explained by these clauses rather than by individual discretion.

10.3 The empirical implication for the paper

The sankyō cases are useful off-rule observations in the paper’s regression of 1884 kazoku rank on Boshin alignment + kokudaka:

  • Their rank is rule-bound (count, by sankyō clause), not Boshin-determined
  • Their alignment is recorded as pro-bakufu (a Tokugawa-family affiliation), but they had no fighting role in the Boshin War (Boshin sensō)
  • Including them in the regression sample shows the kokudaka-mechanical rule has limits — 田安 (Tayasu) is the true null-land case (no kokudaka recorded yet ranked count), while 一橋 (Hitotsubashi) carries a substantial kokudaka of 119,429 and still lands at count by the same clause, so for both the sankyō clause overrides the size-based rule

Practical handling: the paper analyzes a subset that excludes the sankyō (since the mechanical rule doesn’t apply to them), but reports them as documented exceptions.

10.4 Tokugawa Yoshinobu — the post-shōgun

Beyond the 1884 ennoblement, an interesting later story: Tokugawa Yoshinobu himself (the 15th shōgun, who had been Hitotsubashi household head before becoming shōgun) received prince rank in 1902 — 34 years after the Restoration, and well before Mito’s own marquess→prince upgrade in 1929. This was a personal honour to the former shōgun, not a family-line ennoblement, and it is not in the 286-domain dataset because Yoshinobu’s personal line was treated separately from both the Tokugawa main line and Hitotsubashi.
