Who this is for

A reader who knows nothing about Tokugawa Japan should be able to follow along. Every Japanese term is glossed at first appearance. Every claim has a citation on the page where it appears, and the full bibliography sits at the end.

A reader who knows the period well will find this useful as a quick reference to what is actually in the dataset, and where the source’s limits begin.

How to navigate

  • The left sidebar jumps between chapters.
  • Each chapter has its own right-hand table of contents for in-page sections.
  • Maps and images expand on click via the browser’s native zoom.
  • The bibliography aggregates every cited source in one place.

How this was built

The analytic dataset (analytic_wide.csv, 287 domains × 107 columns) is the result of:

  • OCR and manual verification of the 1870 Hansei Ichiran’s 2,000-plus pages (the original is held at the National Diet Library).
  • Cross-validation against published primary sources (明治史要めいじしよう (Meiji Shiyō, “Outline of Meiji History”), Ōkuma papers, 統計集誌とうけいしゅうし (Tōkei Shūshi, “Statistical Journal”)).
  • Hand-coded peerage rank assignments traced to 浅見雅男あさみまさお『華族誕生かぞくたんじょう (Kazoku tanjō, “Birth of the Peerage”)』(1994) and 官報かんぽう (Kanpō, “Official Gazette”) 第307号 (1884).
  • Boshin alignment classifications from Esposito (2020), 工藤威くどうたけし (2002), and Beasley (1972).

A complete per-column provenance reference (data_provenance.md) lists, for every column, the source, the citation guidance, the coverage rate, and any known caveats; it will be released alongside the dataset.
