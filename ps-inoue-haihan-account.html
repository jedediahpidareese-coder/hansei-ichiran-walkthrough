30  Inoue Kaoru’s account

From the 1933–34 authorized biography

Inoue Kaoru was deputy Finance Minister (Ōkura-tayū 大蔵大輔) in 1871 and one of the men who planned and carried out the abolition. His authorized biography devotes a chapter to it, drawing on his own papers and his later recollections. Three passages give the implementer’s view: why the reform was urgent, how it was planned, and how it would be enforced.

30.1 The fiscal case

Asked what brought two colleagues to his door, Inoue answered that only one thing could be meant by “a great matter of state”:

一體政府が舊幕府から受繼いだ領土は八百萬石であつて、その歲入を大蔵省で集めても、實際些細にして使用に窮する。… それより急務なものは無い。

The government as a whole has inherited eight million koku of territory from the former bakufu, but even when the Ministry of Finance collects this revenue, it is in reality so trivial that we struggle to put it to use. … There is no more urgent matter than this.

The central government had ambitions and debts but almost no tax base of its own — because the taxes were still being collected by the domains. That is the fiscal pressure behind the reform, in the words of the man who ran the ministry.

30.2 The planning council, 9 July 1871

[七月九日]…のタ五時から九段上の木戸邸に於て廃藩置縣斷行の會議が開かれた。之に會したのは、公及び西郷兄弟・大久保・山縣・大山等で…知藩事を免官する一條は、その薨京を俟つて之を發令し…

On 9 July, from 5 p.m., a council was convened at Kido’s residence atop Kudan-zaka to decide on carrying out the abolition. Those gathered were Inoue, the Saigō brothers, Ōkubo, Yamagata, Ōyama, and others. … As for dismissing the domain governors, it was decided that the decree would be issued only after they had assembled in Tokyo.

Timing the decree for when the lords were in Tokyo — away from their home territories and retainers — was a deliberate choice, made five days before the edict.

30.3 How it would be enforced

In a later recollection, Inoue described the assurances he sought before the decree — the Imperial Guard to back it, the finances to settle it, and a shared resolve to resign together if the court nobles blocked it:

御親兵となつて遣付けて… 金の方は宜いかと云ふ論。御請合ひ申すと云ふ。… 結局が付かぬ時分には貴所方どうするか… それは決心して皆袖を聯ねて退かう。

“We will use the Imperial Guard (御親兵) to enforce it. … ‘Is the money side in order?’ — they assured me it was. … ‘And when the [court council’s] debate drags on without conclusion, what will you all do?’ … the resolve was: ‘Then we will all join sleeves and withdraw’ [resign together].”

30.4 What it shows

Inoue’s account supplies the half the diaries leave implicit: the reform rested on a fiscal motive (a revenue-starved center), a careful plan (issue the decree when the lords were gathered and the finances were guaranteed), and a credible fallback (the Imperial Guard, and the threat of a mass resignation inside the government). It is the practical underside of the bargain told in The bargain of 1871.

Source: 『世外井上公伝 (Sekkai Inoue-kō Den)』vol. 1, 第三編第三章第一節「廃藩置縣」, book pp. 449–452 (the authorized biography of Inoue Kaoru, 1933–34).
