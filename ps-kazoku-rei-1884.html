33  The peerage decree

Kanpō No. 307 — 7 July 1884 (gazetted 8 July)

The peerage ordinance (kazoku-rei 華族令) of 1884 established the five-rank peerage and sorted the old and new elites into it. It is the status half of the 1871 settlement — the durable, hereditary honor that bound the former-daimyō families and the Restoration’s architects to the new regime.

33.1 The decree

朕惟フニ華族勳冑ハ國ノ藩屏ナリ宜シク授爵スルニ榮爵ヲ以テシ用テ寵光ヲ示スヘシ文武諸臣中興ノ偉業ヲ翼贊シ國ニ大勞アル者宜シク均シク優列ニ陞シ用テ殊典ヲ昭ニスヘシ茲ニ五爵ヲ叙テ其有禮ヲ秩ス…

On reflection: the kazoku and meritorious lineages are the bulwark of the nation. It is fitting that We bestow honored titles upon them, to show imperial favor. Civilian and military officials who have assisted the great enterprise of the Restoration, those who have rendered great service to the nation, should all alike be raised to the higher ranks, that their special honors be made manifest. Hereby We establish the Five Ranks [五爵] and order their proper hierarchy.

— Meiji 17 / 7 / 7 (7 July 1884)

The Five Ranks are kō-kō-haku-shi-dan (公侯伯子男): prince, marquess, count, viscount, baron.

33.2 Two grounds for ennoblement

The decree names two distinct reasons for a title in one sentence — and the peerage was built on both:

  • Lineage — “the kazoku and meritorious lineages are the bulwark of the nation.” The former daimyō and court houses were ranked by who they were. The four leading domains sat at the top: Shimazu (Satsuma) and Mōri (Chōshū) as princes, Nabeshima (Saga) and Yamauchi (Tosa) as marquesses. How these ranks were assigned, and the cases that deviate from the rule, are in the peerage chapter and the nineteen deviations.
  • Merit — “officials who have assisted the great enterprise of the Restoration.” The men who had run the Restoration and the new state were ranked by what they had done. Of the sixteen senior-merit counts created in 1884, all sixteen came from the four leading domains — seven from Satsuma, six from Chōshū, two from Tosa, one from Saga (Yamagata, Itō Hirobumi, Kuroda, Inoue, and the rest).

The coalition that abolished the domains in 1871 supplied the entire top of the merit peerage in 1884.

33.3 What it shows

The peerage made the settlement permanent. It was hereditary, and within five years — under the 1889 constitution — princes and marquesses held automatic seats in the House of Peers. At that point the government could not strip kazoku status without dismantling part of its own legislature. It also reached the losing side: the head of the defeated Sendai-Date house received a count rank under the ordinary lineage formula, listed among the non-penalized houses (see the Aizu–Sendai walkthrough). The narrative is in The settlement.

Source: 『官報』第三百七號 (Kanpō No. 307), Meiji 17/7/8 (8 July 1884), page 1 (the imperial decree, dated 7 July) and pages 3–4 (the ennoblement roster). National Diet Library Digital Collections, pid 2943511 (Public Domain Mark).
