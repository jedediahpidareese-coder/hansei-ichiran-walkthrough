5  Walkthrough: Chōshū (長州藩ちょうしゅうはん / 山口藩やまぐちはん / 萩藩はん)

Chōshū is the second pillar of the Sat-Chō Restoration coalition. Its Kiheitai (奇兵隊きへいたい, “extraordinary corps”) under Takasugi Shinsaku pioneered mixed-class peasant-samurai (侍さむらい) units that defeated the bakufu (幕府ばくふ, the shōgunate)’s Second Chōshū Expedition in 1866 — the military demonstration that made the Restoration possible.

ImportantThree names — same place
  • 長州藩ちょうしゅうはん (Chōshū-han) — historical/canonical project name (after the Chōshū province)
  • 萩藩はん (Hagi-han) — the 1664-era seat city (Hagi Castle)
  • 山口藩やまぐちはん (Yamaguchi-han) — post-1869 administrative rename (seat moved to Yamaguchi)

Hansei Ichiran prints 山口藩やまぐちはん (the post-1869 administrative form). The project’s canonical name is the older 長州藩ちょうしゅうはん. The Mōri family ruled all three iterations.

5.2 Military capacity — modernization first

Where Satsuma was big on traditional samurai, Chōshū was big on modernized doctrine. By the time of Boshin:

  • 17 standing 大隊だいたい (battalions) × 433 troops = 7,361 men in the active modernized force
  • Kiheitai (奇兵隊きへいたい): originally 300 troops, expanded to 622 by Boshin — Japan’s first cross-class regular army unit, with samurai, peasants, and townsmen serving alongside each other
  • 諸隊しょたい (other militia corps): ~5,000 men at peak, consolidated to ~2,250 standing army post-Boshin
  • Warships: 4 named vessels (per Hansei Ichiran)
  • Modernized small arms: French Chassepots + Enfields + Minié rifles

The Chōshū army (and the doctrine it pioneered) became the template for the Imperial Japanese Army that Yamagata Aritomo would build after 1873.

5.3 Why Chōshū got upgraded to prince

Same rule provision as Satsuma — the 1884 叙爵内規じょしゃくないき explicitly named Mōri (Chōshū) for prince rank as a Restoration reward.

Mōri Motonori (毛利元徳もうりもとのり), the last Chōshū daimyō (大名だいみょう), received prince in 1884. The promotion to prince in 1884 was confirmed in the very first publication of the 1884 ennoblement decree (官報かんぽう 第307号, 1884-07-08).
