8  Walkthrough: Aizu (会津 / 斗南) + Sendai (仙台)

These two pro-bakufu (幕府) / Northern Alliance leaders are the inverse of Satsuma + Chōshū: large domains that fought against the Imperial coalition and bore the consequences in the 1884 rank assignments.

8.1 Aizu (会津藩 → 斗南藩)

ImportantPre-Boshin vs Post-Boshin names
  • 会津藩 (Aizu-han) — the pre-Boshin name; ~230,000 koku (marquess range)
  • 斗南藩 (Tonami-han) — post-Boshin name (1869+); reduced to ~30,000 koku as punishment

The project’s canonical name is 斗南藩 because that’s what existed at the moment of Hansei Ichiran’s 1870 survey. The pre-Boshin Aizu was abolished after defeat; the Matsudaira family was relocated to Mutsu-Tonami at one-tenth their previous territory.

8.1.2 The historiographical debate

Lebra (1993) frames Aizu’s viscount rank as a Boshin punishment — Aizu was a major bakufu loyalist (Matsudaira Katamori was Kyoto Protector and a key Tokugawa loyalist), defeated at the Aizu siege (October-November 1868), and got the lowest peerage rank that any major domain received.

Asami (1994) reframes: the punishment was the territorial reduction from 230k to 30k koku, not the rank. Once the territory was reduced, the genmai-rule mechanically yielded viscount. The rank itself is rule-consistent.

Either framing yields the same outcome. The paper records both interpretations.

8.1.3 Military capacity

The Hansei Ichiran record for 斗南藩 reflects the post-reduction reality. For Aizu’s pre-Boshin military:

  • Four-corps age-graded system (Suzakutai 18-35, Seiryūtai 35-49, Byakkotai 16-17, Genbutai 50+)
  • Byakkotai (White Tigers): the famous teenage corps; ~300 youth, many of whom committed seppuku at the fall of Wakamatsu Castle
  • Modernization: limited compared to Chōshū/Satsuma; mostly traditional armor + percussion muskets

(Source: Esposito 2020.)

8.2.2 Why Sendai matters as a paper case

Sendai is the canonical example of the Lebra-vs-Asami debate:

  • Lebra (1993): Sendai was a Tokugawa ally → Boshin defeat → downgrade from marquess to count = explicit Boshin punishment
  • Asami (1994): Sendai’s 1869 genmai was 67,740 koku → squarely in count range mechanically → no punishment, just the rule

Both interpretations yield the same observed outcome (count). The dataset records both deviation values (Lebra framing alongside Asami framing) so the paper can present whichever baseline is appropriate.

NoteA third framing — the Kanpō #307 「依父勳功特授伯爵」 finding (added 2026-05-25)

The project’s 2026-05-25 transcription of Kanpō 第307号 (the 1884-07-08 government gazette publishing the ennoblement decree) records a third formal categorization for Sendai-Date:

從五位　伊達 宗基　← 依父勳功特授伯爵 (Junior Fifth Rank, Date Munemoto, Count rank specially conferred by the FATHER’S meritorious service)

The 「依父勳功特授」 prefix puts Date Munemoto on the merit-based ennoblement track — the same legal track as the 83 Restoration architects (Itō Hirobumi, Yamagata Aritomo, Inoue Kaoru, etc.). The Sendai count rank is therefore not lineage-mechanical in the way most count-tier ranks were; it’s an inherited merit ennoblement on his father’s recorded service.

The three frames are not mutually exclusive — they describe different layers of the same outcome:

Frame Source What it says
Boshin-penalty Lebra (1993) The territorial reduction from ~620k → ~280k post-Boshin was the punishment, lowering the kokudaka basis
Genmai-mechanical Asami (1994) The post-reduction genmai (67,740 koku) put Sendai in count band; the rank was the threshold-rule outcome
Patrilineal-merit Kanpō #307 (2026-05-25) The formal legal track was 「依父勳功特授伯爵」 — merit-track ennoblement inherited from father’s service, distinct from both pure-lineage and pure-merit

For paper writing: Sendai’s Count rank was both the genmai-threshold mechanical outcome (Asami) AND a formal patrilineal merit-track ennoblement (Kanpō #307), atop a post-Boshin territorial reduction (Lebra). See kazoku_rei_decree_kanpo_307.md for the verbatim Kanpō entry and the broader 83-person merit-track roster.

3 years old at haihan-chiken (1871), 16 at the kazoku-rei (1884) — Date Munemoto was too young for his own meritorious service to ground a 1884 ennoblement; the patrilineal-merit framing was the legal instrument that handled that age fact.

8.2.3 Military capacity — a data-quality correction

Sendai had a documented data-quality issue: the original extraction had the sotsu-class headcount of 18,913 mislabelled as the total-troops figure (the OCR conflated 兵卒 with 兵士). The post-fix state:

  • Total troops = 14,262 (兵士 員 — the actual active force, per Hansei Ichiran vol. 2)
  • sotsu-class headcount = 18,913 (sotsu 同人口 — sotsu-class demographics, not troops)

See the data-quality chapter for the audit trail behind this correction.

8.2.4 Northern Alliance leadership

Sendai was the lead signatory of the Ōuetsu Reppan Dōmei (奥羽越列藩同盟, Northern Alliance) at the White Stone Conference of May 6, 1868. The alliance covenant was drafted there. Aizu and Sendai are the only two Northern Alliance members that Beasley (1972) names by name; the full 31-member roster is from 工藤威『奥羽列藩同盟の基礎的研究 (Ōu reppan dōmei no kisoteki kenkyū, “Basic Research on the Northern Alliance”)』(2002).
