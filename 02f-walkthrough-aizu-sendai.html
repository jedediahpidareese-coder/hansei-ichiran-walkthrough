8  Walkthrough: Aizu (会津あいづ / 斗南となみ) + Sendai (仙台せんだい)

These two pro-bakufu (幕府ばくふ) / Northern Alliance leaders are the inverse of Satsuma + Chōshū: large domains that fought against the Imperial coalition and bore the consequences in the 1884 rank assignments.

8.1 Aizu (会津藩あいづはん → 斗南藩となみはん)

ImportantPre-Boshin vs Post-Boshin names
  • 会津藩あいづはん (Aizu-han) — the pre-Boshin name; ~230,000 koku (marquess range)
  • 斗南藩となみはん (Tonami-han) — post-Boshin name (1869+); reduced to ~30,000 koku as punishment

The project’s canonical name is 斗南藩となみはん because that’s what existed at the moment of Hansei Ichiran’s 1870 survey. The pre-Boshin Aizu was abolished after defeat; the Matsudaira family was relocated to Mutsu-Tonami at one-tenth their previous territory.

8.1.2 The historiographical debate

Lebra (1993) frames Aizu’s viscount rank as a Boshin punishment — Aizu was a major bakufu loyalist (Matsudaira Katamori was Kyoto Protector and a key Tokugawa loyalist), defeated at the Aizu siege (October-November 1868), and got the lowest peerage rank that any major domain received.

Asami (1994) reframes: the punishment was the territorial reduction from 230k to 30k koku, not the rank. Once the territory was reduced, the genmai-rule mechanically yielded viscount. The rank itself is rule-consistent.

Either framing yields the same outcome. The paper records both interpretations.

8.1.3 Military capacity

The Hansei Ichiran record for 斗南藩となみはん reflects the post-reduction reality. For Aizu’s pre-Boshin military:

  • Four-corps age-graded system (Suzakutai 18-35, Seiryūtai 35-49, Byakkotai 16-17, Genbutai 50+)
  • Byakkotai (White Tigers): the famous teenage corps; ~300 youth, many of whom committed seppuku at the fall of Wakamatsu Castle
  • Modernization: limited compared to Choshu/Satsuma; mostly traditional armor + percussion muskets

(Source: Esposito 2020.)

8.2 Sendai (仙台藩せんだいはん / 伊達だて)

ImportantSendai = Date

仙台藩せんだいはん is also known by the family name 伊達だて (Date). The Date Masamune lineage. The project’s canonical name is 仙台藩せんだいはん (the seat city).

8.2.2 Why Sendai matters as a paper case

Sendai is the canonical example of the Lebra-vs-Asami debate:

  • Lebra (1993): Sendai was a Tokugawa ally → Boshin defeat → downgrade from marquess to count = explicit Boshin punishment
  • Asami (1994): Sendai’s 1869 genmai was 67,740 koku → squarely in count range mechanically → no punishment, just the rule

Both interpretations yield the same observed outcome (count). The dataset records both deviation values (Lebra framing alongside Asami framing) so the paper can present whichever baseline is appropriate.

8.2.3 Military capacity — recent data fix

Sendai had a documented data-quality issue: the original extraction had the sotsu-class headcount of 18,913 mislabelled as the total-troops figure (the OCR conflated 兵卒へいそつ with 兵士へいし). The post-fix state:

  • Total troops = 14,262 (兵士へいし 員 — the actual active force, per Hansei Ichiran vol. 2)
  • sotsu-class headcount = 18,913 (sotsu 同人口じんこう — sotsu-class demographics, not troops)

See the data-quality chapter for the audit trail behind this correction.

8.2.4 Northern Alliance leadership

Sendai was the lead signatory of the Ōuetsu Reppan Dōmei (奥羽越列藩同盟おううえつれっぱんどうめい, Northern Alliance) at the White Stone Conference of May 6, 1868. The alliance covenant was drafted there. Aizu and Sendai are the only two Northern Alliance members that Beasley (1972) names by name; the full 31-member roster is from 工藤威くどうたけし『Ōu reppan dōmei no kisoteki kenkyū』(2002).
