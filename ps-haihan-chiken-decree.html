28  The abolition decree

Meiji 4 / 7 / 14 — 29 August 1871

On 29 August 1871 the emperor abolished the domains (haihan chiken 廃藩置県). Two contemporary records preserve the moment: the imperial proclamation itself, reproduced in Inoue Kaoru’s biography, and the government’s own chronicle, Meiji Shiyō, which records who was summoned and commended that day.

28.1 The proclamation

The text of the imperial rescript, as reproduced in the Inoue biography:

朕惟フニ、更始ノ時ニ際シ、内以テ億兆ヲ保安シ、外以テ萬國ト對峙セント欲セハ、宜ク名實相副ヒ、政令一ニ歸セシムヘシ。朕嚢ニ諸藩版籍奉還ノ議ヲ聴納シ、新ニ知藩事ヲ命シ、各其職ヲ奉セシム。然ルニ數百年因襲ノ久キ、或ハ其名アリテ其實舉ラサル者アリ。… 仍テ今更ニ藩ヲ廢シ縣ト爲ス。… 汝群臣其レ朕カ意ヲ體セヨ。

On reflection: at this moment of new beginning — if we are to safeguard the millions at home and stand on a par with the nations of the world abroad — name and substance must correspond, and political authority must be unified in one place. I previously heeded the proposal of the various domains for the return of the registers, newly appointed governors, and had each take up that office. But after several hundred years of inherited custom, some hold the name without the substance. … Therefore I now abolish the domains and make them prefectures. … You, my ministers, embody my intent.

The two reasons the decree gives for itself — order at home, standing abroad — are the frame the whole reform was justified in: not punishment of the domains, but a state made coherent enough to survive in a dangerous world.

28.2 The chronicle entry

Meiji Shiyō’s entry for the same day records the ceremony and the commendations:

十四日詔シテ列藩ヲ廢シテ悉ク縣ト爲ス。… 各藩知事ヲ御前ニ召シ、勅シテ島津忠義、毛利元徳、鍋島直大、山内豊範ノ版籍奉還ヲ首唱セシヲ賞シ、又徳川慶勝、細川護久、池田慶徳、蜂須賀茂韶ノ建議ヲ立纒シ、其當ヲ得テ嘉ス。

Day 14: by decree, the domains are abolished and all made into prefectures. … The domain governors were summoned before the emperor; by imperial word, Shimazu Tadayoshi [Satsuma], Mōri Motonori [Chōshū], Nabeshima Naohiro [Saga], and Yamauchi Toyonori [Tosa] were praised for having first proposed the return of the registers; and Tokugawa Yoshikatsu [Owari], Hosokawa Morihisa [Kumamoto], Ikeda Yoshinori [Tottori], and Hachisuka Mochiaki [Tokushima], whose proposals had been adopted, were commended with imperial approval.

28.3 What it shows

The chronicle names eight commended houses, in two groups: the four leading domains of the Restoration — Satsuma, Chōshū, Saga, Tosa — credited for proposing the 1869 return of the registers, and four more credited for helping build the new system. The second group spans the status spectrum, from a senior Tokugawa-related house (Owari) to major outside lords (Kumamoto, Tokushima). The public thanks went to a wider circle than the famous four — one small sign of how broadly the bargain reached. The narrative of the day is in The bargain of 1871.

Proclamation text: 『世外井上公伝 (Sekkai Inoue-kō Den)』vol. 1, book p. 452 (the imperial rescript as reproduced in Inoue Kaoru’s authorized biography, 1933–34).

Chronicle entry: 修史局編『明治史要 (Meiji Shiyō)』本紀 vol. 1, Meiji 4/7/14 entry, book pp. 26–28. National Diet Library Digital Collections.
