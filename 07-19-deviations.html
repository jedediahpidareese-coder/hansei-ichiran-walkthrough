20  Patterns in the 1884 rank assignments

This chapter documents a specific pattern in the dataset: 19 cases where the 1884 鎌族 (華族かぞく (kazoku)) peerage rank a family received departed from what the 叙爵内規じょしゃくないき (assignment rules) would have predicted from their domain’s revenue base alone. These 19 cases are not the paper’s central empirical finding — the paper’s argument is the Political Coase Theorem framing — but they are an interesting set of observations that illuminate the institutional architecture of the 1884 ennoblement, and they cluster cleanly with the paper’s reading of the 華族かぞく system as institutional bargain rather than discretionary reward.

ImportantFamily vs domain framing

A “rank deviation” is a divergence between the rank the 叙爵内規じょしゃくないき predicted for a 大名だいみょう (daimyō) family and the rank that family actually received from the Imperial Court in 1884. Domains had been abolished in 1871; the family was the continuing unit. The map below shows each domain coloured by the type of deviation experienced by the family that ruled it in 1870 — but the rank, and therefore the deviation, belongs to the family, not the domain.

“Satsuma had a marquess → prince upgrade” is shorthand for “the 島津しまづ (Shimazu) family that ruled Satsuma in 1870 had a marquess→prince upgrade in 1884.”

20.1 Why are there deviations at all?

Under the naive 草高くさだか (sōdaka) reading of the threshold rule, 93 domains in the dataset show “deviations” from the 1884 mechanical prediction. That’s a lot of deviations, and an earlier literature (Lebra 1993, for example) reads many of them as discretionary Boshin punishments and rewards.

Two examples were often cited under that reading:

  • 仙台藩せんだいはん (Sendai, Date) — 草高くさだか ~620,000 koku (well into marquess territory), but received only count. Lebra read this as a Boshin penalty.
  • 会津藩あいづはん → 斗南藩となみはん (Aizu → Tonami) — pre-Boshin 草高くさだか ~230,000 koku, but the post-Boshin remnant received only viscount.

The deviations-as-discretionary-punishment reading would be one way to support a “Boshin loyalty matters for rank” story. But it falls apart when you re-baseline the threshold to the figure the rule actually used.

20.2 The Asami (1994) correction

But Asami (1994) argues the threshold rule operated on 現米げんまい (genmai) — the post-Meiji-2 tax-reported rice flow, which is much lower than 草高くさだか for most domains.

Re-checking:

  • Sendai 現米げんまい = 67,740 koku → squarely in count range. Sendai’s rank is mechanical, not punishment.
  • 斗南となみ 現米げんまい = 7,380 koku → squarely in viscount range. Also mechanical.

(The post-Boshin territorial reduction of Aizu from 23万石まんごく to 3万石まんごく was itself the punishment — at the territorial level, not the rank level.)

Applying the 現米げんまい rule mechanically: of the 93 “石高こくだか (kokudaka) rule” deviations, 76 disappear (they were mechanical 現米げんまい outcomes all along). Only 19 remain — the truly anomalous cases.

Of 9 Restoration-service upgrades, 8 are anti-幕府ばくふ / pro-restoration, 1 is pro-幕府ばくふ (Tatsuoka, but the family had Restoration service via 大給おぎゅう松平まつだいら).

The 3 pro-幕府ばくふ deviations in the full table are:

  • Shizuoka — the Tokugawa main line, governed by the sanke-main clause
  • Mito — sanke exception clause (the 1884 叙爵内規じょしゃくないき had a clause written specifically to elevate Mito above the 現米げんまい threshold)
  • Tatsuoka — Ōgyū-Matsudaira, classified pro-幕府ばくふ by family-affiliation but had Restoration service contributions

So the apparent “pro-幕府ばくふ also get deviations” pattern is explained by separate rule provisions, not Boshin alignment per se.

20.7 Why this is a clean paper finding

The simple conditional probability P(deviation | Boshin alignment) is statistically uninformative — roughly equal across groups (6.4% anti-幕府ばくふ vs 7.3% pro-幕府ばくふ). The naive marginal effect of Boshin alignment on “any deviation” is small.

But the structure of deviations is highly informative:

  • All 9 Restoration-service upgrades → anti-幕府ばくふ side
  • All 3 pro-幕府ばくふ deviations → Tokugawa-cadet exception clauses
  • All 8 viscount→baron downgrades → former 付家老つけがろう 陪臣 (orthogonal to Boshin)

The paper’s core empirical claim is therefore narrow but strong: the 1884 peerage system rewarded Restoration-service families specifically; the rule-bound mechanism of those rewards is identifiable in the data.

20.9 The 19, one by one

This section walks each of the 19 cases in turn, organised by deviation type. For families already covered in earlier walkthroughs — Shimazu (chapter 02b), Mōri (chapter 02c), and the five viscount→count houses (chapter 02i) — this section gives a paragraph summary with a cross-link. For the remaining 11 cases (徳川家達とくがわいえさと, 徳川篤敬とくがわあつよし, 松平茂昭まつだいらもちあき, 伊達宗徳だてむねえ, and all eight viscount→baron houses), this section provides first-pass per-family narratives.

TipReading aid

The same family-not-domain framing from the top of this chapter applies throughout. Where a block says “Mōri received prince,” it means the Mōri family received prince rank in 1884, indexed to the domain (Chōshū) they ruled in 1870. Domains were abolished in 1871.

20.9.1 A. Marquess → Prince (3 cases)

The three prince rank assignments span the full Boshin spectrum: two anti-幕府ばくふ Restoration leaders (Shimazu of Satsuma, Mōri of Chōshū) and the Tokugawa main-line exception (Tokugawa Iesato, post-1868 head of the Sunpu/Shizuoka relocation).

A 現米げんまい of nearly 300,000 koku would place Shimazu solidly in marquess territory under the standard rule. The prince rank is the explicit Restoration-leader clause of the 1884 華族かぞく-rei: Shimazu (Satsuma) and Mōri (Chōshū) are named in the rule provisions as recipients of prince rank for their leadership of the Restoration. (See chapter 02b for the full Shimazu / Satsuma walkthrough.)

Family 毛利家もうりけ (Mōri)

A 現米げんまい of 224,000 koku is well into marquess range. The prince rank is the matching half of the named Restoration-leader exception. The Mōri family’s distinctive contribution beyond the joint Sat-Chō diplomacy was the modernised standing army, built around the Kiheitai and related 諸隊しょたい. 20.9.1.3 静岡藩しずおかはん — Tokugawa main-line exception

Family 徳川家とくがわけ (Tokugawa main line)
1884 recipient 徳川家達とくがわいえさと
石高こくだか (Hansei Ichiran 表高おもてだか) 699,182 koku (post-1868 relocation; pre-1868 shogunal tenryō was vastly larger)
Genmai 209,990 koku
Genmai-rule expectation marquess
1884 outcome prince (公爵こうしゃく)
Boshin role Pro-幕府ばくふ (this is the former 幕府ばくふ)
Why prince Tokugawa main-line exception — its own clause, distinct from the sanke and Restoration-leader clauses

After the 幕府ばくふ’s defeat in 1868 the Tokugawa main line was relocated from Edo to a 70-万まん-石こく territory centred on Sunpu (modern Shizuoka). The defeated party was nevertheless granted prince rank in 1884 because the 華族かぞく-rei contained an explicit Tokugawa-main-line clause — separate from both the sanke clause (covering Owari/Kii/Mito) and the Restoration-leader clause (covering Shimazu/Mōri). 徳川家達とくがわいえさと (1863–1940), great-grandson of the 11th 将軍 (shōgun) Ienari and grandson by adoption to the 14th 将軍 Iemochi, was the recipient.

The methodological point: the 1884 rules ennobled by what the rules said, not by who won the war. The defeated central party received the highest rank — because the rules said so.

20.9.2 B. Count → Marquess (3 cases)

Three families received marquess rather than the 現米げんまい-predicted count. One was the explicit sanke clause (Mito-Tokugawa); the other two were Restoration-loyalty rewards arriving years after the initial 1884 ennoblements (1888 for Echizen-Matsudaira/Fukui, 1891 for Date-Uwajima).

20.9.2.1 水戸藩みとはん — Mito-Tokugawa, the sanke clause

Family 水戸徳川家みととくがわけ (one of the 御三家ごさんけ)
1884 recipient 徳川篤敬とくがわあつよし
石高こくだか (Hansei Ichiran 合高ごうだか) 306,776 koku
Genmai 58,553 koku
Genmai-rule expectation count
1884 outcome marquess (侯爵こうしゃく)
Boshin role Pro-幕府ばくふ by family-affiliation (the family produced the 15th 将軍 Yoshinobu; Mito’s internal politics were violently divided)
Why marquess Sanke clause — explicit rule provision for the three Tokugawa cadet houses

Mito’s 現米げんまい of 58,553 koku sits squarely in count range. The 1884 華族かぞく-rei nevertheless elevated the family to marquess because of an explicit “徳川とくがわ旧三家きゅうさんけ” clause: the heads of the three sanke (御三家ごさんけ = Owari, Kii, Mito) were granted marquess rank regardless of 現米げんまい. Asami (1994), pp. 109–110, reproduces the rule text.

The clause was written for all three sanke; Owari and Kii’s 現米げんまい were high enough to qualify for marquess on the standard rule, so only Mito triggered the actual exception. (Mito’s revenue had been substantially reduced through the 1860s; Owari and Kii were less affected.) Like Shizuoka, this is a case where the deviation is explained by a separately-written rule provision, not by Boshin alignment.

1884 recipient 松平茂昭まつだいらもちあき — count, 1884

1884 outcome count → marquess (侯爵こうしゃく), 1888

Why upgraded Restoration role — 松平春嶽まつだいらしゅんがく (=慶永よしなが) was a 四賢侯しけんこう / 公議政体派こうぎせいたいは figure central to the Restoration negotiations

Initially ennobled count in 1884 — exactly the 現米げんまい rule’s prediction — the Fukui-Matsudaira family was promoted to marquess in 1888. The citation given was the role of the previous head 松平春嶽まつだいらしゅんがく (松平慶永まつだいらよしなが, 1828–1890) in the Meiji Restoration. Shungaku led the public-deliberation reformist faction within the broader Tokugawa orbit, served briefly as the 幕府ばくふ’s 政事総裁職せいじそうさいしょく in 1862, brokered between 幕府ばくふ and court during the 1864–1867 negotiations, and joined the new government’s senior advisory ranks in 1868–1872.

The 1888 timing matters: it shows the 1884 cohort was not the only ennoblement event. The Meiji peerage system allowed promotion for Restoration service on a rolling basis as historical assessments matured. Fukui is the canonical example of this rolling-promotion mechanism.

石高こくだか (Hansei Ichiran 草高くさだか) 100,000 koku

Why upgraded Restoration role — Munenari’s status as a 公議政体派こうぎせいたいは figure and Restoration-government advisor

Uwajima received count rank in 1884 — exactly what the 現米げんまい rule predicts. In 1891 the family was promoted to marquess, citing the role of 伊達宗城だてむねなり (1818–1892), one of the 四賢侯しけんこう (Four Wise Lords) of late-Tokugawa Japan alongside Shungaku of Fukui, 山内容堂やまうちようどう of Tosa, and 島津斉彬しまづなりあきら of Satsuma. Like Shungaku, Munenari was a bridging figure during the 1860s court-幕府ばくふ negotiations and served in senior advisory roles in the early Meiji government.

The structural irony: the main Date line at Sendai received only count rank (because their post-Boshin 現米げんまい of 67,740 koku fell into the count band — itself partly a Boshin-induced territorial reduction), while the Date cadet at Uwajima was promoted past them to marquess. The Date name appears on both sides of the Restoration ledger.

20.9.4 D. Viscount → Baron (8 cases — post-1868 newly-elevated 大名だいみょう)

All eight viscount→baron downgrades share a single structural feature: each family became “true 大名だいみょう” only at the end of the Tokugawa era or at the Restoration itself. Either they were 付家老つけがろう (vassals-of-a-vassal, attached to a major-domain house) detached at the 1868 reorganisation, or they were 旗本はたもと (direct Tokugawa banner-men) bumped above the 10,000-koku 大名だいみょう threshold in 1868–1869.

The 1884 華族かぞく-rei contained a separate provision for these post-1868 elevations: rather than receiving viscount as the 現米げんまい rule would predict, they were placed at baron, reflecting their pre-Restoration 陪臣 / hatamoto status. The downgrade is therefore explained by pre-1868 institutional history, not by Boshin alignment — five of the eight are coded anti-幕府ばくふ; one is neutral; two are pro-幕府ばくふ by family affiliation (the 付家老つけがろう cases). The pattern is orthogonal to Restoration coalition.

The eight split 4 + 4 between the two routes.

The 4 付家老つけがろう (vassal-of-a-vassal) cases:

20.9.4.1 今尾藩いまおはん — 竹腰家たけのこしけ, Owari 付家老つけがろう

Family 竹腰家たけのこしけ (Takenokoshi)
1884 recipient 竹腰正旧たけのこしまさよし
石高こくだか (Hansei Ichiran 草高くさだか) 17,907 koku
Genmai 5,937 koku
Genmai-rule expectation viscount
1884 outcome baron (男爵だんしゃく)
Boshin role Anti-幕府ばくふ (followed Owari-Tokugawa’s late-Restoration alignment)
Why baron 付家老つけがろう — Owari attached house, only independent post-1868

The Takenokoshi were one of three 付家老つけがろう houses attached to Owari-Tokugawa (along with Ishikawa and Naruse). Their function for centuries was to serve as senior advisors and de-facto overseers of the Owari main line on the 幕府ばくふ’s behalf — a structural device by which the 幕府ばくふ kept direct visibility into the major cadet branches. The Takenokoshi held about 30,000 koku in Mino (around Imao, modern Gifu) but counted as Owari 陪臣 rather than independent 大名だいみょう. In 1868 the 付家老つけがろう system was dissolved and the three Owari attached houses became formally independent. The 1884 baron rank reflects this very recent independence.

Family 水野家みずのけ (Mizuno)

Of the Kii (紀州きしゅう) attached structure, two 付家老つけがろう houses — Mizuno of 新宮しんぐう (eastern Kii) and Andō of 田辺たなべ (western Kii) — were detached at the Restoration. The Mizuno had administered eastern Kii from Shingū for over two centuries while formally remaining Kii 陪臣. Their post-1868 independence + baron rank follows the same pattern as the Owari 付家老つけがろう.

Why baron 付家老つけがろう — Mito-Tokugawa attached house

The Nakayama were Mito’s 付家老つけがろう — the structural parallel to Takenokoshi in Owari. Their seat at Matsuoka (modern Ibaraki) was small, but they had long held senior administrative roles within the Mito han, including arbiter functions during Mito’s violently divided 1864–1868 internal politics (the 天狗党てんぐとう / 諸生党しょせいとう conflict). Their 1884 baron rank reflects the same post-1868 institutional separation as the other 付家老つけがろう cases.

Why baron 旗本はたもと → 1万石まんごく elevation only in 1868

The Hondō were a Hitachi (modern Ibaraki) 旗本はたもと (hatamoto) house at 8,000 koku for most of the Tokugawa era — just below the 10,000-koku 大名だいみょう threshold the 幕府ばくふ used to distinguish 大名だいみょう from banner-men. In 1868 the new imperial government bumped them to 10,000 koku, making them nominally 大名だいみょう. Their 1884 baron rank reflects this very recent elevation; they had never been “real” multigenerational 大名だいみょう of the kind the standard viscount-tier rule envisioned.

Why baron 交代寄合こうたいよりあい → 1万石まんごく elevation only in 1868; intermediate pre-Restoration status

The Yamasaki of Nariwa (modern Okayama, 高梁市たかはしし) were 交代寄合こうたいよりあい (kōtai yoriai) — an intermediate status between 旗本はたもと and full 大名だいみょう that gave the family some 大名だいみょう-like privileges (sankin kōtai attendance) without being formally counted as 大名だいみょう. They held about 5,000 koku for most of the Tokugawa era and were promoted to 10,000 koku 大名だいみょう status in 1868. Their baron rank follows the same post-1868 elevation rule.

Nariwa is the lone “neutral” in this 8-case group — neither anti- nor pro-幕府ばくふ in the Boshin classification. This is consistent with the deeper claim that the viscount→baron downgrades are about pre-Restoration institutional status rather than Boshin alignment.

20.9.4.8 矢島藩やしまはん — 生駒家いこまけ, late 旗本はたもと elevation with active Boshin role

Family 生駒家いこまけ (Ikoma)
1884 recipient 生駒親敬いこまちかゆき
石高こくだか (Hansei Ichiran 草高くさだか) 16,331 koku
Genmai 6,228 koku
Genmai-rule expectation viscount
1884 outcome baron (男爵だんしゃく)
Boshin role Anti-幕府ばくふ — OREP member who defected to the imperial side during the war
Why baron 旗本はたもと-equivalent → 1万石まんごく elevation only in 1869

The most institutionally interesting case in this 8-group. The Ikoma had been 大名だいみょう of Sanuki-Takamatsu (讃岐高松藩さぬきたかまつはん, ~17 万石まんごく) until the 1640 御家騒動ごけそうどう (succession scandal), after which the main domain was confiscated and the family reduced to a small Dewa (modern Akita) fief at 8,000 koku — 旗本はたもと-equivalent status. They held this position for over two centuries.

In the 戊辰戦争ぼしんせんそう (Boshin sensō) the Ikoma joined the Northern Alliance (奥羽越列藩同盟おううえつれっぱんどうめい) but defected to the imperial side mid-war — one of the OREP defectors covered in chapter 02g. The new government rewarded the defection by elevating them back to 10,000-koku 大名だいみょう status in 1869. Despite this active anti-幕府ばくふ service, however, the 1884 華族かぞく-rei still placed them at baron under the post-1868-elevation provision. The Ikoma case is therefore especially diagnostic: it shows the baron downgrade was structural (about pre-Restoration 大名だいみょう status) rather than discretionary (Boshin reward or punishment).

Methodological hook: 浅見雅男あさみまさお『華族誕生かぞくたんじょう 名誉と体面の明治めいよとたいめんのめいじ』リブロポートりぶろぽーと (1994), pp. 87–88 explicitly articulates the 現米げんまい threshold rule. Lebra (1993) uses the 石高こくだか framing. The dataset stores both deviation values side by side — the 石高こくだか-based (Lebra) variant and the 現米げんまい-based (Asami) variant — so the paper can present whichever baseline the analysis calls for.
