18  The bargain of 1871

On 29 August 1871 (Meiji 4/7/14) the emperor abolished the domains. The edict was read aloud, the governors were dismissed, and the political map of Japan was redrawn in an afternoon. But the edict was only the surface of something that had been assembled over the preceding year — by a small circle of architects inside the government, with the leading domains volunteering rather than being overrun. This chapter follows how the deal was built, and closes on the question David Crego’s notes put at the center of the paper: if four domains had the power to do this, why didn’t they simply become a new shogunate?

18.1 Domains volunteering, before the decree

The standard picture of 1871 is top-down: the government in Tokyo decides, and the domains submit. The contemporary record complicates that picture. Through late 1870 and into 1871, domains were already coming to the central government on their own — asking it to take over.

The official daily record (Daijōkan nisshi 太政官日誌) shows the pattern. Domains petitioned to have their revenues reallocated, to borrow from the treasury, or — in a striking number of cases — to dissolve themselves into prefectures voluntarily. The project’s reading of the 1870–71 record finds on the order of two dozen reallocation petitions plus nine voluntary liquidations, beginning with a Kumamoto petition in the autumn of 1870 and accelerating through the summer of 1871. The petitioners were mostly not the four leading domains; they were small and middling houses across the country, signaling that they could no longer carry themselves.

This is the bottom of the bargain: by 1871 a large part of the daimyō (大名) class was not defending its sovereignty but trying to hand it back on survivable terms.

18.2 The architects

The top of the bargain was a small group inside the government. By their own and each other’s accounts, the central figures were:

  • Kido Takayoshi (木戸孝允) — from Chōshū, the chief political architect of the abolition
  • Ōkubo Toshimichi (大久保利通) — from Satsuma, his operational counterpart
  • Inoue Kaoru (井上馨) — from Chōshū, in the Finance Ministry, working the implementation
  • Iwakura Tomomi (岩倉具視) — the senior court noble who had to sign off

The four leading daimyō — Shimazu Tadayoshi (Satsuma), Mōri Motonori (Chōshū), Nabeshima Naohiro (Saga), and Yamauchi Toyonori (Tosa) — supplied the formal cover by petitioning for the reform; the imperial edict would name exactly these four as having “first proposed” the return of the registers.

Kido’s diary is unusually candid about how the policy was actually pushed. He claims the idea of a county-prefecture system (gunken-sei 郡県制) as his own, and describes the argument he used to make confiscating the domains legitimate:

I contrived a stratagem by saying that the fiefs of the lords had been granted to them by the Tokugawa, an Imperial enemy. They had never been authorized under the Imperial seal; thus, I asked, how can the Empire stand unless it is made very clear that one’s highest loyalty is to the Emperor?

— Kido Takayoshi, diary, 29 August 1871 (trans. Brown & Hirota)

This is the legal-moral hinge of the whole settlement: the lords’ holdings were reframed as never having been legitimate property in the first place, since they came from a defeated enemy. That framing lowered what the state had to offer in exchange — the daimyō were not being bought out of secure property so much as compensated for surrendering claims whose legitimacy was now denied. Kido also notes the personal risk: through 1871 he heard “the misgivings and vilification even from samurai who were my fellow clansmen,” and many “advocated assassinating me.” This was contested, dangerous work, not a formality.

18.3 The decree week

The corrected chronology shows how compressed the final move was. The cabinet reshuffle that put the coalition in place happened around 10 August 1871; the decree came nineteen days later. The decisive week was three days:

  • 27 August — Saigō Takamori, Ōkubo, and Kido held a secret conference to settle the plan, then took it for ratification through two separate channels: Saigō and Kido to Prince Sanjō, Ōkubo and Kido to Lord Iwakura. Kido insisted on including Iwakura over an argument to keep him in the dark:

    Originally the argument was advanced that we should not tell Lord Iwakura beforehand. As Lord Iwakura has been connected with all important matters since the time of the Restoration, I was reluctant not to tell him.

  • 28 August — the proclamation was drafted in secret by the imperial secretaries.

  • 29 August — at 2 p.m. in the Grand Hall, the edict was read to the governors of fifty-six in-Tokyo domains, who were dismissed from their offices on the spot.

  • 30 August — the senior councilors of the remaining ~260 domains were summoned and notified.

The ceremony itself encoded the coalition’s shape. The imperial messages came in two tiers, which Kido records plainly:

An Imperial message to our Lord Governor of Chōshū, and to the Lords Shimazu, Yamanouchi, and Nabeshima stated that the abolition of the domains would be proclaimed today. As the Governor of Nagoya, the Governors Ikeda, Hosokawa, and Hachisuka, have submitted petitions requesting the reform, there were Imperial messages especially for them.

The first tier is the four leading domains — Satsuma, Chōshū, Tosa, Saga — credited for proposing the 1869 return of registers. The second tier adds four more houses (Owari, Tottori, Kumamoto, Tokushima) credited for helping build the new system. Eight domains, spanning the full status spectrum from a senior Tokugawa-related house to major outside lords, were publicly thanked — a wider circle than the famous four.

And at the human center of it is a petition-and-response moment in Kido’s own words. The Chōshū lord, Mōri Motonori — to whom Kido felt a personal debt — had petitioned the throne for the reform days earlier and now prostrated himself among the dismissed governors:

a man to whom my obligations are deeper than the seas and higher than the mountains, prostrated himself as one of the fifty-six domain governors, and listened to the Imperial Edict. At this I choked up… and my tears fell in spite of myself… For this purpose he submitted his petition to the Throne a few days ago. His act made me feel that I should make every effort to have it accepted.

The lord petitions; the architect works to get the petition accepted. That is the bargaining channel running in both directions at once, recorded by the man who built it.

18.4 Why not a four-domain shogunate?

The four leading domains had the military strength to centralize on their own terms. The natural worry — David’s question — is why that strength didn’t simply produce a new hegemony, with Sat-Chō-Tosa-Hizen replacing the Tokugawa. The contemporary record points to four reasons the coalition could not, and did not, entrench:

  1. They could not act unilaterally. Even the three core architects had to route the decision for formal ratification through both Sanjō and Iwakura on 27 August. Approval through the broader court was a procedural requirement, not a courtesy.

  2. They disagreed among themselves. The architects had to negotiate their own differences — over the balance of the new cabinet, and over timing; Saigō, in particular, was the most cautious about moving when they did. A coalition that has to bargain internally cannot dictate externally.

  3. The deal was broad by design. The bargaining channel reached dozens of non-focal domains through the petition record, the public commendation extended to eight houses, and the later peerage settlement would fold in non-focal elites as well (see The settlement). Buying in that many players is the opposite of monopolizing.

  4. The outcome was contingent. The government had nearly settled for making the daimyō hereditary prefecture governors — a far weaker reform that would have left their local power intact. Kido fought it:

    the Imperial Government reached an agreement to appoint the feudal lords as hereditary governors of the domains. In consequence, I argued as strongly as possible against that arrangement, and finally we eliminated the two characters for ‘hereditary.’

    That a single word had to be struck out at the end shows how un-predetermined the result was.

The paper reads these four facts through the Political Coase Theorem: the same factional rivalry that made the coalition strong enough to threaten also made it unable to dominate, so commitment had to be purchased with shared institutions rather than imposed by force. That framework is the paper’s contribution; the record on its own simply shows a coalition that could not move without bringing others along.

What it bought the others is the subject of the next chapter.
