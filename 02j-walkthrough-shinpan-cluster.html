12  Walkthrough: The 29 shinpan (Tokugawa cadet) houses

The shinpan (親藩しんぱん) category (29 domains, ~10% of the dataset) is the smallest of the three major Tokugawa-era classifications but the most internally structured. Almost every shinpan traces back, through fewer than ten generations, to either Tokugawa Ieyasu himself or to a small set of Matsudaira branches related to him. This chapter walks the cluster by lineage: the three big shogunal-family houses (gosanke (御三家ごさんけ)), the Tokugawa main line after 1868 (Shizuoka), and the major Matsudaira sub-clades — Echizen-Matsudaira, Hisamatsu-Matsudaira, and Mito-Matsudaira.

ImportantWhy this chapter, given that shinpan are already mentioned everywhere

The shinpan concept is introduced in chapter 03c. This chapter is the concrete-narrative companion: it walks the 29 houses domain-by-domain, organised by lineage, so the reader can see how the structural category actually decomposes into specific Matsudaira branches and what each branch’s institutional posture and 1884 outcome looked like.

The Aizu cluster (会津藩あいづはん / 斗南藩となみはん — also shinpan via the Aizu-Matsudaira branch) is covered in detail in chapter 02f and not re-narrated here.

12.1 The 29, in distribution

By 1884 kazoku (華族かぞく) rank (family rank, indexed to the domain that family ruled in 1870):

1884 rank shinpan count Examples
Prince (公爵こうしゃく) 1 Shizuoka (Tokugawa main)
Marquess (侯爵こうしゃく) 4 Owari, Wakayama (Kii), Mito, Fukui
Count (伯爵はくしゃく) 4 Matsue, Maebashi, Iyo-Matsuyama, Takamatsu
Viscount (子爵ししゃく) 20 Tsuyama, Akashi, Shimabara, Kuwana, Imabari, Tsuruta, etc.
Baron (男爵だんしゃく) 0 —
Total 29

Note the floor: no shinpan house received baron. The 1884 rule effectively guaranteed shinpan a minimum of viscount, regardless of how small their genmai (現米げんまい) had become. The smallest shinpan in the dataset (谷地藩やちはん, kokudaka (石高こくだか) unrecorded; 母里藩もりはん at 10,000 koku; 清崎藩きよさきはん at 10,000 koku) still received viscount.

By Boshin alignment:

Boshin shinpan count
Anti-bakufu (幕府ばくふ) / pro-restoration 21
Pro-bakufu 6
Neutral 2

The 21 “anti-bakufu” shinpan are mostly end-state classifications: Tokugawa-cadet branches that complied with the post-Toba-Fushimi imperial directive without active resistance. The 6 actively-pro-bakufu shinpan are the cases where the lord stayed loyal to the bakufu through the Boshin War (Boshin sensō) — most prominently Aizu (covered in chapter 02f), Kuwana, Shizuoka (post-relocation), and a few of the Mito-Matsudaira branches.

12.2 The gosanke — Owari, Kii, Mito

The three flagship Tokugawa-cadet houses, established in the early 17th century by three of Ieyasu’s sons. By 1884 all three received marquess rank, two through the standard rule (Owari, Kii) and one through the explicit gosanke clause (Mito).

12.2.1 尾張藩おわりはん (Owari) — Nagoya, the senior gosanke house

Family 尾張徳川家おわりとくがわけ
1884 recipient 徳川義勝とくがわよしかつ (later 徳川義礼とくがわよしのり)
Province 尾張国おわりのくに (modern Aichi prefecture, Nagoya area)
kokudaka (Hansei Ichiran sōdaka (草高くさだか)) 853,115 koku
Genmai 327,468 koku
1884 outcome marquess (侯爵こうしゃく)
Boshin role Anti-bakufu — Owari defected to the imperial side under Tokugawa Yoshikatsu’s leadership

Owari was founded by Tokugawa Yoshinao (徳川義直とくがわよしなお), Ieyasu’s ninth son. It was the senior of the three gosanke and the largest with about 619,500 koku of true holding (plus shinden = 853k full kokudaka). The Owari-Tokugawa anchored bakufu legitimacy in central Japan and held the senior place in court protocol among the cadet branches.

The Boshin alignment is striking: a Tokugawa cadet of this seniority might have been expected to defend the bakufu. Instead Tokugawa Yoshikatsu (徳川義勝とくがわよしかつ, then head of the house) led Owari into the imperial coalition in January 1868, and Owari forces participated in the northern campaigns against the Northern Alliance. This defection mattered for the imperial cause both militarily (Owari had ~1,500 troops + Western-style cannon) and symbolically (a sanke house repudiating the bakufu).

The marquess rank in 1884 was the standard genmai-rule outcome (327k genmai is comfortably in marquess range); the gosanke clause didn’t need to fire for Owari.

12.2.2 和歌山藩わかやまはん (Wakayama / Kii) — the second gosanke house

Family 紀州徳川家きしゅうとくがわけ
1884 recipient 徳川茂承とくがわもちつぐ
Province 紀伊国きいのくに (modern Wakayama prefecture)
kokudaka (Hansei Ichiran sōdaka) 539,469 koku
Genmai 279,151 koku
1884 outcome marquess (侯爵こうしゃく)
Boshin role Anti-bakufu (late-Restoration compliance)

Kii was founded by Tokugawa Yorinobu (徳川頼宣とくがわよりのぶ), Ieyasu’s tenth son. Kii is historically important as the cadet branch that produced the 8th shōgun (将軍しょうぐん) Yoshimune (1716–1745) when the main Tokugawa line failed — establishing that the gosanke could supply a shōgun.

By the bakumatsu period Kii’s main political importance was structural (as a shōgun-reserve), and the family followed the late-Restoration imperial-consolidation path. The Kii 付家老つけがろう houses (Mizuno of 新宮しんぐう and Andō of 田辺たなべ) were detached at the Restoration; both received baron rank in 1884 under the post-1868-elevation rule (see chapter 07 §D).

The 1884 marquess rank was the standard outcome (genmai 279k clears the marquess threshold).

12.2.3 水戸藩みとはん (Mito) — the junior gosanke house, intellectually outsized

Family 水戸徳川家みととくがわけ
1884 recipient 徳川篤敬とくがわあつよし
Province 常陸国ひたちのくに (modern Ibaraki prefecture)
kokudaka (Hansei Ichiran gōdaka (合高ごうだか)) 306,776 koku
Genmai 58,553 koku
1884 outcome marquess (侯爵こうしゃく) (via gosanke clause)
Boshin role Pro-bakufu by family-affiliation (Mito’s internal politics were violently divided)

Mito was founded by Tokugawa Yorifusa (徳川頼房とくがわよりふさ), Ieyasu’s eleventh son. Smaller than Owari and Kii (about 280,000 koku formal kokudaka with 領内りょうない increments), but disproportionately important politically and intellectually.

Mito is famous for mitogaku (水戸学みとがく) — the Confucian-historical school that, by the 19th century, was producing strident loyalist-imperialist ideology even though Mito was itself a Tokugawa-cadet branch. The contradiction (Tokugawa-blood family producing the intellectual foundations for the Restoration) destabilised Mito politics throughout the 1860s. The 天狗党の乱てんぐとうのらん (Tengutō Rebellion, 1864) was a Mito-internal civil war between the radical-loyalist 天狗党てんぐとう faction and the bakufu-loyalist 諸生党しょせいとう faction. Casualties were severe and the rebellion took years to settle.

The Mito branch also produced the 15th shōgun Yoshinobu (徳川慶喜とくがわよしのぶ), who was Mito-born before being adopted into Hitotsubashi and then becoming shōgun in 1866. Yoshinobu’s resignation in 1867 and the bakufu’s military defeat in 1868 closed the cycle.

In 1884 Mito’s genmai of 58,553 koku put it in count range — but the gosanke clause of the kazoku-rei elevated it to marquess. This is the unique case where the gosanke clause actually fired (Owari and Kii cleared the marquess threshold on the standard rule). The deviation is documented in chapter 07 §B.

12.3 The Tokugawa main line — 静岡藩しずおかはん (Shizuoka)

Family 徳川家とくがわけ (main line)
1884 recipient 徳川家達とくがわいえさと
Province 駿河するが / 遠江とおとうみ / 三河みかわ (modern Shizuoka prefecture and surrounds)
kokudaka (Hansei Ichiran omotedaka (表高おもてだか)) 699,182 koku
Genmai 209,990 koku
1884 outcome prince (公爵こうしゃく) — Tokugawa main-line exception
Boshin role Pro-bakufu (this is the former bakufu)

After the bakufu’s military defeat in 1868, the Tokugawa main line was stripped of its Edo-region holdings (formerly some 4 million koku of tenryō plus the shogunal domains) and relocated to a much-reduced 700,000-koku Sunpu (modern Shizuoka) territory. The relocation made the main Tokugawa line, for the first time in 250 years, a daimyō (大名だいみょう) in the conventional sense — they had been the suzerain of all daimyō, not one of them.

The 1884 prince rank applied an explicit “Tokugawa main-line clause” in the kazoku-rei, separate from both the sanke clause (which applied to Owari/Kii/Mito) and the Restoration-leader clause (which applied to Shimazu/Mōri). 徳川家達とくがわいえさと (1863–1940), great-grandson of the 11th shōgun Ienari, was the recipient. This is the methodological centrepiece for the paper’s claim that the 1884 ennoblements ranked by what the rules said, not by who won the war: the defeated central party received the highest rank.

See chapter 07 §A for the full Shizuoka deviation block.

12.4 The Echizen-Matsudaira lineage (越前えちぜん松平まつだいら)

Descended from Ieyasu’s second son 結城秀康ゆうきひでやす (Yūki Hideyasu, 1574–1607), who was adopted out to the Yūki family and later resumed the Matsudaira name. The Echizen-Matsudaira branches multiplied through the Edo period and account for about a third of all shinpan houses in the dataset. Fukui is the senior branch.

Domain Branch kokudaka 1884 outcome Boshin
福井藩ふくいはん (Fukui) Senior — Yūki Hideyasu’s main line 336,194 count 1884 → marquess 1888 anti-bakufu
松江藩まつえはん (Matsue) Cadet from Fukui in 1638 245,341 count anti-bakufu
前橋藩まえばしはん (Maebashi) Cadet from Fukui (川越かわごえ-Maebashi rotation) 217,790 count anti-bakufu
津山藩つやまはん (Tsuyama) Cadet (1697 fudai (譜代ふだい)-of-shinpan status) 104,577 viscount anti-bakufu
明石藩あかしはん (Akashi) Cadet from Fukui 93,341 viscount anti-bakufu
広瀬藩ひろせはん (Hirose) Matsue cadet 33,128 viscount anti-bakufu
母里藩もりはん (Mori) Matsue cadet (10,000 koku) 10,000 viscount anti-bakufu

The Echizen-Matsudaira cluster shows two patterns:

  1. Universal anti-bakufu alignment in the data — the entire branch went over to the imperial side. The key actor was 松平春嶽まつだいらしゅんがく (松平慶永まつだいらよしなが, 1828–1890), the lord of Fukui, who was a 四賢侯しけんこう figure and led the public-deliberation (公議政体派こうぎせいたいは) faction. His personal influence drew the satellite branches into the imperial coalition.
  2. Rank determined by genmai size, not lineage seniority — Fukui’s 1884 count rank (later promoted to marquess in 1888 for Shungaku’s Restoration role) was the standard outcome. Matsue and Maebashi both made count on revenue alone; Tsuyama and below stayed at viscount.

Fukui is covered in detail in chapter 07 §B as one of the three count→marquess upgrades.

12.5 The Hisamatsu-Matsudaira lineage (久松ひさまつ松平まつだいら)

A separate Matsudaira branch descended from Sadakatsu (久松ひさまつ定勝さだかつ, 1560–1624), who was Ieyasu’s half-brother through his mother — Lady Ōdai (於大おだいの方) remarried Hisamatsu Toshikatsu after divorcing Matsudaira Hirotada, and her son by the second marriage was given the Matsudaira name and shinpan treatment.

The Hisamatsu-Matsudaira branches are concentrated in Iyo (modern Ehime) and one domain in Mie (Kuwana):

Domain Province kokudaka 1884 outcome Boshin
伊予松山藩いよまつやまはん (Iyo-Matsuyama) Iyo 157,185 count pro-bakufu
桑名藩くわなはん (Kuwana) Ise 65,631 viscount pro-bakufu
今治藩いまばりはん (Imabari) Iyo 43,235 viscount anti-bakufu

The Hisamatsu cluster is more divided on Boshin than the Echizen one: Iyo-Matsuyama and Kuwana stayed pro-bakufu (Kuwana’s lord 松平定敬まつだいらさだあき was the brother of Aizu’s 松平容保まつだいらかたもり and a senior bakufu defender), while Imabari followed the imperial directive.

TipKuwana reclassification

桑名藩くわなはん was originally coded fudai in some popular sources but is treated here as shinpan. The reasoning: Kuwana was held by the Hisamatsu-Matsudaira branch from 1710 until the Restoration; since the other two Hisamatsu-Matsudaira domains (伊予松山いよまつやま, 今治いまばり) are already coded shinpan, consistency requires Kuwana also be shinpan.

The Hisamatsu-Matsudaira are sometimes ambiguously classified in popular references because the founder Sadakatsu was Ieyasu’s half-brother (not a direct son), and because the lineage took a Matsudaira surname rather than the Tokugawa one. Academic sources (児玉幸多こだまこうた ed.『藩史総覧はんしそうらん (Hanshi sōran, “Comprehensive Survey of Han History”)』1977; 中川なかがわ 学『松平まつだいら氏由緒書ゆいしょがき (Matsudaira-shi yuishogaki, “Matsudaira Lineage Records”)』1989) consistently treat them as shinpan.

See the methodology note in chapter 03c for the full context.

12.6 The Mito-Matsudaira cadet branches

Mito’s own cadet branches form their own shinpan sub-cluster — smaller domains in the eastern Kanto and on Shikoku, all genealogically tied to the Mito-Tokugawa house. By 1884 these branches typically received viscount, with one count exception (Takamatsu).

Domain Branch kokudaka 1884 outcome Boshin
高松藩たかまつはん (Takamatsu) 1640 cadet from Mito → independent shinpan 120,000 count anti-bakufu
守山藩もりやまはん (Moriyama) Mito cadet 29,320 viscount anti-bakufu
石岡藩いしおかはん (Ishioka, formerly 府中ふちゅう藩はん) Mito cadet 29,070 viscount anti-bakufu
宍戸藩ししどはん (Shishido) Mito cadet 15,000 viscount neutral
清崎藩きよさきはん (Kiyozaki) Mito-Echizen junction branch 10,000 viscount neutral
鶴田藩つるたはん (Tsuruta) Late-Edo Mito branch 36,190 viscount pro-bakufu

Takamatsu (Shikoku, 120,000 koku) is the largest Mito-cadet and was held by 松平頼聰まつだいらよりとし at the Restoration. The Takamatsu line had been pro-bakufu by family-affiliation but switched to the imperial side after the Toba-Fushimi disturbance — Takamatsu castle initially flew the bakufu colours, briefly resisted the imperial advance, then surrendered. The 1884 count rank is the standard genmai-rule outcome (genmai 76,857 falls in count range).

The smaller Mito-cadets ranged across positions in the Boshin War (戊辰戦争ぼしんせんそう): most followed the imperial directive after Toba-Fushimi, but Tsuruta was actively pro-bakufu and Shishido / Kiyozaki stayed neutral.

12.7 Other Matsudaira branches

Two smaller branches contribute single shinpan domains:

Family 深溝松平家ふこうずまつだいらけ (Fukamizō-Matsudaira)

12.8 The Aizu cluster — cross-link only

The shinpan lineage most prominently associated with the Boshin War — the Aizu-Matsudaira (会津あいづ松平まつだいら) — is covered in detail in chapter 02f: Aizu and Sendai. The bakumatsu-era Aizu lord 松平容保まつだいらかたもり was Kyoto Shoshidai under the bakufu’s final political reorganisation and led the pro-bakufu Northern Alliance after Toba-Fushimi. After Aizu’s defeat in 1868 the family was relocated to 斗南藩となみはん (3 万石まんごく) in northern Honshu and received only viscount rank in 1884 — at the time framed as a Boshin punishment, but per chapter 07 understood now as a mechanical genmai-rule outcome (post-Boshin genmai of 7,380 koku is squarely in viscount range).

12.9 What the shinpan cluster teaches

Three points:

  1. The lineage structure is dense and traceable. Almost every shinpan traces back through documented branch points to either Ieyasu, the gosanke, or one of the major Matsudaira branches (Echizen, Hisamatsu, Mito, Fukamizō, Yoshii). The category is not just an administrative label — it tracks an actual genealogical structure.
  2. The 1884 ennoblement effectively floored shinpan at viscount, ensuring no Tokugawa-cadet family fell below the daimyō-rank tier. Combined with the standard genmai rule, the result was a compressed rank distribution across shinpan: 1 prince, 4 marquess, 4 count, 20 viscount, 0 baron.
  3. Boshin alignment within shinpan was lineage-segregated. The Echizen-Matsudaira branches were uniformly anti-bakufu (driven by Shungaku of Fukui); the Hisamatsu-Matsudaira branches divided (Iyo-Matsuyama + Kuwana pro-bakufu; Imabari anti-bakufu); the Mito-Matsudaira branches divided (most anti, Tsuruta pro, Shishido/Kiyozaki neutral); the Aizu-Matsudaira lineage went down with the bakufu. The category-level “shinpan = pro-bakufu” intuition is wrong in the Boshin data: 21 of 29 are coded anti-bakufu.
