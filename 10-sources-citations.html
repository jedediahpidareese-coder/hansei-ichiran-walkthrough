21  Sources and citations

This chapter documents the citation chain behind every variable in the analytic dataset: what each source is, what variables it backs, and the form in which it should be cited.

ImportantThe sourcing constraint

The dataset cites 藩制一覧 (Hansei Ichiran, “Overview of Han Institutions”) and published academic sources only. Two ancillary materials are used as finders and cross-validators but are never cited:

  • Japanese Wikipedia — used to locate primary-source candidates and to cross-check disputed cases. When a Wikipedia article references 明治史要 (Meiji Shiyō, “Outline of Meiji History”) or 浅見 1994, that underlying source is what gets cited.
  • A bakumatsu-era domain-listing website — used to seed initial values for domain_category and last_lord. Retained as an internal cross-validation layer only.

Any value that entered the dataset through one of those two channels has been re-cited to a published source before being used in any analytic claim.

21.1 The three source families

The dataset draws on three structurally different families of sources, each with its own role and citation pattern.

21.1.1 Family 1 — Hansei Ichiran 1870 (the primary record)

The book itself: 藩制一覧 (Hansei Ichiran), 1870, two volumes, surveyed and compiled by the early Meiji government. Every hi_* column in the analytic dataset derives from this source, traced through per-domain extracted chunks.

Citation form: Hansei Ichiran (藩制一覧), 1870, vol. N, table N, page N. Compiled by the Meiji-government survey of daimyō* (大名) administrative returns.*

The exact page reference for any Hansei Ichiran-derived value is preserved in the dataset’s master extraction record (one row per per-domain per-field extraction, with the source crop / page noted). When a paper table cites a specific Hansei Ichiran number, this row-level audit trail establishes precisely where in the original document the value lives.

Volume contents:

  • Volume 1: each domain’s fiscal and demographic data — kokudaka (石高) in its several variants, tax receipts, population, household counts.
  • Volume 2: each domain’s military and administrative data — troop strength, warships, officer rosters. Saga (佐嘉藩) is documented as a gap in Volume 2’s military rosters; the dataset substitutes 藤野保 (2010) as a published scholarly substitute for Saga’s military information.

21.1.2 Family 2 — Government primary documents (paper-citable)

Three contemporaneous government compilations that overlap with Hansei Ichiran on population and revenue, plus the official ennoblement gazettes:

21.1.2.1 明治史要 (Meiji Shiyō)

Compiled by the 修史局 (Shūshikyoku, “Bureau of Historiography”). The standard early-Meiji compendium of administrative data: domain populations, household counts, kokudaka, basic fiscal indicators. Backs the population_meiji_shiyo and households_meiji_shiyo columns.

Citation form: 明治史要 (Meiji Shiyō), Bureau of Historiography (修史局), early Meiji compendium.

21.1.2.2 大隈重信関係資料 (Ōkuma Shigenobu kankei shiryō, “Ōkuma Shigenobu Papers”)「府藩県石高人口表 (Fu-han-ken kokudaka jinkō-hyō, “Prefecture-Domain-County Kokudaka and Population Table”)」

Held in the Ōkuma Shigenobu papers (大隈重信関係資料) at the Waseda University Library, call number イ14-A4361. The “府藩県石高人口表” is a contemporary tabulation of administrative-unit kokudaka and population. Backs the population_okuma and uchidaka_okuma_koku columns.

📄 Download the scanned manuscript (1.7 MB PDF) — Waseda University Library digital archive, freely accessible.

Citation form: 「府藩県石高人口表」, 大隈重信関係資料, Ōkuma Shigenobu papers, Waseda University Library archival collection (call number イ14-A4361).

21.1.2.3 旧高旧領取調帳 (Kyūdaka kyūryō torishirabechō, “Register of Former Kokudaka and Former Fief-Holders”), 1872 Meiji cadastral survey

The early Meiji government’s per-village cadastral survey, compiled 1872 from the village-level land-yield rolls (kokudaka) that the daimyō domains had been administering pre-haihan-chiken. The “旧” (“former”) in the title refers to the pre-1869 Edo-period feudal arrangement: each village entry records (a) its old province (旧国), district (旧郡), village (旧村), and lord (旧領); (b) its rated kokudaka (旧高); and (c) the 1872 Meiji prefecture (旧県) administering it post-haihan.

Hosted as a free open-access searchable database by the National Museum of Japanese History (国立歴史民俗博物館 / Rekihaku):

📄 旧高旧領取調帳データベース (Rekihaku) — freely accessible; query by 旧領名 / 旧国 / 旧郡 / 旧村.

Backs the rekihaku_kyudaka_iv_koku column, which feeds the composite kokudaka_iv as Tier 4 for the 13 domains absent from HI vol 1’s kokudaka coverage (仙台, 新発田, 杵築, 母里, 清崎, 足利, 須坂, 岩村, 対馬府中, 岩国, 岩崎, 松岡, 斗南). The Rekihaku 旧高 is a kusadaka-tier metric (rated land yield from village rolls) — the same conceptual tier as HI 草高 / 合高 — restoring methodological consistency to the IV across all 286 rows. See CORRECTIONS_LOG.md Section K for the full audit.

For 仙台藩 specifically the IV uses only the post-Boshin retained territory (仙台県 + 宮城県 + distant 常陸/下総 hatamoto enclaves) = 299,659 koku, EXCLUDING the four 1869 carve-off prefectures (登米県 + 胆沢県 + 江刺県 + 角田県 = 674,350 koku) that were no longer Sendai-Date revenue at the moment of haihan-chiken.

Citation form: 旧高旧領取調帳 (1872 Meiji cadastral survey), per 国立歴史民俗博物館 旧高旧領取調帳データベース (https://www.rekihaku.ac.jp/up-cgi/login.pl?p=param/kyud/db_param), accessed 2026-05-24.

21.1.2.4 統計集誌 (Tōkei Shūshi, “Statistical Journal”) “人員表 (Jin’in-hyō,”Personnel Table”)”

Compiled by 呉文聡 from 野村文夫’s materials, published in Tōkei Shūshi (the early-Meiji statistical journal). Backs the population_tokei_shushi column.

Citation form: 統計集誌 (Tōkei Shūshi), 人員表, compiled by 呉文聡 from 野村文夫 materials.

21.1.2.5 官報 (Kanpō, “Official Gazette”) 第307号 (1884-07-08) — primary ennoblement gazette (daimyō / sekke (摂家) cohort)

The government gazette that recorded the 1884-07-07 ennoblement decree — the original 公侯伯子男 (prince / marquess / count / viscount / baron) rank assignments. This is the primary record underlying every kazoku_rank_actual value, with a small number of late-cohort exceptions handled by Kanpō #308.

📄 View the gazette (NDL Digital Collections, pid 2943511, 9 pages) — freely accessible, Public Domain Mark.

Citation form: 官報 第307号 (1884-07-08), NDL pid 2943511.

21.1.2.6 官報 第308号 (1884-07-09) — primary ennoblement gazette (late cohort)

The follow-up gazette for daimyō whose rank assignments lagged the main decree by one day. Backs a small number of kazoku_rank_actual rows including 半原藩 and several other edge cases.

📄 View the gazette (NDL Digital Collections, pid 2943512, 9 pages) — freely accessible, Public Domain Mark.

Citation form: 官報 第308号 (1884-07-09), NDL Digital Collections (pid 2943512).

21.1.3 Family 3 — Scholarly synthesis (paper-citable)

Secondary scholarly works treated not as the original record but as the synthesis-and-interpretation layer between the primary documents and the dataset’s coded values.

21.1.3.1 浅見雅男『華族誕生 名誉と体面の明治 (Kazoku tanjō: meiyo to taimen no Meiji, “Birth of the Peerage: Honor and Reputation in Meiji”)』(リブロポート 1994)

The single most-important scholarly source for the dataset’s central kazoku (華族)-rank variables. Asami’s work:

  1. Reconstructs the 1884 kazoku-rei threshold rule with sufficient detail that the genmai (現米)-based mechanical-expected rank (kazoku_rank_expected_genmai) can be computed for every domain.
  2. Establishes genmai — not sōdaka (草高) — as the threshold numerator. Pre-Asami sources (including Lebra 1993) implicitly used kokudaka as the threshold; Asami shows the rule explicitly operated on post-1869 genmai. This single point reduces the apparent “Boshin penalty” sample from 93 cases (Lebra framing) to 19 (Asami framing) by mechanically explaining 76 outcomes. The Sendai and Aizu cases that look like Restoration punishments turn out to be genmai-mechanical (see chapter 07).
  3. Documents the explicit exception clauses (gosanke clause, Tokugawa-main-line clause, Restoration-leader clause, sankyō (御三卿) clause, post-1868-elevation clause) — each of which explains a specific subset of deviations from the standard rule.

Pages 87–88 articulate the genmai rule; pages 109–110 give the gosanke and sankyō clauses; pages 105–115 the Restoration-leader exception. Asami is the citation in 94 percent of kazoku_rank_source rows.

Citation form: 浅見雅男『華族誕生 名誉と体面の明治』リブロポート (1994).

21.1.3.2 Beasley, W.G. The Meiji Restoration. Stanford University Press (1972)

The standard English-language synthesis of the Restoration period. Used for:

  • Domain classifications — Beasley p. xii lists “Principal Domains in 1860” with explicit shinpan (親藩) / fudai (譜代) / tozama designations; this is the cross-check used to confirm 仙台 (Date) and 対馬府中 (Sō) as tozama (外様).
  • Boshin alignment — Beasley ch. XI pp. 295–299 provides the standard chronology of Toba-Fushimi and the subsequent imperial consolidation. The bulk boshin_alignment_source entries for roughly 212 small viscount-tier domains cite Beasley.
  • Hikone defection — Beasley p. 297 is the only English-language source that explicitly names Hikone as the pre-Toba-Fushimi defector (the Ii family broke with the bakufu (幕府) after Ii Naosuke’s assassination in 1860 and the subsequent Sakuradamon Incident).

Citation form: Beasley, W. G. The Meiji Restoration. Stanford University Press (1972).

21.1.3.3 Esposito, Gabriele. Japanese Armies 1868–1877: The Boshin War and Satsuma Rebellion. Osprey Men-at-Arms 530 (2020)

A military-history reference with detailed coverage of the Boshin War (戊辰戦争) combatants. Used for Boshin alignment citations on the major-domain combatants (the focal four plus the Northern Alliance core: Aizu / 斗南, Sendai, Yonezawa, Shōnai, Nagaoka, Nihonmatsu, Morioka, Kuwana). Backs ten boshin_alignment_source rows.

The volume is in the project workspace. Page guide for Boshin-alignment claims:

  • pp. 5–7 — Conflicts with Chōshū 1863–66 + European military missions
  • pp. 8–11 — Boshin War narrative: Operations January–July 1868 (Toba-Fushimi, Sat-Chō-Tosa imperial alliance), Northern Alliance May–October 1868, Republic of Ezo October 1868–June 1869
  • p. 22 — South-western forces (Satsuma + Chōshū modernisation)
  • pp. 23–28 — Shogunate forces, Denshutai, police, Aizu forces, Hokkaido 1869
  • pp. 32–33 — Chōshū forces (Kiheitai etc.)

Citation form: Esposito, Gabriele. Japanese Armies 1868–1877: The Boshin War and Satsuma Rebellion. Osprey Men-at-Arms 530, Osprey Publishing (2020), specific pages as cited.

21.1.3.4 工藤威『奥羽列藩同盟の基礎的研究 (Ōu reppan dōmei no kisoteki kenkyū, “Basic Research on the Northern Alliance”)』(岩田書院 2002)

The standard Japanese-language academic monograph on the 奥羽越列藩同盟 (Ōuetsu Reppan Dōmei) — the Northern Alliance of bakumatsu domains that resisted the imperial advance after Toba-Fushimi. Backs the Boshin alignment citations for the twelve Northern Alliance combatants plus the nine Northern Alliance defectors who switched sides mid-war, and supplied the corrections to several Northern Alliance roster mis-classifications.

The monograph is not in the project workspace and would need to be acquired via interlibrary loan. The roster (the load-bearing content for this dataset) is transcribed in the JP Wikipedia article on 奥羽越列藩同盟, which footnotes back to Kudō’s specific pages — the dataset cites Kudō via the Wikipedia transcription with that as the verification chain.

Citation form: 工藤威『奥羽列藩同盟の基礎的研究』岩田書院 (2002), roster transcribed in JP Wikipedia 奥羽越列藩同盟.

21.1.3.5 児玉幸多 ed. 『藩史総覧 (Hanshi sōran, “Comprehensive Survey of Han History”)』新人物往来社 (1977)

The canonical Japanese-language han-classification compendium. The principal source for domain_category (shinpan / fudai / tozama / sankyō / tenryō (天領)). Establishes formal category designations for all roughly 300 domains.

Citation form: 児玉幸多 編『藩史総覧』新人物往来社 (1977).

21.1.3.6 国史大辞典 (Kokushi daijiten, “Great Dictionary of National History”) (Yoshikawa Kōbunkan, 1979–1997)

The standard Japanese-language reference dictionary. Used as the secondary verification layer for individual house histories, daimyō biographies, and domain administrative changes. Not typically cited per row, but listed in the paper bibliography as the underlying reference framework.

Citation form: 国史大辞典 (Yoshikawa Kōbunkan, 1979–1997 edition).

21.1.3.7 華族大鑑 (Kazoku taikan, “Compendium of the Peerage”)刊行会 編『華族大鑑』(1990)

The dedicated reference for the 1884 prince-rank (公爵) ennoblements specifically — used for the three prince cases in the dataset (Shimazu / Satsuma, Mōri / Chōshū, Tokugawa / Shizuoka) and as a supplementary citation alongside Asami for several marquess cases.

Citation form: 華族大鑑刊行会 編『華族大鑑』(1990).

21.1.3.8 小田部雄次『華族 近代日本貴族の虚像と実像 (Kazoku: kindai Nihon kizoku no kyozō to jitsuzō, “The Peerage: Image and Reality of the Modern Japanese Aristocracy”)』中公新書 (2006)

A modern general-reader-facing book on the kazoku system as a whole. Used for several small viscount-tier kazoku_rank_source rows where the family didn’t appear in Asami’s main tables.

Citation form: 小田部雄次『華族 近代日本貴族の虚像と実像』中公新書 (2006).

21.1.3.9 松田敬之『〈華族爵位〉請願人名辞典 (Kazoku shakui seigan jinmei jiten, “Dictionary of Peerage-Title Petitioners”)』吉川弘文館 (2015)

Definitive on the 1884 exclusions — daimyō families that were not ennobled because of the kaieki (改易, confiscation) or similar disqualifying events. Backs the documented exclusion of 堀江藩 (大沢家, 万石事件 1871) and the verification of 牟原藩 (resolved as an OCR misreading of 半原藩).

Citation form: 松田敬之『〈華族爵位〉請願人名辞典』吉川弘文館 (2015).

21.1.3.10 Mark Ravina. Land and Lordship in Early Modern Japan. Stanford University Press (1999)

Provides the dataset’s ratio variables (kusadaka/commoner, retainers/100 commoners, gendaka (現高)/commoner, retainers/1000 koku, gendaka/kusadaka ratio) from Appendix Table A1 of the book. 227 domains in the dataset have Ravina coverage. One confirmed typo: Ravina A1’s value for Akita 久保田藩 gendaka_per_commoner is 0.049 but the primary-derived value is 0.49 (a decimal-place error). The dataset uses 0.49 (the corrected Ravina value) or the primary-derived 0.457, and flags the discrepancy explicitly.

Citation form: Mark Ravina. Land and Lordship in Early Modern Japan. Stanford University Press (1999), Appendix Table A1.

21.1.3.11 Focal-domain published-source supplements

For each of the four focal domains (Saga, Satsuma, Chōshū, Tosa) a published-source supplement lists citable references for military, political, and institutional details that go beyond what Hansei Ichiran provides:

  • Saga supplement — 藤野保『佐賀藩』(2010); Jentschura 1977 (warships); Beasley 1972
  • Satsuma supplement — Esposito 2020; Jentschura 1977; Beasley 1972; Craig 1961/2000
  • Chōshū supplement — Craig 1961/2000; Jansen 1961/1994
  • Tosa supplement — Roberts 1998; Jansen 1961/1994

Each supplement provides a citation-ready reference block for that focal domain’s narrative section.

21.3 The Boshin alignment citation chain

The boshin_alignment_source column has a hierarchical citation pattern reflecting the difficulty of citing a single source for all 286 domains:

Category Citation pattern Count
Major combatants (10 domains) Esposito 2020 + Beasley 1972 ch. XI (Japanese Wikipedia used as finder only) 10
Northern Alliance combatants (12 domains) 工藤威 2002 + Beasley 1972 p. 298 12
Northern Alliance defectors (9 domains) 工藤威 2002 9
Toba-Fushimi-era defectors (4 domains) Various — Beasley p. 297 (Hikone), 淀藩 individual sources (Yodo), Esposito (Tsu), Beasley + late-mover sources (Kaga) 4
Tokugawa-affiliate, not Northern Alliance (~20 domains) Standard Boshin chronology per Beasley 1972 ch. XI; family-affiliation Tokugawa-loyalist ~20
Anti-bakufu bulk (~212 small viscount-tier) Post-Toba-Fushimi imperial consolidation per Beasley 1972 ch. XI pp. 295–299 ~212
Neutral bulk (~10 domains) Standard Boshin chronology per Beasley 1972 ch. XI ~10
Total 286

The bulk-citation pattern (the 212 anti-bakufu, 20 Tokugawa-affiliate, and 10 neutral domains) uses a single Beasley-1972-chapter-XI reference that frames the standard chronology rather than enumerating each domain individually. Beasley’s analysis is that after Toba-Fushimi the imperial side issued a general directive and most small domains complied without taking an active combatant role. The dataset follows this — coding them anti-bakufu by end-state rather than by Boshin participation.

21.5 Materials used as finders only — never cited

Two ancillary materials are retained for internal validation but never appear as citations.

21.5.1 Japanese Wikipedia (and English Wikipedia)

Used as a finder to:

  • Locate primary-source candidates (when a Wikipedia article cites Meiji Shiyō or 浅見 1994, those underlying sources get verified and cited).
  • Cross-validate values that already have a citable source (when Asami 1994 says marquess and the Japanese Wikipedia article on the family also says marquess, that confirms transcription).
  • Seed initial values for last_lord and provincial-location fields. Those fields are kept only where they have an independent primary citation.

Wikipedia is never the citation; the underlying primary source it references is.

21.5.2 Bakumatsu-era domain-listing website

Used as an internal cross-validation layer for:

  • domain_category initial coding (now fully superseded by 児玉 1977 + Beasley 1972 re-citation).
  • boshin_alignment_group initial coding (now fully superseded by the per-row citation chain documented above).
  • last_lord and provincial fields (retained as cross-checks; would require independent re-citation before any paper inclusion).

This source is never cited. It was retained only because the initial bulk-data scrape was the spine that allowed the dataset to bootstrap. Every paper-relevant field has since been re-cited to an academic source.
