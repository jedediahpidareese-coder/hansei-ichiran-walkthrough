14  1664 → 1871: how stable were domain boundaries?

The maps in this walkthrough use the Harvard CHGIS 1664 daimyo shapefile to draw each domain’s polygon. The dataset itself measures 1870 — six years before haihan-chiken. So there’s a 206-year gap between the polygon vintage and the data vintage. This chapter documents what changed between those dates, what didn’t, and how the project handles each case.

TipWhy this chapter exists

For most domains, boundaries were extremely stable between 1664 and 1870 — the Tokugawa system was deliberately designed to lock in territorial arrangements. But about 30 domains experienced changes (administrative renames, post-Boshin reductions, transfer-relocations) that matter for matching the polygon to the 1870 data. Documenting them makes the maps’ approximations transparent.

14.1 The 1664 baseline

By 1664, sixty-four years after Sekigahara, the Tokugawa daimyo system had largely stabilized. Major redistribution of fiefs (the kunigae / 国替 system, where domains were administratively reassigned to different families) had slowed dramatically from the chaotic 1601–1640 period. The 1664 shapefile captures 206 daimyo polygons representing the stable mid-Edo settlement.

Most of these polygons remained the same physical territory through 1870 — even when the ruling family changed, the geographic boundaries usually didn’t. This is what makes the 1664 shapefile usable as an 1870-era basemap for most cases.

14.2 What CHANGED between 1664 and 1870

About 30 domains have meaningful changes. They fall into five categories:

14.2.1 Category 1 — Administrative renames (same place, new name)

These are pure naming changes: the polygon, the family, and the territory all stayed the same, but the domain was administratively renamed in the 1869 haihan-chiken transitional period.

For these, the project handles the alias mismatch via the SEAT_TO_CANONICAL lookup table in the map-build script. Polygon = old name; data row = canonical = new name. No territorial change — the polygon shape is correct.

14.2.2 Category 2 — Post-Boshin territorial reductions (1869–70)

After the Boshin War ended in mid-1869, several pro-bakufu domains were punished with territorial reductions — and in some cases, relocations to entirely new regions. These are the cases where the 1664 polygon does NOT match the 1870 territory because the territory itself was reduced.

WarningMap approximation notice

For these reduced-territory cases, the polygon shown on the map is the 1664 boundary — i.e., larger than the actual 1870 territory. The data values (kokudaka, population, etc.) are correctly the post-Boshin 1870 figures. So when you look at, say, 斗南藩 on the map, the colored polygon visually represents the historic 230,000-koku Aizu territory, while the recorded kokudaka of 30,000 koku reflects the actual reduced Tonami territory.

This is the cleanest documented approximation the project makes. For statistical analysis the kokudaka value enters directly with no issue. For visual interpretation of “size on map,” there’s a mismatch in these ~6 cases.

14.2.3 Category 3 — Mid-Edo transfers (家替, iegae) where polygon = wrong family

A small number of domains had a family transfer that’s not captured in the 1664 shapefile because the transfer happened AFTER 1664. These are minor in number but the lookup table handles them:

  • 倉敷藩 — became a tenryō (bakufu direct land) after a Bakumatsu-era confiscation
  • A handful of small domains created/abolished between 1664 and 1870 (e.g., post-Boshin 真鶴藩 wasn’t extant in 1664; 岩村田藩 changed families)

For these, the polygon may show the 1664 family / territory while the 1870 data is for the current family. The domain_name_crossref.csv lookup table bridges these, linking the polygon-side name to the data-side canonical.

14.2.4 Category 4 — Post-Boshin new domains (no 1664 polygon at all)

A handful of 1870 domains were created during 1868-69 and have no 1664 polygon:

1870 domain Why it didn’t exist in 1664 1870 size
斗南藩 Created 1869 from reduced Aizu 30,000 koku
大泉藩 Brief 1869 form of reduced Shōnai 110,000 koku
鶴田藩 Created from former Aizu sub-fief small
豊津藩 Created from relocated Kokura (after the 1866 Choshu Expedition burned Kokura Castle) 150,000 koku
多古藩 Reactivated 1868 (had been abolished mid-Edo) small

For these, the polygon-to-canonical lookup table either points to the pre-reduction polygon (e.g., 斗南 → uses 会津 polygon, accepting the size mismatch documented in Category 2) or leaves the polygon unshaded and notes the unmatched canonical.

Of the 287 canonicals in analytic_wide.csv, 170 match a 1664 polygon. The remaining ~117 either don’t match (small post-Boshin domains, sub-han, or sankyō without territory) or are minor name variants that didn’t get the alias-table treatment yet.

14.2.5 Category 5 — Sub-han (支藩) — present in 1664, sometimes missing from 1870 data

About 30 domains in 1664 were sub-han of larger parent domains — they had nominal daimyo status but were administratively dependent. Some examples: 大聖寺藩 (Maeda sub-han of Kaga), 富山藩 (another Maeda sub-han), 蓮池藩 (Nabeshima sub-han of Saga), 鹿島藩 (another Nabeshima sub-han), 小城藩 (also Nabeshima), 佐土原藩 (Shimazu sub-han).

By 1870, most sub-han had their own separate listing in Hansei Ichiran — but the 1664 shapefile may show them as carved out within a larger parent polygon, or sometimes as their own polygon. The mismatch is minor; visual interpretation usually doesn’t suffer.

14.3 The 1871 endpoint — haihan-chiken

On August 29, 1871 (Meiji 4, 7th month 14th day), the Meiji government issued the haihan-chiken (廃藩置県) edict abolishing all 280+ daimyo domains in a single proclamation. Every former daimyo became a governor (知事 chiji) of a corresponding prefecture (県 ken), initially with the same boundaries — but within months, these were consolidated into 72 prefectures, then 47 by 1888.

So by late 1871, every polygon on our map ceased to represent a political unit. The daimyo families became kazoku in 1884 (the paper’s DV), but the land was thereafter administered by appointed prefectural governors under the central government.

14.4 Implications for the maps

  1. Polygons are 1664 vintage — used as a geographic approximation of the 1870 stable territory
  2. Reduced post-Boshin cases (~6 domains) have polygons larger than their 1870 actual territory; documented above
  3. Renamed cases (~10 domains) match correctly via alias lookup; no territorial issue
  4. Post-Boshin-created domains (~5) reuse the parent’s polygon with the size-mismatch caveat
  5. For statistical analysis, the polygons don’t enter — only the data values do, and those are 1870-correct
  6. For visual interpretation, the dominant pattern (Kyushu = pro-Restoration coalition, Tōhoku = Northern Alliance, etc.) is unaffected by these approximations; only the size-scale on a few specific reduced-territory cases is misleading
NoteA better basemap would help

If a future contributor finds (or builds) an 1870-era daimyo polygon shapefile — capturing the post-Boshin reductions correctly — it could be swapped in directly. The map-build script is parameterised on the polygon input directory; replacing those files with an 1870-era equivalent would automatically refresh all maps. The data side would not need to change.

Known candidate: the CHGIS V6 dataset has Meiji-era prefecture polygons, but not 1870 daimyo polygons specifically. The Stanford Spatial History Project also has some Edo-era GIS data. Neither has been integrated yet.

Polygon source: Harvard China Historical GIS (CHGIS) 1664 daimyo shapefile. Territorial reduction details: 工藤威『奥羽列藩同盟の基礎的研究』(岩田書院 2002) for OREP members; Lebra (1993) and Asami (1994) for the rank-vs-territory distinction; per-domain JP Wikipedia articles for individual rename / transfer specifics. Haihan-chiken 1871: Beasley (1972) Ch. XII; 「廃藩置県の詔書」, dajōkan-fu, 1871-07-14 (lunar).
