14  1664 → 1871: how stable were domain boundaries?

The maps in this walkthrough use the Harvard CHGIS 1664 daimyō (大名だいみょう) shapefile to draw each domain’s polygon. The dataset itself measures 1870 — six years before haihan chiken (廃藩置県はいはんちけん). So there’s a 206-year gap between the polygon vintage and the data vintage. This chapter documents what changed between those dates, what didn’t, and how the project handles each case.

TipWhy this chapter exists

For most domains, boundaries were extremely stable between 1664 and 1870 — the Tokugawa system was deliberately designed to lock in territorial arrangements. But about 30 domains experienced changes (administrative renames, post-Boshin reductions, transfer-relocations) that matter for matching the polygon to the 1870 data. Documenting them makes the maps’ approximations transparent.

14.1 The 1664 baseline

By 1664, sixty-four years after Sekigahara, the Tokugawa daimyō system had largely stabilized. Major redistribution of fiefs (the kunigae / 国替くにがえ system, where domains were administratively reassigned to different families) had slowed dramatically from the chaotic 1601–1640 period. The 1664 shapefile captures 206 daimyō polygons representing the stable mid-Edo settlement.

Most of these polygons remained the same physical territory through 1870 — even when the ruling family changed, the geographic boundaries usually didn’t. This is what makes the 1664 shapefile usable as an 1870-era basemap for most cases.

14.2 What CHANGED between 1664 and 1870

About 30 domains have meaningful changes. They fall into five categories:

14.2.1 Category 1 — Administrative renames (same place, new name)

These are pure naming changes: the polygon, the family, and the territory all stayed the same, but the domain was administratively renamed in the 1869 haihan chiken transitional period.

14.2.2 Category 2 — Post-Boshin territorial reductions (1869–70)

After the Boshin War (Boshin sensō) ended in mid-1869, several pro-bakufu (幕府ばくふ) domains were punished with territorial reductions — and in some cases, relocations to entirely new regions. These are the cases where the 1664 polygon does NOT match the 1870 territory because the territory itself was reduced.

WarningMap approximation notice

For these reduced-territory cases, the polygon shown on the map is the 1664 boundary — i.e., larger than the actual 1870 territory. The data values (kokudaka, population, etc.) are correctly the post-Boshin 1870 figures. So when you look at, say, 斗南藩となみはん on the map, the colored polygon visually represents the historic 230,000-koku Aizu territory, while the recorded kokudaka of 30,000 koku reflects the actual reduced Tonami territory.

This is the cleanest documented approximation the project makes. For statistical analysis the kokudaka value enters directly with no issue. For visual interpretation of “size on map,” there’s a mismatch in these ~6 cases.

14.2.3 Category 3 — Mid-Edo transfers (家替いえがえ, iegae) where polygon = wrong family

A small number of domains had a family transfer that’s not captured in the 1664 shapefile because the transfer happened AFTER 1664. These are minor in number but the lookup table handles them:

  • 倉敷くらしき藩はん — became a tenryō (bakufu direct land) after a Bakumatsu-era confiscation
  • A handful of small domains created/abolished between 1664 and 1870 (e.g., post-Boshin 真鶴まなづる藩はん wasn’t extant in 1664; 岩村むら田藩はん changed families)

For these, the polygon may show the 1664 family / territory while the 1870 data is for the current family. The domain_name_crossref.csv lookup table bridges these, linking the polygon-side name to the data-side canonical.

A handful of 1870 domains were created during 1868-69 and have no 1664 polygon:

For these, the polygon-to-canonical lookup table either points to the pre-reduction polygon (e.g., 斗南となみ → uses 会津あいづ polygon, accepting the size mismatch documented in Category 2) or leaves the polygon unshaded and notes the unmatched canonical.

Of the 286 canonicals in analytic_wide.csv, 171 match a 1664 polygon. The remaining ~115 either don’t match (small post-Boshin domains, sub-han, or sankyō (御三卿ごさんきょう) without territory) or are minor name variants that didn’t get the alias-table treatment yet.

14.2.5 Category 5 — Sub-han (支藩はん) — present in 1664, sometimes missing from 1870 data

About 30 domains in 1664 were sub-han of larger parent domains — they had nominal daimyō status but were administratively dependent. Some examples: 大聖寺だいしょうじ藩はん (Maeda sub-han of Kaga), 富山とやま藩はん (another Maeda sub-han), 蓮池はすのいけ藩はん (Nabeshima sub-han of Saga), 鹿島かしま藩はん (another Nabeshima sub-han), 小城藩おぎはん (also Nabeshima), 佐土原藩さどわらはん (Shimazu sub-han).

By 1870, most sub-han had their own separate listing in Hansei Ichiran — but the 1664 shapefile may show them as carved out within a larger parent polygon, or sometimes as their own polygon. The mismatch is minor; visual interpretation usually doesn’t suffer.

14.3 The 1871 endpoint — haihan chiken

On August 29, 1871 (Meiji 4, 7th month 14th day), the Meiji government issued the haihan chiken edict abolishing all 280+ daimyō domains in a single proclamation. Every former daimyō became a governor (知事ちじ chiji) of a corresponding prefecture (県 ken), initially with the same boundaries — but within months, these were consolidated into 72 prefectures, then 47 by 1888.

So by late 1871, every polygon on our map ceased to represent a political unit. The daimyō families became kazoku (華族かぞく) in 1884 (the paper’s outcome variable), but the land was thereafter administered by appointed prefectural governors under the central government.

14.4 What the blank white space on the maps means

A reader looking at any of the choropleth maps notices that large stretches of Japan — especially through central Honshū around Kōfu and Hida — are left blank white, with no polygon at all. This is not missing data. It is land that was not a daimyō domain.

The base map contains polygons only for the 206 daimyō domains the 1664 shapefile captures. Roughly a quarter of Edo-period Japan, measured by assessed yield, was not daimyō domain and therefore has no polygon to draw:

  • 天領てんりょう (tenryō) — land administered directly by the Tokugawa shogunate, about 4 million koku, scattered nationwide but concentrated around Edo, Kyoto, Osaka, Nagasaki, the mining districts, and — most visibly on the map — the provinces of Kai (the Kōfu basin) and Hida. The large white expanse through central Honshū is mostly tenryō.
  • 旗本はたもと (hatamoto) holdings — fiefs of the shogun’s direct bannermen, about 3 million koku, densely packed across the Kantō and Tōkai plains. Each individual hatamoto fief sat below the 10,000-koku threshold that defined a daimyō, so none of them counts as a “domain.”
  • 寺社領 / 公家くげ領 — temple, shrine, and imperial-court lands, smaller and concentrated around Kyoto and Nara.

None of that land is in a 286-domain dataset, by definition — the paper is about daimyō, and these lands had no daimyō. So they correctly show as white.

A second, smaller contributor: the 1664 shapefile has 206 polygons but the dataset has 286 domains. The ~115 domains without a 1664 polygon (small post-Boshin creations, sub-han, the landless sankyō) cannot be drawn, and contribute some additional white space where their territory would be.

NoteWhite vs grey — they mean different things
  • White = not a daimyō domain (shogunal tenryō, hatamoto land, temple / court land, or an unmapped small domain). No polygon exists.
  • Grey = this is a daimyō domain with a polygon, but it has no value for the specific variable the map is colouring.

The white space is a feature of the historical map, not a defect in the data. It shows at a glance how much of Japan the shogunate and its direct vassals controlled — the daimyō domains, for all their political weight, were a patchwork around a large shogunal core.

14.5 Implications for the maps

  1. Polygons are 1664 vintage — used as a geographic approximation of the 1870 stable territory
  2. Reduced post-Boshin cases (~6 domains) have polygons larger than their 1870 actual territory; documented above
  3. Renamed cases (~10 domains) match correctly via alias lookup; no territorial issue
  4. Post-Boshin-created domains (~5) reuse the parent’s polygon with the size-mismatch caveat
  5. For statistical analysis, the polygons don’t enter — only the data values do, and those are 1870-correct
  6. For visual interpretation, the dominant pattern (Kyushu = pro-Restoration coalition, Tōhoku = Northern Alliance, etc.) is unaffected by these approximations; only the size-scale on a few specific reduced-territory cases is misleading
NoteCould the shogunate land be drawn in, instead of left white?

Not easily, and the reason is historically substantive. Shogunal land (shihaisho 支配所 in the period; tenryō 天領てんりょう is the Meiji-era retronym) was not contiguous territory. Unlike a daimyō domain — a mostly-connected block — shogunal land was a scatter of individual villages and districts across the whole country, and hatamoto bannermen holdings were more fragmented still. There is no clean polygon to draw, because the thing itself was not a clean polygon.

The CHGIS daimyō shapefile this project uses is, by design, daimyō-only — its documentation lists a single topic, “dmyo.” It has no shogunal-land layer, and the same is true of the other off-the-shelf historical-Japan GIS products.

A correct delineation of shogunal land has to be built from the village level up. Two near-comprehensive shogunal land surveys exist — one for 1664, one for ca. 1868 (the 旧高旧領取調帳, Kyūdaka kyūryō torishirabe-chō) — and the standard approach is to geocode every village in one of those surveys and colour it by holder type. The 1664 survey was mapped this way by Nishioka & Hattori (1956) and by Drixler (2015). The ongoing Digital Atlas of Tokugawa Japan (Fabian Drixler, Yale) is reconstructing the entire territorial structure village-by-village; its geospatial output is promised as a free web resource but is not yet a downloadable shapefile.

So: drawing the shogunate’s land would be a genuine GIS project (acquire or build village-level holder data, then aggregate), not a drop-in basemap swap. For this walkthrough the white space, explained above, is the honest and standard treatment — it accurately reads as “the non-daimyō remainder.” Separately, CHGIS does have 1828 and 1869 daimyō snapshots (this project used only the 1664 one); swapping in the 1869 snapshot would improve the daimyō side, capturing post-Boshin reductions, without addressing the shogunal land.
