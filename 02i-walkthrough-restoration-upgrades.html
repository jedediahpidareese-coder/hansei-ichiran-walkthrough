11  Walkthrough: The 5 small-domain Restoration-service upgrades

This chapter is structured differently from the previous walkthroughs. Instead of going domain-by-domain through one focal case, it walks through a pattern — the 5 viscount → count upgrades that constitute the cleanest empirical evidence for the paper’s central claim.

11.1 The pattern

Per the Asami (1994) 現米げんまい (genmai) rule, all 5 of these domains should have received viscount rank (現米げんまい well below 50,000 koku). All 5 actually received count — a one-tier upgrade. And all 5 were on the anti-幕府ばくふ (bakufu) / pro-restoration side.

Domain Family 石高こくだか (kokudaka) Genmai Expected Actual Restoration role
大村藩おおむらはん 大村家おおむらけ 50,455 36,324 viscount count Active Restoration coalition
佐土原藩さどわらはん 島津家しまづけ (cadet) 27,711 18,826 viscount count Satsuma cadet, joined Restoration
対馬府中藩つしまふちゅうはん 宗家そうけ 52,174 35,236 viscount count Korea diplomacy, 国主格こくしゅかく status, Restoration role
津和野藩つわのはん 亀井家かめいけ 75,061 28,232 viscount count Iwami 譜代ふだい (fudai)-status but became anti-幕府ばくふ; Kamei Koremi (亀井茲監かめいこれみ) influential in early Meiji
龍岡藩たつおかはん 大給松平家おぎゅうまつだいらけ 12,924 5,137 viscount count Restoration-era modernization; classified pro-幕府ばくふ by family-affiliation but received Restoration upgrade
ImportantWhy this pattern matters for the paper

The 19 現米げんまい-rule deviations split into four categories: - 3 prince upgrades (Satsuma, Chōshū, Shizuoka) — Restoration prize + Tokugawa exception - 3 marquess upgrades (Mito, Fukui, Uwajima) — sanke clause + Restoration role - 5 viscount→count upgrades (this chapter) — pure Restoration service - 8 viscount→baron downgrades — 付家老つけがろう (陪臣ばいしん (baishin)) houses, orthogonal to Boshin

The viscount → count upgrades are the cleanest evidence because:

  1. They don’t have a separate rule exception (no sanke clause, no Tokugawa-main exception)
  2. They’re small enough that the rank could plausibly have gone either way
  3. Their 石高こくだか is below 50k, so the rule strictly says viscount
  4. But they got count instead — and all 5 are on the anti-幕府ばくふ side

This is the empirical core of “Boshin / Restoration alignment matters for rank deviations, conditional on revenue.”

11.2 大村藩おおむらはん — small but militarily decisive

Value
Family 大村家おおむらけ
1884 recipient 大村むら純熙すみひろ
Boshin role Active anti-幕府ばくふ; sent troops to fight in northeast
Why upgraded Restoration service

Ōmura was a 40-50k koku domain in Hizen (next to Saga, hence the Saga supplement is sometimes relevant). The Ōmura family converted to Christianity in the 16th century, was suppressed under Hideyoshi, became a typical small 外様とざま (tozama) under Tokugawa, and emerged as a vocal Restoration-supporter under Ōmura Sumihiro (大村むら純熙すみひろ) in the 1860s. Ōmura troops fought as part of the imperial expedition in northern Honshu. The 1884 count rank explicitly cites this service.

11.3 佐土原藩さどわらはん — Shimazu cadet

Value
Family 島津家しまづけ (Sadowara cadet of Satsuma’s Shimazu)
1884 recipient 島津忠寛しまづただひろ
Boshin role Followed Satsuma main line, active anti-幕府ばくふ
Why upgraded Restoration service

Sadowara is a 27k koku Hyūga (modern Miyazaki) domain held by a cadet branch of the Shimazu (Satsuma) family. Initially a fully separate domain, it followed Satsuma’s lead through the Restoration period. Sadowara forces fought alongside Satsuma in 1868. The 1884 count rank is part of the same Restoration-reward logic as Satsuma’s prince upgrade, but at the smaller scale appropriate to Sadowara’s revenue.

11.4 対馬府中藩つしまふちゅうはん — the Korea-diplomacy domain

ImportantTwo names
  • 対馬府中藩つしまふちゅうはん (Tsushima-fuchū-han) — historical/canonical project name
  • 厳原藩いわはらはん (Iwahara-han) — post-1869 administrative rename (the seat was at Iwahara); the Hansei Ichiran form

The 宗 (Sō) family had ruled Tsushima continuously since the 13th century.

Value
Family 宗家そうけ
1884 recipient 宗重正そうしげまさ
Boshin role Anti-幕府ばくふ, late-joining
Why upgraded 国主格こくしゅかく status + Korea-diplomacy role + Restoration alignment

Tsushima is the island domain between Japan and Korea that for centuries managed Japan-Korea diplomacy. The Sō family held 国主格こくしゅかく status (treatment as a country-lord) despite only 52k koku of revenue, because of Tsushima’s strategic importance as Japan’s diplomatic gateway to Korea. The 1884 count rank reflects all three:

  1. 国主格こくしゅかく acknowledgment (the rank-rule had a separate provision for these houses)
  2. The Korea-diplomacy continuity (the Meiji government needed Tsushima’s institutional knowledge for the 1876 Japan-Korea Treaty negotiations)
  3. The pro-Restoration alignment

11.5 津和野藩つわのはん — small but intellectually outsized

Value
Family 亀井家かめいけ
1884 recipient 亀井茲明かめいこれあき
Boshin role Anti-幕府ばくふ
Why upgraded Restoration role; Kamei Koremi (亀井茲監かめいこれみ) was an influential early-Meiji bureaucrat

Tsuwano is a tiny 75k koku Iwami (modern Shimane) domain whose late-Edo lord Kamei Koremi (亀井茲監かめいこれみ) was an unusually scholarly figure who pushed nativist (kokugaku) intellectual movements + was a Restoration sympathizer. Tsuwano was one of the earliest small domains to support the Imperial side. The 1884 count rank reflects Kamei’s personal contributions to the new government as much as the domain’s military role.

11.6 龍岡藩たつおかはん — the puzzling upgrade

Value
Family 大給松平家おぎゅうまつだいらけ (Ōgyū-Matsudaira)
1884 recipient 大給恒おぎゅうゆずる
Boshin role Pro-幕府ばくふ (by family-affiliation: Ōgyū are a 譜代ふだい/親藩しんぱん-adjacent line)
Why upgraded Modernization decoration: Ōgyū Yutaka (大給恒おぎゅうゆずる) was an early-Meiji modernizer who served as Director of the Decoration Bureau (賞勲局総裁しょうくんきょくそうさい) and designed Japan’s modern medal system

Tatsuoka is the odd one out in this list: it’s classified pro-幕府ばくふ by family-affiliation, but the head of the family personally served the new Meiji government as the founder of Japan’s modern decoration system. His viscount → count upgrade is essentially a personal recognition that happened to flow to the family rank. This case shows the 1884 ennoblement system was flexible enough to reward post-Restoration service (not just Restoration-era combat).

11.7 What this teaches about the paper’s empirical claim

The cleanest version of the paper’s central claim:

Of the 9 大名だいみょう (daimyō) families that received explicit 華族かぞく (kazoku)-rank upgrades for Restoration-era service (excluding the structural exceptions like the sanke clause), 9 of 9 were anti-幕府ばくふ / pro-Restoration aligned. The 5 viscount → count upgrades are the largest subgroup of this pattern.

The 4 marquess+prince upgrades (Satsuma, Chōshū, Fukui, Uwajima) are well-known. The 5 viscount → count upgrades are the less-famous, more-numerous, statistically more-persuasive part of the same pattern — and the paper highlights all 9 together as a clean empirical core.
