9  Walkthrough: Northern Alliance defectors (Kubota, Hirosaki, Matsumae)

The Ōuetsu Reppan Dōmei (奥羽越列藩同盟, the “Northern Alliance”) was the pro-Tokugawa military coalition formed in May 1868 by ~31 northern domains. But the coalition wasn’t monolithic: at least 9 signatory domains defected to the Imperial side during the war, and the trajectories of those defectors are paper-relevant.

This chapter walks through three of the most consequential defectors:

  • 久保田藩 (Kubota / Akita) — Satake family, the largest Northern Alliance signatory to defect (333k koku)
  • 弘前藩 (Hirosaki) — Tsugaru family, northern Honshu’s far-tip domain
  • 館藩 (Matsumae) — Matsumae family, Hokkaido’s only daimyō (大名), defected via internal coup
NoteWhy these three together

All three are anti-bakufu (幕府) / pro-restoration in the final dataset — but only because they switched mid-war. The contemporaneous story is one of contested loyalty: each signed the Northern Alliance covenant, sent troops north against the imperial advance, then reversed course. The 1884 kazoku (華族) rank treatment reflects the end-of-war position; their domains’ ranks are rule-consistent (not punished as bakufu-loyal).

9.1 久保田藩 (Kubota / 秋田)

ImportantTwo names — same place
  • 久保田藩 (Kubota-han) — historical/canonical project name (the castle town was Kubota Castle)
  • 秋田藩 (Akita-han) — post-1869 administrative rename (after the surrounding Akita province); the Hansei Ichiran vol. 2 form

Most English-language scholarship uses “Akita.” The project’s canonical name is the older 久保田藩 because Hansei Ichiran was compiled at the moment of transition.

9.1.2 The defection narrative

Kubota signed the Northern Alliance covenant at the Second White Stone Conference (May 11, 1868). But the Satake family had a longstanding rivalry with neighboring Nanbu (Morioka, also a Northern Alliance member) — and a strong Tokugawa-loyalty deficit going back to Sekigahara, when the Satake were transferred from their original Mito domain to Akita as a demotion for siding with the western coalition.

By July 1868, Kubota had defected to the Imperial side. The Satake’s resentment toward the bakufu (200+ years old) probably mattered more than ideology.

The Satake family received marquess rank in 1884 — consistent with their kokudaka of 333k. No “Restoration reward,” no penalty — just the mechanical rule applied to a tozama house above 150k koku.

9.2.2 The defection narrative

Hirosaki is at the very northern tip of Honshū — historically the Tsugaru family’s domain, the archetypal far-northern tozama. The Tsugaru had been semi-independent vassals of the Nanbu (Morioka) before being recognized as independent daimyō at the 1590 Odawara campaign, and there was lingering bad blood between Tsugaru and Nanbu.

Hirosaki signed the Northern Alliance covenant at the Second White Stone Conference but defected shortly thereafter, partly motivated by Tsugaru/Nanbu rivalry (Nanbu was a major Northern Alliance combatant; if Nanbu wins, Tsugaru loses). Hirosaki’s troops fought against the imperial forces briefly before switching, then participated in the imperial advance.

The Tsugaru family received count in 1884. Their genmai (現米) of 142k put them just below the marquess threshold; the actual rank is rule-consistent. No Restoration upgrade despite the defection — defecting to the winning side wasn’t enough on its own to merit a kazoku-rank upgrade beyond the mechanical level.

9.3 館藩 (Matsumae — Hokkaido)

Important1869 rename
  • 松前藩 (Matsumae-han) — the historical Edo-era name, used through Boshin
  • 館藩 (Tate-han) — 1869 administrative rename when the family was briefly moved to a different site; this is the form Hansei Ichiran uses

The project’s canonical name is 館藩 because that’s what existed at the 1870 survey moment. Most English-language Boshin literature uses “Matsumae.”

9.3.2 The defection narrative

Matsumae was Japan’s only daimyō domain in Hokkaidō (then called Ezo). The Matsumae family held a small, geographically isolated, fishery-and-trade-based domain that mattered less for its rice yield (30k koku is small) than for its position controlling Honshu’s gateway to Hokkaido.

Matsumae signed the Northern Alliance covenant at the Second White Stone Conference but defected via internal coup on July 28, 1868: a faction within the Matsumae retainers seized control and committed the domain to the Imperial side. This dramatic switch had downstream consequences:

  • It deprived the Ezo Republic (Enomoto Takeaki’s October 1868–June 1869 holdout) of a friendly base on Hokkaido
  • The defection was so abrupt that the family’s bookkeeping records were ambiguous about which side they were on for several weeks

The Matsumae family received viscount in 1884 — consistent with their small genmai of 23k koku.

9.4 What the three teach us about Boshin alignment

These three cases illustrate why the project codes Boshin alignment as an end-of-war position rather than the initial commitment:

  • All three signed the Northern Alliance covenant in mid-May 1868 (pro-bakufu commitment)
  • All three defected to the Imperial side by mid-to-late summer 1868
  • The 1884 ennoblement treated them as anti-bakufu / pro-restoration — but did not reward them with upgrades: their ranks are rule-consistent with their genmai

This is in tension with the simpler narrative that “Boshin loyalty matters.” Defecting to the winning side was good enough to avoid the territorial reduction that fell on the non-defecting Northern Alliance members (Aizu, Nanbu, Nihonmatsu, etc.) — but it was not enough to merit explicit rank upgrades. The 9 documented Restoration-service upgrades all went to active anti-bakufu leaders, not late-defectors.
