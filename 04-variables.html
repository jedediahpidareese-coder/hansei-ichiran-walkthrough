16  The variables

The dataset has 130 columns organized into nine logical families. This page is a reference; you don’t need to memorize it.

16.1 The variable families at a glance

NoteThe several kokudaka — and which one the paper uses

A reader can reasonably ask why the revenue row lists five fields. They belong to two families:

  • Assessed yield — what the land was rated to produce. The Tokugawa cadastral surveys rated every domain in koku, and Hansei Ichiran reports that rating in several variants. The two that matter conceptually are omotedaka (表高) — the “face” figure registered with the shogunate, frozen at early-1600s levels, which fixed a domain’s rank, precedence, and military obligation — and naidaka (内高), the domain’s own internal figure from its later surveys, which by the 1860s usually ran well above the official omotedaka. Sōdaka (草高), gōdaka (合高 — gross plus reclaimed new fields), and gendaka (現高) are further variants of this assessed-capacity number.
  • Actual rice — genmai (現米): the rice that actually flowed as tax in 1869. Because a domain taxed only a fraction of its assessed yield, genmai runs far below any assessed figure.

The paper uses both, for different jobs. The composite kokudaka (kokudaka_iv), drawn from the assessed-yield family, is the paper’s predictor — its measure of a domain’s size and revenue base. Genmai is used only to compute the expected 1884 peerage rank under the Asami (1994) threshold rule. They are not competing measures of one quantity.

The gap between the official omotedaka and a domain’s real productive base is a standard theme of early-modern Japanese fiscal history (see Ravina 1999). The kokudaka-versus-genmai question as it bears on the peerage is the Asami-versus-Lebra debate walked through in the kazoku-rank chapter and the 19-deviations chapter.

16.2 The most important columns for the paper

16.2.1 Revenue base — kokudaka (column kokudaka_iv)

The composite kokudaka — the maximum of the available Hansei Ichiran fields (sōdaka, gōdaka, omotedaka), then Kyūdaka kyūryō torishirabechō (旧高旧領取調帳, 1872 Meiji cadastral survey, hosted by the National Museum of Japanese History / Rekihaku) for the 13 domains absent from HI. This is the denominator in the paper’s headline retainers-per-1,000-koku fiscal-distress metric (see chapter 04b).

  • Coverage: 276/286
  • Range: ~2,000 to ~1,400,000 koku
  • Why composite: Hansei Ichiran reports different fields for different domains. Saga uses 貮口合 (= gōdaka); Chōshū uses sōdaka; some small domains only have omotedaka. The composite picks the most-comprehensive figure available.
  • The Rekihaku tier: 13 domains have no recordable kokudaka in HI vol 1 (仙台, 新発田, 杵築, 母里, 清崎, 足利, 須坂, 岩村, 対馬府中, 岩国, 岩崎, 松岡, 斗南). For these, the IV is sourced from the 1872 Kyūdaka kyūryō torishirabechō cadastral survey — summed per-village from the Rekihaku open-access database. This is a kusadaka-tier (actual rated land yield) metric, the same conceptual tier as HI sōdaka / gōdaka, restoring methodological consistency. For 仙台 specifically, the IV uses only post-Boshin retained territory (excluding 1869 carve-off prefectures 登米/胆沢/江刺/角田), giving 299,659 koku instead of the raw 974,010-koku all-territory figure.
  • Citation: a sibling source column records which field was actually used for each domain (hi_sodaka, hi_godaka, hi_omotedaka, rekihaku_kyudaka_1872).
NoteThe Sendai-Date case in detail

仙台藩 is the one case in the dataset where the pre-Boshin and at-haihan-chiken kokudaka diverge dramatically. The 1869 Boshin punishment was a full territorial confiscation followed by re-grant of 28万石 to the three-year-old heir Date Munemoto 伊達宗基 (Meiji 1/12/12 = 1869-01-24, per 仙台市史 通史編6 近代1, 2008; 栗原伸一郎『戊辰戦争と「奥羽越」列藩同盟』, 2017; Bakkalian, The Sparrow’s Dream, Pittsburgh PhD 2017).

This shows up in Hansei Ichiran itself: vol 2 p.116R carries an explicit 「草高等不詳細」 (“kokudaka and similar details unclear”) annotation directly under the ○仙臺藩 header. HI itself documented being unable to record Sendai’s kokudaka in 1870, almost certainly because the new post-Boshin cadastral was incomplete at the time of the survey — strong provenance for using a substitute kokudaka drawn from the 1872 cadastral.

The dataset carries four Sendai kokudaka tiers for transparency:

Column Value Interpretation
pre_boshin_omotedaka_koku (sensitivity) 620,057 Pre-Boshin formal omotedaka (Date Masamune’s 1601 投与, frozen)
rekihaku_kyudaka_raw_koku (sensitivity) 974,010 Pre-Boshin all-territory cadastral kusadaka (Rekihaku unfiltered)
kokudaka_iv (the paper’s IV) 299,659 At-haihan-chiken kusadaka (Rekihaku-filtered to retained territory)
wiki_omotedaka_koku (cross-ref) 280,000 Post-Boshin formal grant to Date Munemoto

The carved-off territory (974,010 − 299,659 = 674,350 koku, in the 1869-created 登米県 / 胆沢県 / 江刺県 / 角田県) matches the documented Sendai 62万→28万 punitive reduction almost exactly — the 1872 cadastral preserves the Boshin penalty’s territorial fingerprint via the 旧県 column.

A methodological caveat: the 旧領 column in 旧高旧領取調帳 uses different temporal-reference conventions across prefectures (per Rekihaku’s own documentation and Kimura Motoi 校訂, 1969-79). The Tōhoku volumes (where Sendai sits) treat 旧領 as the pre-Boshin Bakumatsu lord — so the carve-off filter is correct for Sendai but the convention should be re-verified for other regions before being applied elsewhere.

16.2.2 1884 kazoku rank (column kazoku_rank_actual)

The peerage rank awarded in 1884, one of 5 tiers.

  • Coverage: 284/286 (2 documented exclusions)
  • Distribution: 3 prince, 15 marquess, 34 count, 222 viscount, 10 baron
  • Citation chain: 浅見雅男『華族誕生 名誉と体面の明治 (Kazoku tanjō: meiyo to taimen no Meiji, “Birth of the Peerage: Honor and Reputation in Meiji”)』(1994) + Kanpō 第307号 (1884) + 華族大鑑刊行会編『華族大鑑 (Kazoku taikan, “Compendium of the Peerage”)』(1990) for princes
  • See the kazoku Rank chapter for the full story.

16.2.3 Treatment — Boshin alignment (column boshin_alignment_group)

Each domain’s side in the Boshin War (Boshin sensō).

  • Coverage: 286/286
  • Values: anti-bakufu (幕府) / pro-restoration, pro-bakufu, neutral
  • Distribution: 236 anti-bakufu, 40 pro-bakufu, 10 neutral
  • Citation chain: Esposito (2020) + 工藤威 (2002) + Beasley (1972). See the Boshin Alignment chapter.

16.2.4 Paper-relevant — Genmai-rule deviations (column kazoku_rank_deviation_genmai)

The 19 daimyō (大名) families whose actual 1884 rank diverged from the mechanical prediction.

  • Coverage: 19/286 (most domains land where the rule predicts)
  • Why this column exists: Asami (1994) argues the 1884 threshold operated on genmai (現米) — post-1869 tax-reported rice flow — not sōdaka/omotedaka. Applying the genmai rule, 19 cases show real deviations. These are the paper’s core sample.
  • See The 19 Deviations chapter.

16.2.5 Paper-relevant — Focal-han tier (columns is_focal_han_tier1, is_focal_han_tier2, focal_han_tier_source)

The two-tier focal-domain commendation issued by the haihan-chiken imperial decree on 1871/7/14.

  • Tier 1 (4 domains, is_focal_han_tier1): Sat-Chō-Saga-Tosa = 薩摩 / 長州 / 佐賀 / 土佐 — the four domains explicitly commended for having first proposed the 1869 hanseki hōkan (return of registers). Synonymous with is_focal_han (both columns kept for naming clarity).
  • Tier 2 (8 domains, is_focal_han_tier2): Tier 1 plus 尾張 (Owari/Nagoya, Tokugawa) + 鳥取 (Tottori, Ikeda) + 熊本 (Kumamoto, Hosokawa) + 徳島 (Tokushima, Hachisuka) — the four additional domains commended on the same day for implementation merit (hanseki ritsu-sei, establishment of the [post-Restoration] register system).
  • Why this column exists: the project’s reading of the haihan-chiken-day primary sources documents two tiers of imperial commendation, not just one. The eight Tier-2 domains together represent the political coalition that publicly underwrote the abolition — useful as an alternative control variable for analyses where the four-domain Sat-Chō-Saga-Tosa indicator might be too narrow.
  • Coverage: 100% (binary indicators populated for every domain). Tier 1 count = 4; Tier 2 count = 8.
  • Citation chain (per focal_han_tier_source): triple-confirmed by the haihan-chiken imperial decree (Meiji Shiyō vol 1 pp.27–28), Inoue Kaoru’s biography (vol 1 p.452, which gives the explicit list 熊本・名古屋・徳島・鳥取), and Kido Takayoshi’s diary (vol II p.65, 28–29 Aug 1871 entries, in the Brown & Hirota 1983 translation). See the Primary sources section: the abolition decree, Inoue Kaoru’s account, and Kido’s diary. A fourth-source corroboration is in Ōkubo Toshimichi’s contemporaneous diary and the 1928 editorial 【辨説】 commentary in the Ōkubo Monjo vol. 4.
  • Note on Ikeda ambiguity: Kido’s diary mentions “the Governors Ikeda” without specifying Tottori versus Okayama (both Ikeda lines). Inoue’s biography is explicit on Tottori (鳥取), which the project treats as definitive.

16.3 A note on military data sparsity

The military columns are interesting but sparse:

Gap state Count What it means
COMPLETE 29 Full troop/warship/artillery roster
PARTIAL 215 Some military data, some missing
PARTIAL_UNBUCKETED 10 Military data present but not yet sorted into a partial sub-bucket
HI_OMITTED 15 Source explicitly says 書ナラス (“not recorded”)
HI_OMITTED_PARTIAL 15 Mixed — some military fields recorded, some 書ナラス
NO_MILITARY_DATA 2 No recoverable military entry at all (only 和歌山藩 and 薩摩藩)

For the paper’s actual military analysis, the focal-4 supplements (Satsuma, Chōshū, Tosa, Saga) draw from published scholarly sources (Esposito 2020, Craig 1961, Roberts 1998, 藤野保 2010) rather than from Hansei Ichiran’s sparse military columns alone.

16.4 How to find anything

The single-source-of-truth is data_provenance.md — every column has its source, citation guidance, coverage, and known caveats. The next chapters tell the narrative; that file is the lookup, and it will be released alongside the dataset.

Reference: data_provenance.md is the per-column technical reference. Per-row citations are stored in adjacent *_source columns where applicable (e.g., kazoku_rank_source, boshin_alignment_source, domain_category_source).
