17  Kazoku rank (the paper’s DV)

The paper’s dependent variable is the kazoku peerage rank each former daimyo family received in 1884.

ImportantWho gets a rank, and what gets mapped

The 1884 kazoku ennoblement awarded peerage ranks to individuals heading daimyo families — not to domains. Domains (藩はん) had already been abolished in 1871 by haihan chiken (廃藩置県はいはんちけん), three years after the Restoration and thirteen years before the Kazoku Law was promulgated. So when this chapter says “Tosa received marquess,” the literal claim is: Yamauchi Toyonori (山内やまうち豊範とよのり), the head of the Yamauchi family that had ruled Tosa domain in 1870, received marquess rank from the Imperial Court in 1884.

A useful temporal sequence to keep in mind:

1870 — Hansei Ichiran surveys 287 daimyo domains
       │  Each domain has a ruling family (Yamauchi for Tosa, Shimazu for Satsuma, ...)
       │
1871 — Haihan chiken: ALL domains abolished. Daimyo become 知藩事 (interim governors).
       │  Domains as political units cease to exist.
       │
1869–84 — Former daimyo and their families remain as households (家); the *family* is the
          continuing unit, no longer the domain.
       │
1884 — Kazoku Law (華族令): the heads of former-daimyo families are awarded peerage
       ranks (公侯伯子男 = prince/marquess/count/viscount/baron).
       │  This is the paper's DV.

Why we map by domain anyway: each polygon on the map is a 1870 domain (a place); the colour shows the rank awarded in 1884 to the family that ruled that domain in 1870. The domain is just a useful spatial unit for displaying which families got which ranks. Click any polygon in the interactive map to see the actual recipient name.

17.1 What is the kazoku system?

In 1884, sixteen years after the Restoration, the Meiji government formalized a Western-style peerage. The 華族令かぞくれい (Kazoku-rei, “Kazoku Law”) of 1884-07-07 created five tiers, calqued on the British system but with Japanese vocabulary:

Rank Kanji Romanization Rough English N of daimyo families
Prince/Duke 公爵こうしゃく kōshaku Prince/Duke 3
Marquess 侯爵こうしゃく kōshaku (different kanji) Marquess 15
Count 伯爵はくしゃく hakushaku Count/Earl 35
Viscount 子爵ししゃく shishaku Viscount 222
Baron 男爵だんしゃく danshaku Baron 9

(N’s above are for former-daimyo families only; the 1884 ennoblement also included kuge court nobility on a parallel track.)

TipTwo different 公爵こうしゃく / 侯爵こうしゃく

Both prince (公爵こうしゃく) and marquess (侯爵こうしゃく) are romanized kōshaku — they sound the same but use different kanji. They are different ranks. Context disambiguates.

17.2 The threshold rule

The 1884 law itself was vague about how ranks would be assigned. The 叙爵内規じょしゃくないき (jōshaku naiki, internal assignment rules) made it explicit and quantitative:

Threshold Rank
≥ 150,000 koku Marquess
50,000 – 149,999 koku Count
< 50,000 koku Viscount
(Special — see below) Prince

So most daimyo were assigned mechanically: large → marquess, medium → count, small → viscount.

Prince was reserved for: - The Tokugawa main line (徳川とくがわ宗家そうけ) — symbolic - The five Sekke kuge court houses (近衛このえ, 鷹司たかつかさ, 九条くじょう, 一条いちじょう, 二条にじょう) - Shimazu (Satsuma) and Mōri (Chōshū) — explicit Restoration rewards - A handful of later upgrades (e.g., Itō Hirobumi, 1907)

17.3 The plot twist: 現米げんまい (genmai), not 草高くさだか

Here’s a methodological wrinkle that’s paper-critical:

The threshold rule above is usually quoted with 草高くさだか (sōdaka) — the nominal yield assessment. The famous “Sendai (Date) was demoted from marquess to count as a Boshin punishment” framing follows from this: Sendai’s 草高くさだか was ~620,000 koku (well into marquess territory), but the family got count, so it must have been punishment.

Asami (1994) argues this is wrong. The threshold was actually applied to 現米げんまい (genmai) — the post-Meiji-2 tax-reported actual rice flow, which is much lower than 草高くさだか for most domains.

Sendai’s 現米げんまい was ~67,740 koku — squarely in count range. By the genmai rule, Sendai’s rank is mechanical, not punishment.

This matters because once you apply the genmai rule, only 19 domains show real deviations. Those 19 are the paper’s core sample — the cases where Boshin/Restoration loyalty actually mattered for rank. See The 19 Deviations.

17.5 How to read this map

A few key patterns to notice:

  • The purple cluster in Kyushu (Satsuma, Chōshū at the western Honshu tip) shows the Restoration coalition that got prince/marquess upgrades.
  • The orange (count) cluster in the north — Sendai, Yonezawa, Morioka, Aizu/Tonami area — these are mostly Northern Alliance domains that stayed at count rank.
  • Light purple (marquess) is scattered — large tozama domains (Kaga, Tosa, Hizen, Kumamoto, Hiroshima, Okayama, Fukuoka, Tokushima, Akita) and the sanke (Owari, Kii, Mito).
  • Viscount is everywhere small — the 222 small-medium domains who passed the threshold but weren’t significant enough for count.
  • Brown (baron) is just 9 cases — former 付家老つけがろう (tsukegarō, “attached elders”) who served as vassals of larger domains pre-Restoration and were elevated to independent-daimyo status at the Restoration. Their barony reflects this lower pre-Restoration standing.

17.6 Cite the data this way

For a regression of the 1884 kazoku rank on kokudaka, control variables, and Boshin alignment:

  • DV citation: 浅見雅男あさみまさお『華族誕生かぞくたんじょう 名誉と体面の明治めいよとたいめんのめいじ』リブロポートりぶろぽーと (1994), with 官報かんぽう 第307号 (1884-07-08) as the primary government gazette (available via NDL Digital Collections, pid 2943511).
  • Methodological note: Following Asami (1994), the ‘expected’ rank is computed from 現米げんまい (genmai), not 表高おもてだか (omotedaka). That change reduces the number of apparent deviations from 93 (by the kokudaka rule) to 19, isolating the cases where Boshin and Restoration loyalty actually mattered.

Citation chain: 浅見雅男あさみまさお『華族誕生かぞくたんじょう』(1994) backs 94% of the rank assignments. 華族大鑑刊行会かぞくたいかんかんこうかい編『華族大鑑かぞくたいかん』(1990) is the primary scholarly source for the 20 princes. 官報かんぽう 第307号 (1884-07-08) is the primary government gazette. Small-domain edge cases additionally cite 小田部雄次おたべゆうじ『華族かぞく』(2006), 森岡浩もりおかひろし (2012), 松田敬之まつだたかゆき (2015).
