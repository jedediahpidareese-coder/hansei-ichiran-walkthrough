13  The 286 domains

The project’s dataset covers 286 daimyō (大名だいみょう) domains — the full universe of han enumerated in Hansei Ichiran 1870, plus a handful of post-1869 administrative renames the source treats as separate entries.

13.1 Map: kokudaka (石高こくだか) across all 286 domains

The map below shows each domain colored by its kokudaka — the revenue assessment in koku of rice. This is the paper’s main predictor variable.

NoteWhy so much of the map is blank white

The base map draws only the 206 daimyō-domain polygons in the 1664 Harvard CHGIS shapefile. Roughly a quarter of Edo-period Japan was not a daimyō domain — it was land the Tokugawa shogunate held directly (tenryō 天領てんりょう), land held by the shogun’s bannermen (hatamoto 旗本はたもと), or temple / shrine / imperial-court land. None of that has a daimyō, so none of it is in a 286-domain dataset, and it shows as blank white. The large white expanse through central Honshū (around Kōfu and Hida) is mostly shogunal tenryō. See the territorial-changes chapter for the full explanation, including the white-vs-grey distinction.

13.2 How to read the map

A few orientation points:

  • The four colored labels (Satsuma, Chōshū, Tosa, Saga) are the Sat-Chō-Do-Hi coalition that led the Restoration. Three are in Kyushu (Satsuma, Chōshū at the western tip of Honshu, Saga) and one is on Shikoku (Tosa).
  • Aizu, Sendai, Yonezawa, Shōnai, Morioka, Nagaoka in northern Honshu are the core of the Northern Alliance (pro-bakufu (幕府ばくふ)) — visible as a band of medium-sized domains.
  • The Edo (modern Tokyo) city marker is mid-Honshu; Kyoto and Osaka are to the west of it.
  • The largest single domain in green near the centre-north is Kaga (加賀藩かがはん), the Maeda house, with kokudaka over 1.3 million koku — the largest non-Tokugawa daimyō in all of Japan.

13.4 Why 286 domains and not, say, 270?

The “exactly N daimyō” count varies by source because the definition shifts:

  • In 1864 (mid-Edo): ~265 daimyō with productive fiefs
  • In 1868 (Restoration year): ~280
  • In 1870 (Hansei Ichiran): Adds post-Boshin reductions/relocations (e.g., 斗南藩となみはん carved from reduced Aizu) — pushing the count up
  • In 1871 (haihan chiken (廃藩置県はいはんちけん)): All abolished. The final count just before abolition was about 281.

The project’s 286 includes: - All 281 domains existing at haihan chiken (1871) - 2 Tokugawa sankyō branches (sankyō: Tayasu and Hitotsubashi) — hatamoto-rank but had administrative existence (the third sankyō line, Shimizu, had gone extinct by 1870) - 3 post-1869 special cases that Hansei Ichiran records as their own entries (e.g., 鶴田藩つるたはん, carved from a former Aizu sub-fief)

An earlier version of the dataset listed 287 rows. One was a cross-volume duplicate: Hansei Ichiran writes the name of the Honjō domain (Dewa province, the Rokugō (六郷) family) as 本庄藩ほんじょうはん in volume 1 and 本荘藩ほんじょうはん in volume 2, and the two spellings were first recorded as separate rows. They are one domain, now merged into a single row — which gives the corrected count of 286.

A second, subtler duplicate was caught later (2026-05-30): Hansei Ichiran records Shōnai under its 1869 name 大泉藩おおいずみはん (120,000 koku, the Sakai family), and a separate “庄内藩しょうないはん” row had been created from an entry mis-read as 鶴岡つるおか藩はん — which was in fact 邨そん岡藩はん (村むら岡藩はん, Muraoka), an unrelated 11,000-koku tozama domain of the Yamana family in Tajima. De-duplicating Shōnai (now the single canonical 庄内藩しょうないはん) and restoring 村むら岡藩はん as its own row left the total unchanged at 286. See Data Quality.

NoteWhy this granularity matters

A study that uses prefecture-level (47 prefectures, post-1871) data loses all variation within the 286-domain set. A study that uses a 1864 daimyō dataset misses the post-Boshin reductions that are crucial for understanding which families fell from rank vs. stayed. Hansei Ichiran’s 1870 timing — between Restoration and abolition — is uniquely informative.

13.5 What’s NOT in the dataset (by design)

  • tenryō (天領てんりょう) — bakufu direct lands. These were governed by daikan (intendants), not daimyō. They became Meiji prefectures directly. The project includes one symbolic entry (大山領だいせんりょう = the Daisen-ji temple territory) for documentation but excludes them from analysis.
  • kuge — kuge (公家くげ), court nobility. Became kazoku (華族かぞく) in 1884 but on a different track (without revenue or military). The outcome variable (kazoku rank) is restricted to former-daimyō lines.
  • Rows with no 1884 peerage rank — two rows in the dataset carry no kazoku rank, and both are genuine exclusions: 堀江藩ほりえはん (the Ōsawa family, denied peerage after its kokudaka claim was found falsified in the 1871 Mangoku jiken) and 大山領だいせんりょう, the Daisen-ji temple territory — never a daimyō domain, so no peerage applied. (A row that once appeared to be a third exclusion, 本庄藩ほんじょうはん, turned out to be a cross-volume duplicate of 本荘藩ほんじょうはん and has been merged — see “Why 286 domains” above.) Other kaieki (改易かいえき, forced confiscation) cases — 林家はやしけ of 請西じょうざい藩はん, for one — fall outside the dataset entirely, their domains dissolved before the 1870 survey.
