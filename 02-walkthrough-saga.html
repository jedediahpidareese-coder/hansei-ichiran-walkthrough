3  A walkthrough of one domain: Saga (佐嘉藩 / Hizen)

To make Hansei Ichiran concrete, we’ll walk through the entry for Saga (佐嘉藩) — one of the four domains that led the 1868 Meiji Restoration. Saga is one of the paper’s focal cases.

ImportantSaga = Hizen (薩長土肥 explained)

The four Restoration-leading domains are abbreviated 薩長土肥 (Sat-Chō-Do-Hi):

  • 薩 (Sat-) = 薩摩藩 (Satsuma)
  • 長 (Chō-) = 長州藩 (Chōshū)
  • 土 (Do-) = 土佐藩 (Tosa)
  • 肥 (-Hi) = 肥前 (Hizen) = 佐賀藩 (Saga)

“Hizen” (肥前) is the ancient province name; “Saga” (佐賀) is the daimyō (大名) seat / castle town. They refer to the same domain under two different naming conventions. The Hansei Ichiran uses 佐嘉 (the old kanji form of 佐賀); the modern canonical form 佐賀藩 is used throughout the dataset. Anglophone scholarship sometimes uses “Hizen” (to match the 薩長土肥 mnemonic) and sometimes “Saga”; this walkthrough uses “Saga” throughout but you’ll see both.

NoteThe 4 + 4 = 8 commended-domains finding

The 薩長土肥 mnemonic captures the first tier of the haihan-chiken commendation — the four domains the imperial decree explicitly credits with first proposing the 1869 hanseki hōkan. The same day’s decree commended a second tier of four additional domains for implementation merit (hanseki ritsu-sei): Owari (Nagoya), Tottori, Kumamoto, and Tokushima (the Tokugawa, Ikeda, Hosokawa, and Hachisuka families). Together the 4 + 4 = 8 are the publicly-commended coalition that underwrote the abolition.

This two-tier structure is documented verbatim in three independent primary sources transcribed by the project: the Meiji Shiyō vol 1 decree text itself, Inoue Kaoru’s biography (vol 1 p. 452), and Kido Takayoshi’s diary (vol II p. 65). The dataset carries this as is_focal_han_tier1 and is_focal_han_tier2 columns. See the variables chapter for the full citation chain.

3.1 What you’ll see on the page

A Saga entry in Hansei Ichiran reads from right to left in vertical columns. The first column always begins with the domain-name marker ○佐嘉藩 (note the old kanji 嘉 rather than the modern 賀). Hansei Ichiran’s Volume 1 and Table-6 fascicles use this pre-1870 form — but, as we’ll see below, its Volume 2 military fascicle (Table 9) switches to the modern 佐賀 — a quirk that makes Saga’s army entry easy to overlook if you search only for the old-kanji form.

WarningOld kanji vs modern kanji

The 1870 print uses pre-Meiji-era character forms (旧字体, kyūjitai). The dataset uses the modern forms (新字体, shinjitai):

  • 佐嘉藩 → 佐賀藩 (Saga)
  • 鹿兒島藩 → 鹿児島藩 (Kagoshima / Satsuma)
  • 金澤藩 → 金沢藩 (Kanazawa / Kaga)

An alias table bridges these.

TipWhy three different “kokudaka”?

Hansei Ichiran reports several different revenue measures because they meant different things in different bureaucratic contexts:

  • sōdaka / omotedaka (表高) = the nominal yield assessment used for protocol and rank
  • 新田高 = newly reclaimed productive land added on top
  • gōdaka (合高) = the sum (what Saga calls 貮口合)
  • genmai (現米) = the actual rice flow currently collected, which is almost always much lower than sōdaka due to natural variation, land degradation, and tax-exemptions

This distinction matters for the paper: the 1884 peerage rank was determined by genmai, not sōdaka (per Asami 1994). See The 19 Deviations.

3.3 What Saga’s military block looks like (Volume 2, Table 9)

Saga’s military entry is easy to overlook — and the reason is a neat illustration of how Hansei Ichiran’s old-kanji conventions work.

Saga’s army appears in Volume 2, Table 9 (第九), on book pages 429–430. Table 9 indexes and prints the domain with the modern form 佐賀藩 (the 賀), rather than the 佐嘉藩 (old 嘉) used in the Volume 1 / Table 6 fiscal entry — so a search keyed only to the old-kanji form slides right past it. On the page the entry is unambiguous — the ○佐賀藩 marker is bracketed by 足利藩 (before it, whose own troops are marked “unrecorded”) and 佐倉藩 (after it, with its own 636-man army), so the block in between is Saga’s:

Field on page Value
Formation 六大隊 (6 battalions)
士官 (officers) 864
砲士 (artillery soldiers) 180
兵士 (infantry) 1,440
砲卒 (artillery privates) 108
兵卒 (infantry privates) 260
樂卒 (bandsmen) 120
Total personnel 2,972
Cannon 36 門 (砲三十六門)

The scale — six battalions and 36 field guns — is exactly the modernised Nabeshima army you’d expect from the domain that built Japan’s first reverberatory furnace and manufactured Armstrong guns at Mietsu (三重津). Hansei Ichiran is a directly citable primary source for Saga’s 1870 standing army.

What Table 9 does not record for Saga is warships: there is no ship line in the entry. For Saga’s navy — the Western-style 凌風丸 and 日進丸 acquired in the bakumatsu period — a published source is still needed (藤野保『佐賀藩』(2010), which also details the 大組 (daikumi) infantry organisation and the Armstrong-gun programme). So the division of labour is clean: Hansei Ichiran gives the army (2,972 men, 36 guns); the secondary literature fills in the navy the survey omitted.

3.4 What this looks like in the dataset

NoteWhat “1884 rank” means in the table below

The 1884 peerage rank is not assigned to a domain — it’s assigned to the head of the former-daimyō family that used to rule that domain. Domains had been abolished in 1871, thirteen years before the Peerage Ordinance.

When the table says “Saga: 1884 rank = marquess,” the literal claim is: Nabeshima Naohiro (the head of the Nabeshima family that had ruled Saga domain in 1870) received marquess rank from the Imperial Court in 1884. The walkthrough indexes the rank by the 1870 domain purely for visualisation.

Chapter 05 explains what the kazoku (華族) peerage system was, how the five ranks were assigned, and why it functioned as a credible-commitment mechanism in the paper’s argument.

3.5 Saga in the Restoration

Saga’s weight among the four leading domains came less from size than from technology. Under Nabeshima Naomasa (lord 1830–1861) the domain ran the most aggressive military-industrial program of the late-Tokugawa era: Japan’s first iron refinery (1849), its first reverberatory furnace (1852), the Mietsu (三重津) naval training station, the domestically built steamship Ryōfū-maru (1865), and the near-domestic manufacture of Armstrong rifled cannon by the mid-1860s. Two of Saga’s Armstrong guns proved decisive at the Battle of Ueno in 1868, where the new government broke the shōgun’s loyalists at long range (藤野保 2010; Yamamura 1974).

That gave Saga an unusual profile — a moderate headline kokudaka but hardware no revenue figure captures, one reason a purely revenue-based measure of a domain’s weight tends to understate it. Saga joined the imperial coalition late, but once it did, its guns and ships made it indispensable. When the abolition came in 1871, Nabeshima Naohiro was one of the four lords the decree named for “first proposing” the return of the registers (see The bargain of 1871); the house received a marquess rank in 1884 — below Satsuma’s and Chōshū’s princes, but secure (see the peerage chapter).
