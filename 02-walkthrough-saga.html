3  A walkthrough of one domain: Saga (佐嘉藩さがはん / Hizen)

To make Hansei Ichiran concrete, we’ll walk through the entry for Saga (佐嘉藩さがはん) — one of the four domains that led the 1868 Meiji Restoration. Saga is one of the paper’s focal cases.

ImportantSaga = Hizen (薩長土肥さっちょうどひ explained)

The four Restoration-leading domains are abbreviated 薩長土肥さっちょうどひ (Sat-Chō-Do-Hi):

  • 薩 (Sat-) = 薩摩藩さつまはん (Satsuma)
  • 長 (Chō-) = 長州藩ちょうしゅうはん (Chōshū)
  • 土 (Do-) = 土佐藩とさはん (Tosa)
  • 肥 (-Hi) = 肥前ひぜん (Hizen) = 佐賀藩さがはん (Saga)

“Hizen” (肥前ひぜん) is the ancient province name; “Saga” (佐賀さが) is the daimyō (大名だいみょう) seat / castle town. They refer to the same domain under two different naming conventions. The Hansei Ichiran uses 佐嘉が (the old kanji form of 佐賀さが); the modern canonical form 佐賀藩さがはん is used throughout the dataset. Anglophone scholarship sometimes uses “Hizen” (to match the 薩長土肥さっちょうどひ mnemonic) and sometimes “Saga”; this walkthrough uses “Saga” throughout but you’ll see both.

3.1 What you’ll see on the page

A Saga entry in Hansei Ichiran reads from right to left in vertical columns. The first column always begins with the domain-name marker ○佐嘉藩さがはん (note the old kanji 嘉が rather than the modern 賀 — Hansei Ichiran uses pre-1870 forms throughout).

WarningOld kanji vs modern kanji

The 1870 print uses pre-Meiji-era character forms (旧字体きゅうじたい, kyūjitai). The dataset uses the modern forms (新字体しんじたい, shinjitai):

  • 佐嘉が藩はん → 佐賀藩はん (Saga)
  • 鹿兒じ島藩はん → 鹿児島藩はん (Kagoshima / Satsuma)
  • 金澤さわ藩はん → 金沢藩はん (Kanazawa / Kaga)

An alias table bridges these.

TipWhy three different “kokudaka”?

Hansei Ichiran reports several different revenue measures because they meant different things in different bureaucratic contexts:

  • sōdaka / omotedaka (表高おもてだか) = the nominal yield assessment used for protocol and rank
  • 新田高しんでんだか = newly reclaimed productive land added on top
  • gōdaka (合高ごうだか) = the sum (what Saga calls 貮口合ふたくちごう)
  • genmai (現米げんまい) = the actual rice flow currently collected, which is almost always much lower than sōdaka due to natural variation, land degradation, and tax-exemptions

This distinction matters for the paper: the 1884 peerage rank was determined by genmai, not sōdaka (per Asami 1994). See The 19 Deviations.

3.3 What Saga’s military block looks like (vol. 2)

Saga’s military and administrative data would normally appear in Volume 2 — the rosters of troop strength, officers, and warships that every other major domain has. But they don’t appear. Saga has only a partial Volume 2 fiscal entry; the military rosters are absent altogether.

The dataset handles this with a SOURCE_GAP marker on Saga’s military fields and substitutes data from a published scholarly source: 藤野保ふじのたもつ『佐賀藩さがはん』(2010). From that source:

  • 13 大組おおぐみ (daikumi) — main infantry units
  • ~1,000 troops from the Takeo branch (武雄たけお)
  • Naval: 凌風丸りょうふうまる + 日進丸にっしんまる (two Western-style warships acquired in the bakumatsu period)
  • Artillery: Saga’s famous Armstrong gun programme

This is one of the documented cases where the primary source has a gap and a published scholarly secondary supplies the substitute. The replacement is transparent: Saga’s Boshin-alignment citation reads “Esposito (2020) Osprey + 藤野保ふじのたもつ (2010) 佐賀藩さがはん — late-joining anti-bakufu (幕府ばくふ)”.
