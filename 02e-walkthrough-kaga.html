7  Walkthrough: Kaga (加賀藩 / 金沢藩)

Kaga is the largest non-Tokugawa domain in all of Japan. The Maeda family — famously cautious, famously rich, famously not a major Boshin combatant — sat out the active fighting and let history happen around them.

ImportantTwo names — same place
  • 加賀藩 (Kaga-han) — historical/canonical project name (after the Kaga province)
  • 金沢藩 (Kanazawa-han) — post-1869 administrative rename (seat at Kanazawa Castle); the Hansei Ichiran 1870 form uses old kanji 金澤藩

The Hansei Ichiran uses 金澤藩. The project’s canonical name is the older 加賀藩. The Maeda family ruled both iterations.

7.2 Military capacity — paradoxically the most complete data

Kaga is one of the only 8 COMPLETE-RICH military entries in the entire dataset — its Hansei Ichiran vol. 2 record includes troops, officers, warships, and artillery in full detail. This is partly because:

  • Kaga had no real Boshin combat, so its 1870 establishment was unchanged
  • Kaga’s bureaucracy was unusually thorough about reporting
  • The Maeda family’s ~1.35 million koku assessment funded a substantial paper army

But despite this paper army, Kaga did not deploy in the Boshin War (Boshin sensō).

7.3 Why Kaga’s “anti-bakufu” classification is a late-mover label

Kaga’s recorded Boshin alignment = “anti-bakufu / pro-restoration” — but this reflects end-of-war posture, not active participation.

The narrative:

  1. Early 1868: Kaga initially favors the shōgun (将軍) (Maeda’s traditional bakufu-loyalty)
  2. Late January 1868: Toba-Fushimi battle ends; Tokugawa Yoshinobu surrenders
  3. February 1868: Maeda publicly submits to the new Imperial Hokuriku pacification army
  4. Throughout the Boshin War (戊辰戦争): Kaga sends no significant force to either side

So Kaga is “anti-bakufu” only in the sense that it ratified the imperial directive once that was clearly the winning side. Beasley (1972) is silent on Kaga as an active combatant; the JP Wikipedia article describes Kaga’s stance as cautious late-acceptance.

TipWhy this matters for the empirical analysis

Kaga’s case shows why the simple Boshin-alignment label is a noisy treatment variable in isolation. A binary “anti-bakufu vs pro-bakufu” doesn’t distinguish active Restoration leaders (Satsuma, Chōshū) from late-mover non-combatants (Kaga, plus 200+ smaller domains). The paper accounts for this with auxiliary citations and by focusing analyses on the genmai-deviation subsample where the action actually lives.

7.4 Why Kaga stayed at marquess

kokudaka 1.35 million → marquess range mechanically (Maeda was the largest tozama, but still tozama). The 1884 ennoblement followed the rule exactly: Maeda Toshitsugu received marquess. No deviation; no Restoration reward.

Boshin alignment: JP Wikipedia 加賀藩 (late-mover, post-Toba-Fushimi imperial submission); Beasley (1972) silent on Kaga as combatant.
